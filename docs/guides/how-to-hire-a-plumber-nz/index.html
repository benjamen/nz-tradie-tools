🔧 How to Hire a Plumber in NZ (2026 Guide)

Hiring the right plumber saves money and prevents costly mistakes. This guide covers how to check a plumber's licence, what to pay, how to get quotes, and what to do if something goes wrong — all specific to NZ.

Step-by-Step: Hiring a Plumber in NZ

  1. Decide if it's an emergency or planned work

    For burst pipes, sewage backups, or flooding — call any licensed plumber immediately. For planned work (new bathroom, hot water cylinder, drain repairs), take time to get quotes.

  2. Verify their licence at pgdb.co.nz

    All NZ plumbers must hold a current Practising Licence from the Plumbers, Gasfitters and Drainlayers Board (PGDB). Ask for their licence number and check it at pgdb.co.nz. It takes 30 seconds and protects you.

  3. Check reviews

    Look them up on NoCowboys or Builderscrack. A plumber with 20+ reviews and a rating above 4.5 is a solid choice. Look at how they respond to any negative reviews.

  4. Get 2–3 written quotes for planned work

    For jobs over $500 in labour, always get at least 2 quotes. Ask each plumber to quote on the same scope so you can compare fairly. Quotes should be in writing — not over the phone.

  5. Ask the right questions before confirming

    See the checklist below. Key things: is the quote fixed or hourly? Is GST included? What's their process for unexpected issues? Do they carry public liability insurance?

  6. Get the job documented

    For any significant work, a written contract or job confirmation is important. For work requiring certification (drainage, gas, new installations), ensure you receive the compliance certificate when the job is done.

Questions to Ask Before You Hire

  • Are you currently licensed with the PGDB? (Get their licence number)
  • Do you carry public liability insurance — and can you show proof?
  • Is your quote fixed price or hourly? If hourly, what's the maximum estimate?
  • Are materials included in the quote, or charged on top?
  • Is GST included in the price you've quoted?
  • What happens if you find additional issues once work starts?
  • Will the work require a compliance certificate — and is that included?
  • What's your availability? When can you start?
  • What's your payment process — deposit, progress payments, or on completion?
  • Do you guarantee your workmanship? For how long?

Red Flags to Watch For

No licence or won't share it

Any legitimate plumber will readily share their PGDB licence number. Refusal is a major red flag — unlicensed work is illegal.

Cash-only, no receipt

You need a GST receipt for any home maintenance work. "Cash only, no invoice" means no compliance trail and no recourse if something goes wrong.

Quote way below others

If one quote is 40% cheaper than the others, ask why. It may mean lower-grade materials, cutting compliance corners, or a bait-and-switch on variations.

Pressure to decide immediately

Genuine plumbers don't pressure you to sign on the spot. A high-pressure sales approach — especially combined with a big discount "only if you sign today" — is a warning sign.

Your Rights If Plumbing Work Goes Wrong

If a plumber's work is faulty or incomplete, you have several avenues:

  • Ask them to fix it — put the issue in writing (email is fine) and give them a reasonable opportunity to remediate.
  • PGDB complaint — if the plumber is unresponsive or the work is dangerous, file a complaint with the PGDB. They investigate and can impose sanctions including licence suspension.
  • Disputes Tribunal — for financial disputes up to $30,000, the Disputes Tribunal is an inexpensive option (no lawyers required).
  • Consumer Guarantees Act — plumbing services must be completed with reasonable care and skill, in a reasonable time, and be fit for purpose. If they're not, the CGA gives you rights to have the issue remedied or to claim compensation.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the difference between a plumber and a drainlayer?

A plumber handles indoor water supply, hot water, and sanitary fixtures (taps, toilets, baths). A drainlayer handles underground drainage and sewer connections. Many companies are licensed for both — if you have a blocked drain or need new drainage connected to the street, confirm your plumber is also licensed as a drainlayer.

Do plumbers charge for quotes?

For small jobs, most plumbers provide a free quote over the phone or after a quick site visit. For large, complex jobs (bathroom additions, drainage work), some plumbers charge a quoting fee of $100–$250 — often refundable if you proceed. Always ask upfront.

Can I supply my own fixtures and fittings?

Yes — you can supply your own tap, toilet suite, or sink. The plumber will still charge their full labour rate. The advantage is you control the product quality; the disadvantage is the plumber may not warranty the installation if the product is substandard. Some plumbers add a small surcharge for customer-supplied fixtures.

What is a sanitary plumbing certificate?

For new plumbing installations (not repairs), licensed plumbers must issue a Certificate of Authorised Person (CoA) or similar compliance certificate confirming the work meets NZ plumbing standards. You may need this for building consent, insurance, or resale. Always request it at the end of any significant new installation.

Is it legal to fix a leaking tap yourself in NZ?

Minor DIY plumbing maintenance is allowed in NZ — including replacing tap washers, toilet internals, and shower heads. However, any work involving cutting into or joining pipes, new drainage, or hot water connections must be done by a licensed plumber. When in doubt, call a plumber — the cost of getting it wrong outweighs the saving.
