Bathroom Renovation Cost NZ 2026 — How Much Should You Budget?

Published 14 June 2026 · NZ Tradie Tools
bathroomrenovationcostsNZ2026

A bathroom renovation is one of the most popular home upgrades in New Zealand — and one of the most variable when it comes to cost. Ask around and you'll hear everything from "we did ours for ten grand" to "ours blew out past fifty." Both can be true. A bathroom is a small room that packs in plumbing, waterproofing, tiling, electrical and joinery all at once, so the choices you make on layout and finishes have an outsized effect on the final bill.

This guide lays out realistic 2026 budgets for three tiers of renovation, breaks the cost down trade by trade, and covers the questions that trip people up — consent rules, timeframes, and where it's safe to save versus where it absolutely isn't.

Bathroom Renovation Cost by Tier

Here's the big-picture view for 2026. These are total project costs including labour, materials and fixtures, for a standard-sized family bathroom.

Basic refresh — $8,000–$15,000. New tiles, vanity, toilet and taps, with no change to the layout. You're replacing what's there in roughly the same positions, which keeps plumbing and drainage costs down. This is the sweet spot for a tired-but-functional bathroom that just needs modernising.

Mid-range renovation — $15,000–$30,000. A full gut-and-redo with a new layout, quality fixtures and a proper tiled shower. You're moving things around, replacing the lot, and choosing better-than-builder-grade fittings. This is what most homeowners picture when they think "bathroom reno."

High-end / wet room — $30,000–$70,000+. Premium tiles, heated floor, frameless glass shower, custom joinery, designer tapware and often underfloor changes. A full wet-room conversion with tanked walls and floor, a freestanding bath and a curbless shower lands at the top of this range and beyond.

Cost Breakdown by Component

Knowing where the money goes helps you spot a padded quote and decide where to spend. These are typical 2026 ranges for the main parts of a bathroom job.

  • Plumber (labour): $1,500–$4,000 for rough-in and fit-out. More if you're relocating the toilet, shower or vanity to new positions.
  • Electrician: $800–$2,000 for the extractor fan, heated floor connection, lighting and any new circuits. Heated floors and heated towel rails push this up.
  • Tiler (labour only): $50–$100/m² in labour, plus the tiles themselves at $30–$150/m². Large-format tiles, mosaics and feature walls take longer and cost more to lay.
  • Waterproofing: $800–$2,000. Non-negotiable for wet areas — see the warning below.
  • Vanity: $300–$3,000+. A flat-pack vanity is at the bottom; custom-built joinery with a stone top is at the top.
  • Toilet suite: $300–$1,200 depending on whether you go standard close-coupled or wall-hung with an in-wall cistern.
  • Shower / bath: $500–$5,000+. A basic acrylic shower is cheap; a frameless glass enclosure or a freestanding designer bath is not.
  • Building consent: usually not required for like-for-like work, but required (and an added cost) if you're moving walls or plumbing.

⚠️ Don't skimp on waterproofing. It's the most important part of any wet-area renovation. If it fails, water gets into the floor and wall framing — and the repair bill dwarfs what you "saved." Always use a qualified applicator and make sure it's done to NZ standards before tiling goes on.

Regional Cost Differences

Where you live matters. Auckland and Wellington typically run around 20% above the national average, driven by higher labour rates, busier trades and bigger business overheads. Christchurch, Hamilton and the regions generally sit closer to the lower end of each range. If you're getting quotes in a main centre, anchor your expectations toward the top of the brackets above.

How Long Does It Take?

A mid-range bathroom renovation usually takes 2–4 weeks of on-site work. That's not continuous — there's a natural rhythm where the plumber and electrician rough in, the waterproofer and tiler do their bit (with curing and drying time built in), then everyone returns for the fit-out. Waterproofing and tile adhesive need time to cure properly, and trying to rush those stages is how problems start. The biggest cause of delays is fixtures or tiles arriving late, so order everything well before demolition begins.

Do I Need a Building Consent?

For a straight like-for-like renovation — same layout, fixtures in the same positions — you generally don't need a building consent, as this falls under exempt building work. The plumbing and drainage still has to be carried out by a licensed plumber or drainlayer, and an electrician must do the wiring, but you won't be lodging consent paperwork.

You do need a consent if you're moving walls, relocating plumbing or drainage, or doing anything structural. If your "refresh" turns into "let's move the shower to the other side," that's the line you've crossed. When in doubt, a quick call to your local council saves grief later. Our guide on whether you need a building consent in NZ goes into more detail.

Tips to Keep the Budget Under Control

  • Keep the layout. Moving plumbing and drainage is the single biggest avoidable cost. If the existing layout works, leave it.
  • Get three quotes. Bathroom pricing varies hugely between tradies — comparing three written quotes for the same scope is the best way to spot fair pricing.
  • Use licensed plumbers and electricians. It's a compliance requirement for this kind of work, and it protects you if something goes wrong.
  • Spend on waterproofing, save on cosmetics. A flat-pack vanity and mid-range tiles look great and last; failed waterproofing is a disaster. Spend where it counts.
  • Order fixtures early. Delivery delays stall the whole job and keep trades waiting (which costs money).
