Asbestos in NZ Homes — What Homeowners Need to Know

Published 24 June 2026 · NZ Tradie Tools
asbestoshomeownerDIYsafetyNZ2026

Introduction to Asbestos in NZ Homes

Asbestos is a significant concern for many New Zealand homeowners, particularly those living in homes built before 1990. It's estimated that about one in three homes from this era contain asbestos-containing materials (ACM). Asbestos was widely used in construction due to its durability, fire-resistance, and insulation properties. However, it's now well-known that asbestos poses serious health risks, including lung cancer, mesothelioma, and asbestosis. The good news is that asbestos in good condition, which is not disturbed, is generally safe. This comprehensive guide aims to inform homeowners about the risks associated with asbestos, where it's commonly found, how to identify it, and the legal requirements for its removal.

Where Asbestos is Found in NZ Homes

Asbestos can be found in various parts of a home, both inside and outside. Exterior cladding made from fibrous cement sheet (such as Hardiflex, Monlex, or Super Six) is a common location, as well as corrugated roof sheets. Inside the home, asbestos might be present in textured ceilings (often referred to as "popcorn" ceilings), stipple texture paint, and vinyl floor tiles along with the adhesive underneath, which was common in homes built from the 1950s to the 1980s. Insulation materials like pipe lagging and some ceiling batts in older homes may also contain asbestos. Additionally, some bathrooms may have asbestos in wall tiles and tile adhesive, while older fibrous cement products can be found in window surrounds, gutters, and soffits.

Common Locations of Asbestos in NZ Homes:

  • Exterior cladding: Fibrous cement sheet
  • Roofing: Corrugated asbestos cement roofing sheets (Super Six)
  • Interior linings: Textured ceilings, stipple texture paint
  • Flooring: Vinyl floor tiles and adhesive
  • Insulation: Pipe lagging, some ceiling batts
  • Bathrooms: Wall tiles, tile adhesive
  • Exterior features: Window surrounds, gutters, soffits

How to Identify Asbestos

Identifying asbestos requires more than just a visual inspection. While some materials may appear to contain asbestos based on their appearance, the only definitive way to confirm the presence of asbestos is through laboratory testing. Homeowners can arrange for an asbestos test by taking a small sample of the material in question, using gloves and a mask to minimize exposure, and sealing it in a zip-lock bag. This sample can then be sent to an accredited New Zealand laboratory for analysis, which typically costs between $50 to $150 per sample. Alternatively, hiring an asbestos inspector to conduct a thorough inspection of the home can provide a comprehensive report, costing between $200 to $600.

Key Laboratories in NZ for Asbestos Testing:

  • Hill Laboratories
  • IANZ (International Accreditation New Zealand) accredited labs

What You Can Legally Do Yourself

Under the Health and Safety at Work (Asbestos) Regulations 2016, homeowners are allowed to remove up to 10m² of non-friable (bonded) asbestos from their own home without the need for a licensed removalist. This includes small areas of fibrous cement sheet, provided it is undamaged. However, there are strict limitations on what can be done without professional help. For instance, the removal of friable asbestos (which is loose or powdery, such as insulation or sprayed coatings) and any asbestos from the roof if the area is large, requires the services of a licensed removalist.

When to Use a Licensed Removalist

The use of a licensed removalist is mandatory for the removal of friable asbestos and for any job exceeding 10m² of non-friable asbestos. WorkSafe NZ has specific notification requirements for larger asbestos removal jobs, which must be adhered to. To find a licensed removalist, homeowners can look for Class A (friable asbestos) or Class B (non-friable asbestos) licensed professionals. These professionals have the necessary training and equipment to safely remove asbestos, minimizing the risk of exposure to themselves and others.

Costs of Asbestos Removal in NZ

The cost of asbestos removal in New Zealand can vary widely, depending on the type of asbestos, its location, and the quantity to be removed. For small removals of fibrous cement (under 10m²), homeowners might choose to do it themselves if they are careful and follow safety guidelines, or they might hire a professional for $500 to $2,000. Replacing fibrous cement cladding on an entire house can cost anywhere from $15,000 to $50,000. Removing and replacing roof sheets can range from $5,000 to $20,000. The removal of friable asbestos, such as pipe lagging or ceiling insulation, can be particularly costly, with prices starting from $3,000 and potentially exceeding $20,000.

Estimated Costs for Asbestos Removal:

Type of Removal Estimated Cost
Small fibrous cement removal (DIY or professional) $0 - $2,000
Whole house fibrous cement cladding replacement $15,000 - $50,000
Roof sheet removal and replacement $5,000 - $20,000
Friable asbestos removal $3,000 - $20,000+

Safe DIY Removal (If Legal)

For homeowners who are legally allowed to remove asbestos themselves, it's crucial to follow safe removal practices. This includes using the wet method to minimize dust, wearing a full face P2 respirator (not just a dust mask), and disposable coveralls. The asbestos should be double-bagged in 0.2mm polythene bags labelled "ASBESTOS" and then disposed of at a council-approved asbestos disposal facility. Most NZ councils have such facilities, and it's essential to check with local authorities for specific disposal guidelines.

Renovation Rule: Always Test Before Demo

The most critical rule for anyone planning to renovate a pre-1990 home is to always test for asbestos before starting any demolition work. This simple step can prevent unnecessary exposure to asbestos fibers, which can become airborne and pose a significant health risk. Testing is a precautionary measure that should never be skipped, as it provides homeowners with the information they need to plan a safe renovation.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Is fibrous cement cladding dangerous?

A: Fibrous cement cladding itself is not typically dangerous if it's in good condition and not disturbed. However, if it's damaged or deteriorated, it can release asbestos fibers, posing a health risk.

Q: Do I have to disclose asbestos when selling my NZ home?

A: Yes, sellers are required to disclose known asbestos in the property to potential buyers. This is part of the seller's obligations under New Zealand law.

Q: Can I paint over asbestos fibrous cement?

A: It's generally not recommended to paint over asbestos-containing materials. While painting might seem like a way to seal in the asbestos, it's not a reliable method for encapsulation and can actually increase the risk of asbestos fiber release over time.

Q: How much does asbestos testing cost?

A: The cost of asbestos testing can range from $50 to $150 per sample for laboratory analysis. Hiring an asbestos inspector for a home inspection report can cost between $200 to $600.

Q: What is a friable vs non-friable asbestos?

A: Friable asbestos refers to materials that are loose or powdery and can easily release asbestos fibers into the air, such as insulation or sprayed coatings. Non-friable asbestos, on the other hand, is bonded and less likely to release fibers, examples include fibrous cement sheets.
