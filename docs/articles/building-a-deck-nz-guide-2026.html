Building a Deck NZ 2026 — Costs, Consent Rules and DIY Guide

Published 27 June 2026 · NZ Tradie Tools
deckdeckingDIYhomeownerbuilding consentNZ2026

Introduction

Building a deck in New Zealand is one of the most popular outdoor projects for homeowners, and for good reason. A well-designed deck can add significant value to a property, provide a comfortable outdoor living space, and enhance the overall aesthetic appeal of a home. However, before embarking on a deck-building project, it's essential to consider several decision points, including the type of timber to use, the design and construction process, and the costs involved. This guide will cover these topics in detail, providing homeowners and builders with a comprehensive overview of the process. From understanding the building consent rules to selecting the right timber and designing the deck, we'll explore the key factors to consider when building a deck in NZ in 2026.

Do you need building consent for a deck?

Under Schedule 1 of the Building Act 2004, decks that are less than 1.5m above ground level generally do not require building consent. However, there are some exceptions and additional considerations to keep in mind. For example, if your deck is near a pool, you may need to comply with pool fencing requirements. Additionally, if your deck is attached to a consented structure in a way that affects its weathertightness, or if it includes a significant structural element, you may need to obtain consent. It's also important to check your district plan for any setback rules or other regulations that may apply. The minimum height for balustrades is 1m, and openings must not exceed 100mm to ensure child safety. To determine whether you need building consent for your deck, it's best to call your local council's duty planner for free advice. Timber selection

When it comes to selecting timber for your deck, there are several options to consider, each with its own advantages and disadvantages. Here are some of the most popular types of timber used for decking in NZ:

  • Pine (treated): H3.2 treated pine is suitable for above-ground decking, while H4 treated pine is required for posts that will be in contact with the ground. Kiln-dried pine is recommended for decking to minimize cupping and warping. The cost of pine decking ranges from $30 to $60 per linear metre.
  • Hardwood (kwila/merbau, vitex): Hardwood decking offers a beautiful finish and is more durable than pine. However, it is also more expensive, with prices ranging from $60 to $120 per linear metre.
  • Composite decking (Trex, Futurewood, EasyDek): Composite decking is a low-maintenance option that is resistant to splintering and rot. It is also more expensive than traditional timber options, with prices ranging from $100 to $200 per square metre. Composite decking often comes with a 25-year warranty.

Design considerations

When designing your deck, there are several factors to consider to ensure that it is safe, functional, and aesthetically pleasing. Here are some key design considerations:

  • Board spacing: The spacing between decking boards will depend on the type of timber used. For timber decking, a 5mm gap is recommended, while composite decking should be spaced according to the manufacturer's specifications.
  • Joist spacing: The typical joist spacing for a 90mm decking board is 450mm centres.
  • Post sizing: The minimum post size for a deck is 90x90mm.
  • Ledger attachment: The ledger attachment to the house is critical for ensuring weathertightness.
  • Drainage falls: The deck should be designed with a minimum fall of 1:100 to ensure that water drains away from the house.

Step-by-step construction overview

Building a deck can be a complex process, but here is a step-by-step overview of the construction process:

  1. Layout and posts: Mark out the deck area and dig holes for the posts. Concrete the posts into place and allow to set.
  2. Bearer installation: Install the bearers between the posts, ensuring that they are level and securely attached.
  3. Joist frame: Install the joist frame, spacing the joists according to the design specifications.
  4. Decking boards: Install the decking boards, starting from the outside edge and working inwards.
  5. Balustrades: If required, install the balustrades, ensuring that they meet the minimum height and opening requirements.
  6. Finishing: Finish the deck with oil or paint, depending on the type of timber used.

Balustrade requirements

Balustrades are required for decks that are more than 1m above the adjacent ground level. The minimum height for balustrades is 1m, and openings must not exceed 100mm to ensure child safety. Balustrades can be DIY or purchased as kit systems.

Cost breakdown

The cost of building a deck will depend on several factors, including the size of the deck, the type of timber used, and the complexity of the design. Here are some estimated costs for building a 20m² deck:

  • 20m² pine deck (materials only): $3,000-$6,000
  • 20m² pine deck (professionally built): $8,000-$15,000
  • 20m² composite deck (materials only): $5,000-$9,000
  • 20m² composite deck (professionally built): $12,000-$22,000
  • Regional variation: Auckland has a 20% premium on deck-building costs due to higher labour and material costs.

Deck maintenance

To ensure that your deck lasts for many years, regular maintenance is essential. Here are some tips for maintaining your deck:

  • Pine: Re-oil or paint every 2-3 years to protect the timber from the elements.
  • Hardwood: Oil annually for the first 2 years, then as needed to maintain the timber's natural colour and texture.
  • Composite: Wash annually with soap and water to keep the deck clean and free from debris. No oiling is required.

FAQ

Here are some frequently asked questions about building a deck in NZ:

  1. How close to the boundary can I build a deck in NZ?: The distance from the boundary will depend on the local council's regulations and the type of deck being built. It's best to check with your local council for specific guidance.
  2. Does a deck add value to a NZ home?: Yes, a well-designed and well-built deck can add significant value to a home, both in terms of its aesthetic appeal and its functionality.
  3. Can I build a deck myself?: Yes, but it's essential to ensure that you have the necessary skills and experience to build a deck that is safe and compliant with the Building Act.
  4. What's the best decking material for NZ?: The best decking material will depend on your budget, personal preferences, and the intended use of the deck. Pine, hardwood, and composite decking are all popular options in NZ.
  5. Do I need a balustrade on my deck?: If your deck is more than 1m above the adjacent ground level, you will need to install a balustrade to ensure safety and compliance with the Building Act.

Conclusion

Building a deck in NZ can be a complex process, but with the right guidance and planning, it can also be a rewarding and enjoyable project. By considering the factors outlined in this guide, you can ensure that your deck is safe, functional, and aesthetically pleasing.
