Home Insulation NZ 2026 — What You Need, Costs and Subsidies

Published 25 June 2026 · NZ Tradie Tools
insulationhome insulationWarmer Kiwi HomeshomeownerNZ2026

Introduction

When it comes to home improvements in New Zealand, insulation stands out as the highest-return-on-investment (ROI) option. Not only does it significantly reduce heating costs, but it also enhances the overall comfort and healthiness of a home. As of 2023, the NZ Building Code H1 requirements have been updated, emphasizing the importance of adequate insulation in new builds and retrofits. This guide will cover the essential aspects of home insulation in NZ, including the types of insulation, costs, and how to access the Warmer Kiwi Homes subsidy. Whether you're a homeowner, landlord, or tradesperson, understanding the intricacies of insulation is crucial for making informed decisions.

NZ Building Code H1 Requirements

The NZ Building Code H1 requirements outline the minimum insulation standards for buildings in different climate zones. As of 2023, the updated requirements specify the following minimum R-values: * Ceiling: R6.6–R7.0 * Underfloor: R2.5–R3.2 * Walls: R2.4–R2.8 A map of NZ climate zones can be found on the MBIE website, helping you determine the specific requirements for your region. It's essential to note that new builds and retrofits have different requirements, with new builds typically needing to meet more stringent standards.

For new builds, the updated H1 requirements aim to ensure that homes are built with adequate insulation from the outset. However, for existing homes, retrofits can be more complex and costly. The NZ Building Code provides a framework for retrofitting insulation, but it's crucial to consult with a professional to determine the best approach for your specific situation.

Types of Insulation

The type of insulation you choose depends on the area of your home you're looking to insulate: ceiling, underfloor, or walls. Each area has its unique challenges and requirements.

Ceiling Insulation

Ceiling insulation is one of the most critical aspects of home insulation. The most common types of ceiling insulation are: * Bulk batts: Made from materials like polyester, glasswool, or rockwool, these batts are installed between the ceiling joists. Brands like Pink Batts, Bradford Gold, and Knauf offer high-quality bulk batts. * Blown-in loose fill: This type of insulation is blown into the ceiling space using a specialized machine. It's often used in areas with limited access or where bulk batts are not feasible.

The R-value of ceiling insulation varies depending on the material and installation method. A higher R-value indicates better insulation performance. Here's a comparison of R-values for different ceiling insulation types: | Insulation Type | R-Value | Cost per m² | | --- | --- | --- | | Pink Batts R3.6 | R3.6 | $15-$25 | | Bradford Gold R6.0 | R6.0 | $25-$40 | | Knauf R7.0 | R7.0 | $35-$50 |

Underfloor Insulation

Underfloor insulation is another crucial aspect of home insulation. The most common types of underfloor insulation are: * Foil: While foil insulation is still used in some cases, its effectiveness is limited, and it's sometimes banned if old and degraded. * Bulk batts: These batts are stapled up between the floor joists and provide better insulation than foil. * Rigid foam boards: These boards are installed between the floor joists and offer high R-values.

The cost of underfloor insulation varies depending on the material and installation method. Here's a comparison of costs for different underfloor insulation types: | Insulation Type | R-Value | Cost per m² | | --- | --- | --- | | Foil | R1.0-R2.0 | $10-$20 | | Bulk batts | R2.5-R3.2 | $20-$40 | | Rigid foam boards | R3.5-R4.5 | $30-$60 |

Wall Insulation

Wall insulation is often the most challenging and costly aspect of home insulation. The most common types of wall insulation are: * Blown-in: This type of insulation is blown into the wall cavity using a specialized machine. * Internal rigid foam: These boards are installed inside the wall cavity and offer high R-values. * External wrap: This type of insulation is installed on the outside of the wall and provides a high R-value.

Wall insulation is usually only done during recladding, as it requires significant disruption to the existing wall structure. The cost of wall insulation varies widely depending on the material, installation method, and accessibility of the wall cavity.

Warmer Kiwi Homes Subsidy

The Warmer Kiwi Homes programme, administered by the Energy Efficiency and Conservation Authority (EECA), provides subsidies for insulation and heating upgrades. To be eligible, you must: * Hold a Community Services Card or live in a low-income area * Own and occupy the home * Not have received a subsidy for the same type of work in the past

The subsidy amount is up to 80% of the total cost, with a cap of $2,500 for insulation and $2,500 for a heat pump or pellet fire. To apply, you'll need to find a registered provider through the EECA website. Some approved providers include: * Smart Energy Solutions * Insulation NZ * Warm NZ

Ceiling Insulation Costs

The cost of ceiling insulation varies depending on the material, R-value, and installation method. For a 100m² ceiling, here are some estimated costs: * Pink Batts R3.6 DIY: $700 (materials only) * Pink Batts R3.6 installed: $1,800-$3,500 * R6.6 (code-compliant) installed: $2,800-$5,000

Underfloor Insulation Costs

The cost of underfloor insulation also varies depending on the material and installation method. For an 80m² underfloor area, here are some estimated costs: * Foil (if permitted): $600-$1,500 * Bulk polyester batts installed: $2,000-$4,000

Is Ceiling Insulation DIY?

Yes, ceiling insulation is one of the more achievable homeowner DIY jobs, especially when using bulk batts in an accessible ceiling space. However, it's essential to take safety precautions, including: * Wearing a P2 mask * Eye protection * Full coverall

No electrical work is involved, making it a relatively safe DIY project.

Return on Investment

The return on investment for insulation is significant, with typical NZ heating cost savings ranging from $400 to $900 per year. The payback period for insulation varies from 3 to 8 years, depending on the material, R-value, and installation method. In addition to cost savings, insulation also improves comfort and health benefits, particularly for rental properties subject to the Healthy Homes Standards.

Healthy Homes Standards for Landlords

The Healthy Homes Standards, introduced in 2019, set minimum insulation requirements for rental properties. The standards require: * Ceiling insulation with a minimum R-value of R6.6 * Underfloor insulation with a minimum R-value of R2.5 * Wall insulation with a minimum R-value of R2.4

Compliance deadlines vary depending on the type of rental property, but most landlords must comply by 2025.

FAQ

  1. What R-value insulation do I need in NZ? The required R-value depends on the climate zone and area of your home. For example, in Auckland, you'll need a minimum R-value of R6.6 for ceiling insulation.
  2. Can I install ceiling insulation myself? Yes, ceiling insulation is a relatively safe and achievable DIY job, especially when using bulk batts in an accessible ceiling space.
  3. Am I eligible for the Warmer Kiwi Homes subsidy? To be eligible, you must hold a Community Services Card or live in a low-income area, own and occupy the home, and not have received a subsidy for the same type of work in the past.
  4. How long does insulation last? The lifespan of insulation varies depending on the material and installation method. Most insulation materials last for 20-50 years or more.
  5. Can I use Xero to manage my insulation business? Yes, Xero is a popular accounting software that can help you manage your insulation business, including invoicing and job management. You can also consider using Fergus or Tradify for more specialized job management and invoicing solutions.

Insulation is a critical aspect of home improvement in NZ, offering significant cost savings, comfort, and health benefits.
