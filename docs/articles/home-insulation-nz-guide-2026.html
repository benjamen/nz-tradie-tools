Home Insulation NZ 2026 — What You Need, Costs and Subsidies

Published 26 June 2026 · NZ Tradie Tools
insulationhome insulationWarmer Kiwi HomeshomeownerNZ2026

Introduction

In New Zealand, home insulation is widely regarded as the highest-return-on-investment (ROI) home improvement, offering significant benefits in terms of energy efficiency, comfort, and health. As of 2023, the NZ Building Code H1 requirements have been updated, setting new minimum R-values for ceiling, underfloor, and wall insulation. In this guide, we will cover the essential information you need to know about home insulation in NZ, including the types of insulation available, costs, and how to access the Warmer Kiwi Homes subsidy. Whether you're a homeowner looking to improve your living space or a landlord seeking to comply with the Healthy Homes Standards, this article will provide you with the knowledge and resources you need to make informed decisions.

NZ Building Code H1 Requirements

The NZ Building Code H1 requirements outline the minimum insulation standards for new builds and retrofits. As of 2023, the updated requirements specify the following minimum R-values: * Ceiling insulation: R6.6 (Auckland and Northland), R6.8 (Waikato, Bay of Plenty, and Gisborne), R7.0 (Hawke's Bay, Manawatu-Wanganui, Taranaki, and Wellington) * Underfloor insulation: R2.5 (Auckland and Northland), R2.8 (Waikato, Bay of Plenty, and Gisborne), R3.2 (Hawke's Bay, Manawatu-Wanganui, Taranaki, and Wellington) * Wall insulation: R2.4 (Auckland and Northland), R2.6 (Waikato, Bay of Plenty, and Gisborne), R2.8 (Hawke's Bay, Manawatu-Wanganui, Taranaki, and Wellington)

A map of NZ climate zones can be found on the MBIE website, which helps determine the specific requirements for your region. It's essential to note that new builds and retrofits have different requirements, and it's crucial to consult with a professional to ensure compliance with the Building Code.

Types of Insulation

There are various types of insulation available for ceiling, underfloor, and wall insulation. The choice of insulation depends on factors such as climate zone, budget, and accessibility.

Ceiling Insulation

Ceiling insulation can be installed using bulk batts (polyester, glasswool, or rockwool) or blown-in loose fill. Bulk batts are a popular choice, with brands like Pink Batts, Bradford Gold, Knauf, and Autex offering high-quality products. The R-value of ceiling insulation ranges from R2.5 to R7.0, with higher R-values providing better insulation. A comparison of R-values and costs is shown in the table below:

Insulation Type R-Value Cost per m² (DIY) Cost per m² (Installed)
Bulk Batts (Polyester) R2.5 $10-$20 $30-$50
Bulk Batts (Glasswool) R3.6 $15-$30 $40-$70
Blown-in Loose Fill R4.0 $20-$40 $50-$100
Rockwool Batts R6.0 $30-$50 $70-$120

Underfloor Insulation

Underfloor insulation can be installed using foil, bulk batts stapled up, or rigid foam boards. Foil insulation is not recommended, as it has limited effectiveness and may be banned in some areas due to degradation over time. Bulk batts stapled up are a more effective option, but can be tricky to install. Rigid foam boards offer high R-values but are more expensive.

Wall Insulation

Wall insulation is the most challenging to retrofit, as it often requires significant construction work. Blown-in insulation (polystyrene beads or glasswool) is a common method, but can be expensive. Internal rigid foam boards or external wrap insulation are also options, but are usually only done during recladding.

Warmer Kiwi Homes Subsidy

The Warmer Kiwi Homes subsidy is an EECA programme that provides financial assistance to low-income households to improve their home insulation. To be eligible, you must hold a Community Services Card or live in a low-income area. The subsidy amount is up to 80% of the insulation cost, with a cap of $2,500 for home insulation and $500 for heating. To apply, you must contact a registered provider, who will assess your eligibility and provide a quote for the insulation work.

Ceiling Insulation Costs

The cost of ceiling insulation varies depending on the type and R-value of the insulation. For a 100m² ceiling, the cost of Pink Batts R3.6 insulation is around $700 (DIY) to $1,800-$3,500 (installed). For a code-compliant R6.6 insulation, the cost is around $2,800-$5,000 (installed).

Underfloor Insulation Costs

The cost of underfloor insulation also varies depending on the type and R-value of the insulation. For an 80m² underfloor area, the cost of foil insulation (if permitted) is around $600-$1,500, while bulk polyester batts installed can cost between $2,000-$4,000.

Is Ceiling Insulation DIY?

Yes, ceiling insulation can be a DIY job, especially if you have an accessible ceiling space. However, it's essential to take safety precautions, such as wearing a P2 mask, eye protection, and a full coverall. No electrical work is involved, making it a relatively straightforward DIY project.

Return on Investment

The return on investment for home insulation is significant, with typical NZ heating cost savings ranging from $400-$900 per year. The payback period for insulation is around 3-8 years, depending on the type and R-value of the insulation. In addition to cost savings, insulation also improves comfort and health benefits, particularly for rentals that must comply with the Healthy Homes Standards.

Healthy Homes Standards for Landlords

The Healthy Homes Standards, introduced in 2019, set minimum insulation requirements for rental properties. Landlords must ensure that their rental properties meet the minimum R-value requirements for ceiling insulation, which range from R2.5 to R6.6, depending on the climate zone. Compliance deadlines vary, but most landlords must comply by July 2024.

  4. How long does insulation last? The lifespan of insulation depends on the type and quality of the insulation. Most insulation products have a lifespan of 20-50 years, depending on the manufacturer's warranty and maintenance.
