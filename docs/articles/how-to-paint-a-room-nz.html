How to Paint a Room NZ — Step-by-Step Guide

Published 29 June 2026 · NZ Tradie Tools
paintingDIYhomeownerNZ2026

Introduction

Painting a room is one of the most rewarding and cost-effective DIY jobs for New Zealand homeowners. Not only can it completely transform the look and feel of a space, but it's also a great way to add value to your property. With the right tools, materials, and a bit of patience, you can achieve professional-looking results without breaking the bank. In this comprehensive guide, we'll take you through the step-by-step process of painting a room in NZ, covering everything from preparation to completion. By the end of this article, you'll be equipped with the knowledge and confidence to tackle your next painting project with ease.

What You'll Need

Before you start painting, it's essential to have the right tools and materials for the job. Here's a list of what you'll need:

  • A high-quality roller and extension pole (approx. $50-$100)
  • A paint tray (approx. $20-$50)
  • A selection of brushes, including a 2.5" angled sash brush (approx. $10-$30)
  • Drop cloths (approx. $20-$50)
  • Masking tape (approx. $5-$10)
  • Filler (e.g., Polyfilla) (approx. $10-$20)
  • Sandpaper (approx. $5-$10)
  • Sugar soap (approx. $10-$20)
  • Paint (see below for types and prices)

When it comes to paint, there are several types to choose from, including water-based and oil-based. In NZ, water-based paints are the most popular choice, and brands like Resene, Dulux, and Wattyl offer a wide range of options. Water-based paints are generally easier to clean up and have less odour than oil-based paints, but they may not be as durable. Oil-based paints, on the other hand, are more resistant to wear and tear, but they can take longer to dry and have stronger fumes.

The finish type you choose will also depend on the room and the look you're going for. Flat (matte) finishes are great for ceilings and low-traffic areas, while low-sheen finishes are suitable for most walls. Semi-gloss and gloss finishes are best for trim and doors, as they're easy to clean and resistant to moisture.

To calculate how much paint you'll need, you'll need to consider the surface area of the room and the coverage rate of the paint. Most paints in NZ have a coverage rate of around 12-16 square metres per litre, depending on the surface. A standard bedroom, for example, might require around 2-3 litres of paint, depending on the colour and finish.

Prep: The Key to a Good Result

Preparation is the most critical step in painting a room. It's what sets the stage for a smooth, even finish and ensures that your paint job lasts for years to come. Here's what you need to do:

  • Wash the walls with sugar soap to remove grease, grime, and old smoke residue. This will help the paint adhere better and prevent stains from showing through.
  • Fill any holes or cracks with a filler like Polyfilla. Sand back the filler once it's dry to create a smooth surface.
  • Sand any rough patches or areas with old paint to create a key for the new paint to adhere to.
  • Mask up skirting boards, architraves, and power points with masking tape to prevent paint from getting on these areas.
  • Protect floors with drop cloths to prevent paint spills and stains.
  • Prime new GIB or repaired patches with a primer to ensure a uniform finish. If you're using a different colour, it's essential to prime the area first to prevent the old colour from showing through.

Order of Painting

When it comes to painting a room, the order in which you paint is crucial. Here's the recommended order:

  1. Ceiling: Start by painting the ceiling, as this will prevent any paint from getting on the walls or trim.
  2. Walls: Once the ceiling is dry, move on to the walls. Work in sections, using a roller to cover large areas and a brush to cut in around edges and corners.
  3. Trim: Finally, paint the trim, including skirting boards, architraves, and doors.

The reason for this order is to prevent lap marks, which can occur when paint is applied over a previously painted area. By painting the ceiling first, you can work from top to bottom, preventing any paint from getting on the walls or trim.

Cutting In

Cutting in is the process of painting the edges and corners of a room, where the walls meet the ceiling, trim, or other surfaces. It's a critical step in painting a room, as it requires a steady hand and a bit of patience. Here's how to do it:

  • Use an angled brush to cut in around edges and corners. Hold the brush at a 45-degree angle, with the bristles pointing towards the surface you're painting.
  • Use the 3-wet-edge technique to prevent lap marks. This involves painting a section of the wall, then moving on to the next section, while the first section is still wet.
  • Take your time and work slowly, as rushing the cutting-in process can lead to mistakes and uneven lines.

Rolling

Rolling is the fastest way to cover large areas of a room. Here's how to do it:

  • Use a W-pattern to roll the paint onto the wall, working in sections.
  • Keep a wet edge by maintaining a consistent pace and not allowing the paint to dry out.
  • Avoid applying too much pressure, which can create roller marks.
  • Apply a second coat once the first coat is touch dry, but not bone dry. This will help prevent the paint from becoming too thick and uneven.

Trim and Skirting

When it comes to painting trim and skirting, it's essential to use the right brush and technique. Here's what you need to do:

  • Use a high-quality brush with synthetic bristles, which will hold up better to water-based paints.
  • Apply a thin coat of paint, working in small sections.
  • Use a gentle, smooth motion to paint the trim, taking care not to get paint on the surrounding surfaces.

Drying Times and Second Coats

The drying time for paint in NZ will depend on the brand, type, and environmental conditions. Here are some general guidelines:

  • Resene and Dulux paints typically take around 2-4 hours to dry, depending on the temperature and humidity.
  • Wattyl paints may take longer to dry, up to 6 hours in some cases.
  • It's essential to wait until the paint is touch dry before applying a second coat. This will help prevent the paint from becoming too thick and uneven.

Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

Here are some common mistakes to avoid when painting a room:

  • Skipping primer: Failing to prime new GIB or repaired patches can lead to a uneven finish and poor paint adhesion.
  • Painting over dusty or dirty surfaces: This can lead to a poor paint finish and reduced durability.
  • Using the wrong roller nap: Using a roller with the wrong nap length can lead to roller marks and an uneven finish.
  • Painting in direct sunlight: This can cause the paint to dry too quickly, leading to an uneven finish and poor adhesion.

FAQ

Here are some frequently asked questions about painting a room in NZ:

  • Q: How long does it take to paint a room? A: The time it takes to paint a room will depend on the size of the room, the number of coats, and the complexity of the job. On average, it can take around 2-5 days to paint a standard bedroom.
  • Q: How much paint do I need for a standard bedroom? A: The amount of paint you'll need will depend on the surface area of the room and the coverage rate of the paint. A standard bedroom typically requires around 2-3 litres of paint.
  • Q: Can I paint over mould? A: No, it's not recommended to paint over mould. Mould can continue to grow under the paint, causing damage to the surface and potentially leading to health problems. It's essential to remove the mould before painting.
  • Q: What's the best paint for NZ kitchens and bathrooms? A: The best paint for NZ kitchens and bathrooms will depend on the specific conditions and requirements of the room. Generally, a water-based paint with a semi-gloss or gloss finish is recommended, as it's easy to clean and resistant to moisture.
  • Q: How much does it cost to paint a room in NZ? A: The cost of painting a room in NZ will depend on the size of the room, the type of paint, and the complexity of the job. On average, the cost of materials can range from $150 to $300 for a standard bedroom.
