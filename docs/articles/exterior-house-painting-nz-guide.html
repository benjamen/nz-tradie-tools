Exterior House Painting NZ 2026 — Costs and How to Prep Properly

Published 29 June 2026 · NZ Tradie Tools
exterior paintingpaintinghomeownercostsNZ2026

Introduction

Exterior house painting is one of the most important maintenance jobs on a New Zealand home. The country's unique combination of high UV exposure, heavy rainfall, and strong winds can take a toll on a house's exterior, causing paint to deteriorate and leaving the underlying surfaces vulnerable to damage. In NZ, it's essential to repaint your house's exterior before it's too late, as neglecting to do so can lead to costly repairs down the line. This comprehensive guide will cover everything you need to know about exterior house painting in NZ, including how often to repaint, surface types and considerations, prep work, paint selection, and costs. Whether you're a homeowner looking to DIY or hire a professional, this guide will provide you with the knowledge and tools to get the job done right.

How Often Does Exterior Paint Last in NZ?

The lifespan of exterior paint in NZ varies depending on the region and climate. In coastal areas like Northland and Auckland, exterior paint typically lasts between 7-10 years, while in central NZ, it can last 10-15 years. In the southern regions of Canterbury and Otago, where the climate is cooler and drier, exterior paint can last 12-18 years. However, these are general guidelines, and the actual lifespan of exterior paint will depend on various factors, including the quality of the paint, the surface it's applied to, and the level of maintenance. Signs that it's time to repaint include peeling, chalking, cracking, bare timber showing, and mould that won't wash off. If you notice any of these signs, it's essential to address the issue promptly to prevent further damage.

Surface Types and Considerations

Different surface types require different approaches when it comes to exterior painting. Here are some common surface types and considerations:

  • Weatherboard (timber): Weatherboard is one of the most prep-intensive surfaces, requiring sanding and priming of bare patches. It's essential to use a flexible paint, such as Resene Lumbersider or Dulux Weathershield, to ensure a long-lasting finish.
  • Fibrous cement (Hardiflex): Fibrous cement surfaces can contain asbestos if they were installed before 1990, so it's crucial to test for asbestos before sanding. If your surface is asbestos-free, you can use a paint like Resene Lumbersider or Dulux 10yr exterior.
  • Plaster/render (solid plaster, roughcast, monolithic): Plaster surfaces require breathable paint to prevent moisture buildup. Resene Roof & Spouting or specialist plaster paints are good options. If you have a monolithic plaster home, it's essential to check for leaky home symptoms before painting.
  • Colosteel/metal surfaces: Metal surfaces require a rust-inhibiting primer and a direct-to-metal top coat to ensure a durable finish.

Prep — The Most Important Step

Preparation is the most critical step in exterior painting. Here are some essential prep tasks:

  • Water blasting: Hire a water blaster for $80-$150 per day or hire a contractor for $300-$800 to blast your entire house.
  • Drying time: Allow 2-3 fine days for the surface to dry before painting.
  • Scrape and sand: Scrape off peeling paint and sand the surface to create a smooth finish.
  • Fill cracks: Fill cracks with exterior-grade filler, such as Resene Filler or Selleys No More Gaps exterior.
  • Spot prime: Spot prime bare timber and repairs to ensure a uniform finish.
  • Mask up: Mask up glass and trim where you're changing the colour to prevent paint from getting on these surfaces.

Paint Selection

When it comes to exterior paint, there are two main types: water-based and oil-based. In NZ, most painters now use water-based exterior paint due to its faster drying time, better flexibility, and lower VOC (volatile organic compound) levels. When selecting a paint, consider the sheen level: low-sheen on walls and semi-gloss on trim. Resene, Dulux, and Wattyl are the three main NZ brands with full exterior systems.

Number of Coats

One coat of paint is rarely enough on exterior surfaces. Two coats are standard, but three coats may be necessary on previously bare or heavily sanded surfaces.

DIY vs Hire a Painter

A full exterior repaint of a 3-bedroom house is a significant job that requires 2-3 people for a week. It involves working at heights and accessing difficult areas, which can be risky. While some homeowners may be able to paint ground-level and garage areas, two-storey houses require scaffolding, which can be hired for $400-$1,200 per week. Professional painters have the right equipment and expertise to do the job safely and efficiently. They also use tools like Xero for invoicing and Fergus for job management to streamline their work.

Exterior Painting Costs NZ

The cost of exterior painting in NZ varies depending on the surface type, size of the house, and location. Here are some estimated costs:

  • Professional painter — weatherboard 3-bed house: $6,000-$15,000 (includes prep, materials, and 2 coats on walls and trim)
  • Professional painter — plaster house (less prep): $5,000-$12,000
  • Per m² rate: $20-$45 per m² of wall area (surfaces vary significantly)
  • Scaffolding (if required): $800-$2,500 extra
  • Materials only (DIY): $1,500-$4,000 for a 3-bed house

How to Get the Best Painter Quote

To get the best painter quote, ensure you provide a written scope that includes:

  • Prep details
  • Number of coats
  • Paint brand and system specified
  • What's included (spouting, eaves, deck?) Get at least three quotes from different painters and compare them to find the best value for your money. You can also use tools like Tradify to manage your quotes and invoices.

FAQ

Here are some frequently asked questions about exterior house painting in NZ:

  • How often should you paint the exterior of a house in NZ?: The frequency of exterior painting depends on the region and climate, but generally, it's every 7-18 years.
  • How do I get rid of mould before painting?: Use a mould-killing solution and scrub the area before rinsing and drying.
  • Can I paint fibrous cement cladding myself?: Yes, but ensure you test for asbestos first and take necessary safety precautions.
  • What's the best exterior paint for NZ conditions?: Water-based paints from Resene, Dulux, or Wattyl are popular choices for NZ's climate.
  • Do painters supply the paint or do I?: It depends on the agreement; some painters may supply the paint, while others may require you to purchase it.

Conclusion

Exterior house painting is a significant maintenance job that requires careful planning, preparation, and execution. By following this guide, you'll be well-equipped to tackle the job yourself or hire a professional painter. Remember to always follow safety guidelines and use high-quality materials to ensure a long-lasting finish.
