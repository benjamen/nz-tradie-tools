House Painting Cost NZ 2026 — Interior & Exterior Prices for Homeowners

Published 14 June 2026 · NZ Tradie Tools
paintinginteriorexteriorcostsNZ2026

A fresh coat of paint is one of the highest-return home improvements in New Zealand, but getting an accurate budget before calling painters isn't straightforward — quotes vary enormously depending on how the job is scoped. This guide gives you real 2026 New Zealand prices for interior and exterior painting, broken down by job type, so you know what to expect before the first painter walks through the door.

Note: This guide is written for homeowners. If you're a painter looking for advice on pricing your own jobs, see our painter pricing guide for NZ tradies.

Auckland and Wellington typically run 10–20% above these national figures. Painter availability is tighter in the main centres, and overheads are higher.

What Drives Painting Costs

Surface Preparation

Preparation is the biggest factor in both cost and outcome. A house that hasn't been painted for 15+ years needs water blasting, scraping, sanding, filling cracks and holes, and possibly a full prime coat before topcoats go on. This can add $1,000–$3,000 to an exterior repaint. A well-maintained house that was last painted 8 years ago needs far less prep.

Ask your painter: "What preparation is included?" If the answer is vague, request an itemised breakdown. Poor prep is the leading cause of paint failure.

Paint Quality

Premium NZ paints (Resene Zylone Sheen, Dulux Weathershield) cost $70–$120 per 10-litre pail. Budget paints run $30–$50. The difference matters: premium paint has better UV resistance, better pigment loading, and better adhesion. In NZ's UV intensity, skimping on paint quality often means repainting 3–4 years earlier than necessary.

Scaffolding

Any two-storey exterior requires scaffolding for safe working at height. Scaffolding hire typically adds $1,500–$4,000 to the job, depending on how much of the house needs coverage and how long it's up. Most painters factor this into their quote — confirm it's included, not extra.

Number of Coats

A full exterior repaint should include two topcoats minimum — one on bare or primed areas may not be enough for colour consistency and durability. Interior repaints are typically two coats; new builds often need a primer coat plus two finish coats. Any quote offering "one coat" for an exterior repaint should be questioned.

DIY vs Professional Painting

For small interior jobs (a single room, a hallway), DIY is genuinely viable and saves $500–$1,500. For anything larger — exterior, multi-room interior, ceilings, or trim — professional painters offer real advantages:

  • Speed: A two-person crew can repaint a full 3-bedroom interior in 3–4 days. DIY typically takes a weekend per room.
  • Trade paint pricing: Painters buy paint at trade prices, typically 15–25% below retail. This partially offsets their labour charge on larger jobs.
  • Quality finish: Cutting in, rolling technique, and surface prep all take skill that shows in the finished result.
  • Workmanship warranty: Most professional painters offer a 2–5 year warranty on their work. DIY has no warranty.

Resene vs Dulux — Which Paint?

Both are premium NZ market leaders with equivalent quality at similar price points. Resene is NZ-owned and has long-standing local R&D for NZ climate conditions. Dulux is part of Akzo Nobel (global). Which brand your painter uses often comes down to what they buy at their trade account — both are excellent.

The brand matters less than the product grade. Compare products in the same tier: Resene Zylone Sheen vs Dulux Weathershield. Don't compare a premium Resene product against a budget Dulux product and draw conclusions about the brand.

Tips for Getting the Best Painting Quote

  • Get 3 written quotes — painter pricing varies substantially
  • Get quotes in spring — painters book up in December–February; spring quotes are more competitive and get better scheduling
  • Specify paint brand and grade — ask what product is being used; don't let "quality paint" go undefined
  • Clarify exactly what's included — "exterior" could mean all four sides or just the street-facing side; confirm in writing
  • Ask about prep — what surface preparation is included? Water blasting? Sanding? Filling? Prime coat where needed?
  • Check they're insured — ask for a certificate of public liability insurance before any painter starts work

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does it cost to paint a house in NZ?

A 3-bedroom interior (walls, ceilings, trim) typically costs $5,000–$10,000. An exterior repaint of a single-storey 3-bedroom runs $6,000–$14,000; two-storey with scaffolding is $12,000–$25,000. Single rooms are $600–$1,500. Add 10–20% for Auckland and Wellington.

How long does an exterior house paint job last in NZ?

A quality exterior repaint with premium paint and proper surface preparation typically lasts 8–12 years. Poor prep or budget paint can fail in 4–5 years. UV exposure is high in NZ; products rated for NZ conditions (Resene, Dulux Weathershield) last significantly longer than basic exterior paints.

Should I use Resene or Dulux paint?

Both are premium brands with equivalent quality. Resene is NZ-owned with strong local climate research. Dulux is a global brand with broad product range. The product grade matters more than the brand — compare like-for-like (premium vs premium). Most painters use the brand they have a trade account with and can get at best price.

Do I need to prepare the surface before the painter arrives?

Surface preparation is the painter's job and should be itemised in the quote. Before they arrive, clear rooms of furniture, remove picture hooks, and patch obvious large holes if you want to save cost. The exterior prep (water blasting, scraping, sanding, filling) is a major part of the job — make sure it's explicitly included in the quote.

How do I get an accurate painting quote?

Get 3 written quotes and specify: how many coats, what paint brand and grade, whether ceilings and trim are included, and for exterior quotes whether scaffolding is included. Compare the full scope, not just the bottom-line price. The cheapest quote often has a narrower scope.
