How to Build a Fence NZ — DIY Guide

Introduction to Building a Fence in NZ

Building a fence is a popular DIY project in New Zealand, and for good reason. Not only can it add value to your property, but it can also provide a sense of security and privacy. However, before you start digging, it's essential to determine whether you can DIY the project or if you need to hire a professional. Generally, if you're building a simple fence, you can DIY it, but if you're dealing with complex designs or large areas, it's best to consult a professional. This comprehensive guide will walk you through the step-by-step process of building a fence in NZ, covering everything from materials to painting or staining. Whether you're a seasoned DIYer or a beginner, this guide will provide you with the knowledge and confidence to tackle your fencing project.

Do You Need Building Consent?

Before you start building your fence, it's crucial to check if you need building consent. According to the Building Act 2004, Schedule 1, fences under 2.5m in height do not require a building consent. However, it's essential to check your district plan for boundary setbacks and height rules, as these can vary depending on your location. For example, if you live in a heritage area or a coastal zone, there may be specific rules and regulations you need to follow. Additionally, if you're building a pool fence, there are separate mandatory requirements you must comply with. The Fencing of Swimming Pools Act 1987 requires that all swimming pools be fenced with a barrier that meets specific safety standards. It's always best to consult with your local council or a professional to ensure you're meeting all the necessary requirements.

Materials for Your Fence

When it comes to choosing materials for your fence, there are several options to consider. The most common materials used for fencing in NZ are timber, metal, and concrete.

Timber

Timber is a popular choice for fencing, and for good reason. It's durable, versatile, and can be stained or painted to match your home's exterior. When choosing timber for your fence, it's essential to select the right type and grade. For posts, you'll want to use H3.2 treated pine, which is resistant to rot and insect damage. If the posts will be in contact with the ground, you'll need to use H4 treated pine, which has a higher level of treatment. Other options for timber fencing include macrocarpa and cedar, which are naturally resistant to rot and insect damage. When choosing post size, a minimum of 90x90mm is recommended for a 1.8m fence.

Metal

Metal fencing is another popular option, and it's available in a range of materials, including Colorbond, tubular steel, and aluminium. Metal fencing is durable, low-maintenance, and can be powder-coated to match your home's exterior. It's also a great option for coastal areas, as it's resistant to salt damage.

Concrete

Concrete fencing is a cost-effective option that's quick to install. Pre-cast concrete panels are available in a range of sizes and can be used to create a solid, durable fence. However, concrete fencing can be heavy, so it's essential to ensure your posts are sturdy enough to support the weight.

Bamboo and Privacy Screen Options

If you're looking for a more unique and eco-friendly option, bamboo fencing is a great choice. Bamboo is a sustainable, durable material that's resistant to rot and insect damage. You can also use bamboo to create a privacy screen, which is perfect for blocking out neighbours or creating a sense of seclusion.

Post Caps

To prevent water ingress and extend the life of your fence, it's essential to use post caps. Post caps are available in a range of materials, including metal and plastic, and can be screwed or nailed onto the top of your posts.

Laying Out the Fence Line

Before you start digging, it's essential to lay out your fence line. This involves marking the boundary of your property and determining the position of your posts. To do this, you'll need a string line, spirit level, and a measuring tape. Start by marking the corners of your property, then run a string line between the marks to create a straight line. Use a spirit level to ensure the line is level, then mark the position of your posts, spacing them 1.8-2.4m apart for timber fencing.

Setting Posts

Setting posts is the most critical step in building a fence. If you get this wrong, everything else will follow suit. To set posts, you'll need to dig holes that are 1/3 of the post length in the ground, minimum. For a 1.8m fence, this would be approximately 600mm deep. The hole should also be 3 times the width of the post to allow for concrete. Once you've dug the holes, fill the first 100mm with compacted soil, then add rapid-set concrete. Use a spirit level to ensure the post is plumb, then brace it with temporary supports while the concrete sets. This can take anywhere from 24-48 hours, depending on the weather.

Rails and Palings

Once your posts are set, you can start installing your rails and palings. The rail height will depend on the type of fence you're building, but generally, it's around 1.2-1.5m from the ground. Use galvanised fixings to attach the palings to the rails, and space them 15mm apart to allow for swelling. It's essential to check the level of your fence as you go to ensure it's straight and even.

Gates

Gates are an essential part of any fence, and they require special attention. When building a gate, it's crucial to use hinge sizing that's suitable for the weight of the gate. You'll also need to choose a latch type that's secure and easy to use. To ensure the gate frame is sturdy, brace it diagonally with a piece of timber. Finally, allow clearance for ground movement to prevent the gate from binding.

Painting or Staining

Once your fence is built, you'll need to protect it from the elements. You can do this by painting or staining the timber. Penetrating oil, such as Cutek or Deks Olje D1, is a great option for bare treated pine, as it helps to extend the life of the timber. If you prefer to paint, use a high-quality exterior paint that's suitable for timber. It's essential to re-oil or re-paint your fence regularly to maintain its appearance and durability.

Common Mistakes

When building a fence, there are several common mistakes to avoid. These include:

  • Posts not deep enough
  • Wrong timber treatment rating
  • No post caps
  • Palings butted too tight, which can cause them to swell and warp
  • Not checking for underground services before digging

By avoiding these mistakes, you can ensure your fence is sturdy, durable, and long-lasting.

Frequently Asked Questions

Here are some frequently asked questions about building a fence in NZ:

  • Q: How close to the boundary can I build a fence? A: The distance you can build a fence from the boundary will depend on your local council's rules and regulations. Generally, you can build a fence up to the boundary, but it's essential to check with your neighbour and local council before starting the project.
  • Q: How long will a treated pine fence last? A: A treated pine fence can last anywhere from 10-20 years, depending on the quality of the timber and the conditions it's exposed to.
  • Q: Do I need my neighbour's agreement for a boundary fence? A: Yes, if you're building a fence on a shared boundary, you'll need to get your neighbour's agreement before starting the project. It's essential to discuss the design, materials, and cost with your neighbour to ensure you're both on the same page.
  • Q: How much does it cost to hire a fencer? A: The cost of hiring a fencer will depend on the type of fence, materials, and location. On average, you can expect to pay between $80-$160 per metre for a professional fence installation.

A: Using a job management tool like Fergus or Tradify can help you streamline your business, manage your finances, and communicate with your clients more effectively. For example, you can use Xero to manage your invoicing and accounting, and Fergus to manage your jobs and scheduling.

Conclusion and Next Steps

Building a fence in NZ can be a rewarding DIY project, but it requires careful planning and attention to detail. By following the steps outlined in this guide, you can create a sturdy, durable fence that will last for years to come.
