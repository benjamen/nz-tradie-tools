How to Build a Fence NZ — DIY Guide

Published 24 June 2026 · NZ Tradie Tools
fenceDIYhomeownerNZbuilding consent2026

Introduction

Fencing is a popular DIY project in New Zealand, and for good reason. Not only can it add value to your property, but it can also provide a sense of security and privacy. However, before you start building, it's essential to determine whether you can DIY or if you need to hire a professional. If your fence is under 2.5m high and doesn't require a building consent, you can likely tackle the project yourself. On the other hand, if your fence is higher than 2.5m or requires a building consent, it's best to hire a licensed builder or fencer. This guide will walk you through the step-by-step process of building a fence in NZ, covering everything from materials to painting or staining. Whether you're a seasoned DIYer or a beginner, this guide will provide you with the knowledge and confidence to build a high-quality fence that will last for years to come.

Do you need building consent?

Before you start building your fence, it's crucial to check if you need a building consent. Under Schedule 1 of the Building Act 2004, fences under 2.5m high do not require a building consent. However, it's essential to check your district plan for boundary setbacks and height rules, as these can vary depending on your location. For example, if you live in a residential area, you may be required to set your fence back a certain distance from the boundary. Pool fencing, on the other hand, has separate mandatory requirements, and you will need to ensure that your fence meets these requirements to avoid any potential safety risks. You can check with your local council or visit the MBIE website to determine if you need a building consent for your fence.

Materials

When it comes to choosing materials for your fence, there are several options to consider. Here are some of the most popular materials used for fencing in NZ:

  • Timber: H3.2 treated pine is a popular choice for fence posts, as it is resistant to rot and insect damage. However, if you're planning to put the posts in the ground, you'll need to use a minimum of H4 treated timber. Other options include macrocarpa and cedar, which are naturally resistant to rot and insect damage. When choosing post size, a minimum of 90x90mm is recommended for a 1.8m high fence.
  • Metal: Colorbond, tubular steel, and aluminium are all popular choices for metal fencing. These materials are durable, low-maintenance, and can be powder-coated to match your desired colour.
  • Concrete: Pre-cast concrete panels are a cheap and quick option for fencing, but they can be heavy and require additional support.
  • Bamboo and privacy screen options: Bamboo and privacy screens are great options for adding a natural, rustic touch to your fence. They can also provide additional privacy and security.
  • Post caps: Post caps are essential for preventing water ingress and rot. They can be made from a variety of materials, including metal, plastic, and timber.

The cost of materials can vary depending on the type and quality of the material. On average, you can expect to pay between $25-$60 per metre for DIY fencing materials. Here is a rough estimate of the cost of different materials:

Laying out the fence line

Before you start setting posts, it's essential to lay out the fence line. Here's how to do it:

  • Use a string line and spirit level to mark out the fence line.
  • Ensure the line is straight and level.
  • Mark the location of the corner posts and any intermediate posts.
  • Use a measuring tape to check the post spacing, which should be between 1.8-2.4m centres for timber posts.

Setting posts

Setting posts is the most critical step in building a fence. If you get this wrong, everything else will follow suit. Here's how to set posts:

  • Dig holes for the posts, making sure they are at least 1/3 of the post length in the ground. For a 1.8m high fence, this would be approximately 600mm deep.
  • The hole diameter should be at least 3 times the post width.
  • Tamp the first 100mm of soil to create a solid base.
  • Fill the hole with rapid-set concrete, making sure to fill it to the top.
  • Use a spirit level to ensure the post is plumb.
  • Bracing the post while the concrete sets is essential. This can take anywhere from 24-48 hours, depending on the weather.

Rails and palings

Once the posts are set, you can start attaching the rails and palings. Here's how to do it:

  • Rail height should be approximately 100mm from the top of the post.
  • Use galvanised fixings to attach the palings to the rails. Do not use untreated fixings, as they will rust in NZ's humid climate.
  • Paling spacing should be approximately 15mm to allow for swelling.
  • Check the level as you go to ensure the fence is straight and level.

Gates

Gates are an essential part of any fence. Here's how to build a gate:

  • Hinge sizing is critical. Ensure the hinges are large enough to support the weight of the gate.
  • Use a latch that is secure and easy to use.
  • Bracing the gate frame diagonally will provide additional support and prevent the gate from sagging.
  • Allow clearance for ground movement to prevent the gate from getting stuck.

Painting or staining

Once the fence is built, you can start painting or staining it. Here are some options:

  • Penetrating oil, such as Cutek or Deks Olje D1, is a great option for protecting the timber and enhancing its natural colour.
  • Paint is also an option, but it's essential to use a high-quality exterior paint that can withstand the elements.
  • Bare treated pine can last longer with oil, as it allows the timber to breathe and reduces the risk of rot and insect damage.
  • Re-oiling is recommended every 1-2 years, depending on the weather conditions.

Common mistakes

Here are some common mistakes to avoid when building a fence:

  • Posts not deep enough: This can cause the fence to lean or collapse.
  • Wrong timber treatment rating: Using the wrong treatment rating can lead to rot and insect damage.
  • No post caps: Post caps are essential for preventing water ingress and rot.
  • Palings butted too tight: This can cause the palings to swell and warp, leading to a uneven fence.
  • Not checking for underground services: Before digging, it's essential to check for underground services, such as power and water lines.

FAQ

Here are some frequently asked questions about building a fence in NZ:

  • How close to the boundary can I build a fence?: The distance from the boundary will depend on your local council's regulations. It's best to check with your local council before building your fence.
  • How long will a treated pine fence last?: A treated pine fence can last anywhere from 10-20 years, depending on the quality of the timber and the weather conditions.
  • Do I need my neighbour's agreement for a boundary fence?: Yes, it's recommended to get your neighbour's agreement before building a boundary fence. This can help prevent any potential disputes or issues.
  • How much does it cost to hire a fencer?: The cost of hiring a fencer can vary depending on the location, materials, and complexity of the job. On average, you can expect to pay between $80-$160 per metre for professional fencing services.

Conclusion

By following this guide, you can build a high-quality fence that will last for years to come.
