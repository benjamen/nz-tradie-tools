How to Build a Fence NZ — DIY Guide

Published 25 June 2026 · NZ Tradie Tools
fenceDIYhomeownerNZbuilding consent2026

Introduction

Fencing is a popular DIY project in New Zealand, and for good reason. Not only can it add value to your property, but it can also provide a sense of security and privacy. However, before you start building, it's essential to determine whether you can DIY or if you need to hire a professional. Generally, if your fence is under 2.5m high and doesn't require any complex design or engineering, you can tackle it yourself. But, if you're unsure or need a more intricate design, it's best to consult a professional. This comprehensive guide will walk you through the step-by-step process of building a fence in NZ, covering everything from materials to painting or staining. Whether you're a seasoned DIYer or a beginner, this guide will provide you with the knowledge and confidence to build a high-quality fence that will last for years to come.

When it comes to managing your DIY project, it's essential to have the right tools and software. Consider using job management and invoicing tools like Xero, Fergus, or Tradify to streamline your workflow and keep track of your expenses. These tools can help you organise your project, create quotes, and manage your finances, making it easier to stay on top of your DIY project.

Do you need building consent?

Before you start building your fence, it's crucial to check if you need a building consent. Under Schedule 1 of the Building Act 2004, fences under 2.5m high do not require a building consent. However, it's essential to check your district plan for boundary setbacks and height rules, as these can vary depending on your location. For example, if you live in a coastal area, you may need to comply with specific regulations regarding fence height and design. Additionally, if you're building a pool fence, there are separate mandatory requirements that you must comply with. The Fencing of Swimming Pools Act 1987 requires that all swimming pools be fenced with a barrier that meets specific safety standards. It's always best to check with your local council to determine if you need a building consent or to clarify any specific requirements.

To ensure compliance with these regulations, it's a good idea to consult with a professional, such as a registered builder or a certified fencer. They can provide you with guidance on the specific requirements for your area and help you navigate the process of obtaining a building consent, if needed.

Materials

When it comes to choosing materials for your fence, there are several options to consider. Here are some of the most common materials used for fencing in NZ:

  • Timber: H3.2 treated pine is a popular choice for fence posts, as it's durable and resistant to rot. However, if the posts will be in contact with the ground, it's recommended to use H4 treated timber. Other options include macrocarpa and cedar, which are naturally resistant to rot and can add a touch of elegance to your fence. When choosing post size, a minimum of 90x90mm is recommended for a 1.8m fence.
  • Metal: Colorbond, tubular steel, and aluminium are all popular choices for metal fencing. They're durable, low-maintenance, and can be powder-coated to match your desired colour.
  • Concrete: Pre-cast concrete panels are a cheap and quick option for fencing, but they can be heavy and may require additional support.
  • Bamboo and privacy screens: Bamboo and privacy screens are a great option for adding a touch of natural beauty to your fence. They're also a good choice for creating a sense of seclusion and blocking out noise.
  • Post caps: Post caps are an essential component of any fence, as they help to prevent water from entering the post and causing rot. Choose post caps that are made from durable materials, such as metal or plastic, and are designed to fit your specific post size.

The cost of materials can vary depending on the type and quality of the material. On average, you can expect to pay between $25-$60 per metre for DIY fencing materials. However, if you're hiring a professional, the cost can range from $80-$160 per metre, depending on the complexity of the design and the materials used.

Laying out the fence line

Before you start digging, it's essential to lay out the fence line. Here's how:

  • String line: Use a string line to mark out the fence line, ensuring it's straight and level.
  • Spirit level: Use a spirit level to check that the string line is level and plumb.
  • Post spacing: Space your posts 1.8-2.4m apart, depending on the type of fence you're building. For a timber fence, 1.8m is a good starting point.
  • Corner posts: Use corner posts to create a strong and stable corner, ensuring that the fence line is secure and level.

Setting posts

Setting posts is the most critical step in building a fence. If you get this wrong, everything else will follow suit. Here's how to set posts correctly:

  • Hole depth: Dig holes that are at least 1/3 of the post length in the ground, typically 600mm for a 1.8m fence.
  • Diameter: Make sure the hole is 3x the width of the post to allow for concrete and drainage.
  • Tamping: Tamp the first 100mm of soil to create a solid base for the post.
  • Rapid-set concrete: Use rapid-set concrete to set the post, following the manufacturer's instructions.
  • Keeping posts plumb: Use a spirit level to ensure the post is plumb and level, and use temporary bracing to hold it in place while the concrete sets.
  • Bracing: Leave the bracing in place for 24-48 hours to allow the concrete to set properly.

Rails and palings

Once your posts are set, it's time to add the rails and palings. Here's how:

  • Rail height: Space your rails 1.2-1.5m apart, depending on the type of fence you're building.
  • Nailing vs screwing: Use galvanised nails or screws to attach the palings to the rails. Nailing is a more traditional method, but screwing provides a stronger hold.
  • Paling spacing: Leave a 15mm gap between each paling to allow for swelling and shrinkage.
  • Checking level: Check the level of the fence as you go, ensuring that it's straight and plumb.
  • Galvanised fixings: Use galvanised fixings to prevent rust and corrosion, especially in NZ's humid climate.

Gates

Gates are an essential component of any fence, providing access to your property. Here's how to build a gate:

  • Hinge sizing: Choose hinges that are suitable for the size and weight of your gate.
  • Latch type: Choose a latch that is secure and easy to use, such as a magnetic latch or a traditional gate latch.
  • Bracing: Brace the gate frame diagonally to provide additional strength and stability.
  • Clearance: Allow for clearance at the bottom of the gate to accommodate ground movement and prevent the gate from dragging.

Painting or staining

Once your fence is built, it's time to add a finish. Here's how:

  • Penetrating oil: Use a penetrating oil, such as Cutek or Deks Olje D1, to protect the timber and enhance its natural beauty.
  • Paint: If you prefer a painted finish, choose a high-quality exterior paint that is suitable for the type of timber you're using.
  • Re-oiling: Re-oil your fence every 1-2 years to maintain its appearance and protect it from the elements.

Common mistakes

Here are some common mistakes to avoid when building a fence:

  • Posts not deep enough: Make sure your posts are deep enough to provide stability and prevent the fence from toppling over.
  • Wrong timber treatment rating: Choose the right timber treatment rating for your fence, depending on the type of timber and the location.
  • No post caps: Use post caps to prevent water from entering the post and causing rot.
  • Palings butted too tight: Leave a gap between each paling to allow for swelling and shrinkage.
  • Not checking for underground services: Check for underground services before digging to avoid damaging pipes or cables.

FAQ

Here are some frequently asked questions about building a fence in NZ:

  • Q: How close to the boundary can I build a fence? A: The distance you can build a fence from the boundary depends on your local council's regulations. Typically, you can build a fence up to 1m from the boundary, but it's best to check with your local council to confirm.
  • Q: How long will a treated pine fence last? A: A treated pine fence can last for 15-20 years, depending on the quality of the timber and the maintenance.
  • Q: Do I need my neighbour's agreement for a boundary fence? A: Yes, it's recommended to get your neighbour's agreement before building a boundary fence, as it will affect both properties.
  • Q: How much does it cost to hire a fencer? A: The cost of hiring a fencer can range from $80-$160 per metre, depending on the complexity of the design and the materials used.
  • Q: Can I use untreated timber for my fence? A: No, it's not recommended to use untreated timber for your fence, as it will be prone to rot and decay. Use treated timber or naturally resistant timber, such as macrocarpa or cedar.

Conclusion

Building a fence in NZ can be a rewarding DIY project, but it's essential to follow the right steps and use the right materials. By following this guide, you'll be able to build a high-quality fence that will last for years to come. Remember to check with your local council for any specific regulations or requirements, and don't hesitate to consult a professional if you're unsure about any aspect of the project.
