How to Build a Fence NZ — DIY Guide

Published 27 June 2026 · NZ Tradie Tools
fenceDIYhomeownerNZbuilding consent2026

Introduction

Fencing is a popular DIY project in New Zealand, and for good reason. Not only can it add value to your property, but it can also provide a sense of security and privacy. However, before you start building, it's essential to determine whether you can DIY or if you need to hire a professional. Generally, if your fence is under 2.5m high and doesn't require any complex designs or materials, you can tackle the project yourself. On the other hand, if your fence requires building consent or involves working with electricity or water, it's best to leave it to the experts. In this comprehensive guide, we'll walk you through the step-by-step process of building a fence in NZ, covering everything from materials to painting or staining. Whether you're a seasoned DIYer or a beginner, this guide will provide you with the knowledge and confidence to build a sturdy and attractive fence.

Do you need building consent?

Before you start building your fence, it's crucial to check if you need building consent. Under Schedule 1 of the Building Act 2004, fences under 2.5m high don't require building consent. However, you'll still need to comply with your district plan's boundary setbacks and height rules. It's also important to note that pool fencing has separate mandatory requirements, so if you're building a fence around a pool, you'll need to ensure it meets the relevant standards. You can check with your local council or the Ministry of Business, Innovation and Employment (MBIE) to determine if you need building consent for your fence. Additionally, you can visit the MBIE website to access resources and guides on building consent and compliance.

Materials

Choosing the right materials for your fence is crucial to its durability and appearance. Here are some popular options:

  • Timber: H3.2 treated pine is a popular choice for fence posts, but if you're planning to bury the posts in the ground, you'll need to use a minimum of H4 treated pine. Other options include macrocarpa and cedar, which are naturally resistant to rot and insect damage. When choosing post size, a minimum of 90x90mm is recommended for a 1.8m high fence.
  • Metal: Colorbond, tubular steel, and aluminium are popular metal options for fencing. They're durable, low-maintenance, and can be powder-coated in a range of colours.
  • Concrete: Pre-cast concrete panels are a cheap and quick option for fencing, but they can be heavy and may require additional support.
  • Bamboo and privacy screens: Bamboo and privacy screens are great options for adding a natural, rustic touch to your fence. They're also eco-friendly and can be used to create a sense of seclusion.
  • Post caps: Post caps are essential for preventing water ingress and rot. They come in a range of materials, including metal, plastic, and timber.

When selecting materials, consider factors such as durability, maintenance, and cost. You can expect to pay between $25-$60 per metre for DIY fencing materials, depending on the type and quality of materials you choose.

Laying out the fence line

Before you start digging, it's essential to lay out the fence line accurately. Here's how:

  • Use a string line and spirit level to mark out the fence line.
  • Ensure the line is straight and level, using a spirit level to check for any deviations.
  • Mark the position of the corner posts, using a string line to guide you.
  • Space the posts at 1.8-2.4m centres, depending on the type of fence you're building.

Setting posts

Setting the posts is the most critical step in building a fence. Here's how to do it right:

  • Dig holes that are at least 1/3 of the post length in the ground, typically 600mm for a 1.8m high fence.
  • Use a post hole digger or a shovel to dig the holes, making sure they're deep and wide enough for the posts.
  • Tamp the first 100mm of soil at the bottom of the hole to create a solid base.
  • Fill the hole with rapid-set concrete, making sure to fill it to the top.
  • Use a spirit level to ensure the post is plumb, and brace it while the concrete sets (24-48 hours).

Rails and palings

Once the posts are set, you can start attaching the rails and palings. Here's how:

  • Use galvanised fixings to attach the rails to the posts, spacing them at 1.8-2.4m centres.
  • Nail or screw the palings to the rails, leaving a 15mm gap between each paling to allow for swelling.
  • Check the level of the fence as you go, making sure it's straight and even.
  • Use a string line to guide you, ensuring the palings are evenly spaced and the fence is straight.

Gates

Building a gate can be a bit tricky, but with the right techniques, you can create a sturdy and functional gate. Here's how:

  • Use a hinge sizing chart to determine the right size hinge for your gate.
  • Choose a latch type that suits your needs, such as a simple hook and eye or a more complex locking system.
  • Brace the gate frame diagonally to prevent sagging and ensure the gate opens and closes smoothly.
  • Allow clearance for ground movement, such as a 10mm gap between the gate and the post.

Painting or staining

Once your fence is built, you can add a finish to protect it from the elements. Here's how:

  • Use a penetrating oil, such as Cutek or Deks Olje D1, to protect the timber and enhance its natural colour.
  • Apply the oil according to the manufacturer's instructions, typically every 6-12 months.
  • Consider using a paint or stain if you want to add a specific colour or finish to your fence. However, keep in mind that paint can peel and flake over time, while stain can fade and discolour.

Common mistakes

Here are some common mistakes to avoid when building a fence:

  • Posts not deep enough: This can cause the fence to lean or collapse over time.
  • Wrong timber treatment rating: Using untreated timber or the wrong treatment rating can lead to rot and insect damage.
  • No post caps: Failing to use post caps can lead to water ingress and rot.
  • Palings butted too tight: Leaving no gap between palings can cause them to swell and warp over time.
  • Not checking for underground services: Failing to check for underground services, such as power or water lines, can lead to costly repairs and safety hazards.

FAQ

Here are some frequently asked questions about building a fence in NZ:

  • How close to the boundary can I build a fence?: The distance you can build a fence from the boundary depends on your local council's rules and regulations. Generally, you can build a fence up to the boundary, but you may need to obtain your neighbour's agreement or a building consent.
  • How long will a treated pine fence last?: A treated pine fence can last for 10-20 years or more, depending on the quality of the materials and the conditions it's exposed to.
  • Do I need my neighbour's agreement for a boundary fence?: Yes, if you're building a fence on a shared boundary, you'll need to obtain your neighbour's agreement or a building consent.
  • How much does it cost to hire a fencer?: The cost of hiring a fencer can vary depending on the type of fence, the materials used, and the location. On average, you can expect to pay between $80-$160 per metre for a professional fencer.

Conclusion

Building a fence in NZ can be a rewarding DIY project, but it requires careful planning and execution. By following the steps outlined in this guide, you can create a sturdy and attractive fence that will last for years to come. Remember to choose the right materials, lay out the fence line accurately, and set the posts correctly. Don't forget to add a finish to protect your fence from the elements, and consider using a job management tool like Xero, Fergus, or Tradify to streamline your workflow.
