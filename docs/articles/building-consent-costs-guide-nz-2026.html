The Ministry of Business, Innovation and Employment (MBIE) sets the building levy, while local councils determine their processing fees. What Building Consent Fees Include

Building consent fees in NZ include a range of expenses, from the MBIE building levy to council processing fees and inspection costs. Here's a breakdown of what you can expect to pay:

  • MBIE Building Levy: The MBIE building levy is 0.1% of the project value, capped at $2,609. For example, if your project value is $500,000, the MBIE levy would be $500 (0.1% of $500,000). If your project value is $2,609,000 or more, the levy would be capped at $2,609.
  • BRANZ Levy: The BRANZ levy is 0.1% of the project value over $20,000. For example, if your project value is $300,000, the BRANZ levy would be $280 (0.1% of $280,000).
  • Council Processing Fee: Council processing fees vary widely depending on the council and project type. These fees can range from a few hundred dollars to several thousand dollars.
  • Inspection Fees: Inspection fees typically range from $150 to $300 per inspection, with 4-8 inspections typical for a standard building project.
  • Engineer's Report: If required, an engineer's report can cost between $500 and $2,500, depending on the complexity of the project.

To give you a better understanding of these costs, let's consider an example. Suppose you are planning a $700,000 new build in Auckland. The MBIE building levy would be $700 (0.1% of $700,000), the BRANZ levy would be $680 (0.1% of $680,000), and the council processing fee might be around $2,000. Inspection fees could add up to $2,400 (8 inspections at $300 each), and an engineer's report might cost $1,500. In total, your building consent fees could exceed $7,000.

Consent Timeline

The building consent process can take several weeks to several months, depending on the complexity of the project and the council's processing times. Here's a general outline of what you can expect:

  • Pre-Application Meeting: 1-2 weeks
  • Application Submission: 1-2 weeks
  • Council Processing: 2-6 weeks
  • Inspections: 2-4 weeks
  • Consent Issuance: 1-2 weeks

Delays can occur due to incomplete applications, insufficient information, or council workload. It's essential to plan ahead and allow sufficient time for the consent process.

What Doesn't Need Consent

Not all building work requires a consent. Exempt building work under Schedule 1 of the Building Act 2004 includes:

  • Decks under 1.5m high
  • Small sheds (less than 10m²)
  • Internal fit-outs (e.g., kitchen renovations)
  • Painting and decorating
  • Fencing (less than 2.5m high)

However, it's always best to check with your local council to confirm whether your project requires a consent.

Cost of Getting it Wrong

If you start building without a consent or fail to comply with the Building Act, you may face significant costs and consequences, including:

  • Retrospective Consent Process: Up to 5 times the original consent cost
  • Legal Issues: Problems when selling the property or resolving disputes
  • Insurance Problems: Difficulty obtaining insurance or making claims
  • WorkSafe Implications: Fines and penalties for non-compliance with health and safety regulations

It's crucial to get it right from the start and obtain the necessary consents before commencing building work.

PIM (Project Information Memorandum)

A Project Information Memorandum (PIM) is a document that provides information about the proposed building project, including any potential issues or requirements. The cost of a PIM can range from $300 to $800, depending on the complexity of the project.

A PIM can help you:

  • Identify potential issues early on
  • Understand council requirements
  • Plan your project more effectively

It's recommended to obtain a PIM before submitting your building consent application.

Tips to Reduce Consent Costs

To minimize your building consent costs, consider the following tips:

  • Accurate Project Value Estimates: Ensure your project value estimate is accurate to avoid overpaying the MBIE levy and BRANZ levy.
  • Pre-Application Meetings: Meet with your local council to discuss your project and identify potential issues before submitting your application.
  • Complete Applications: Ensure your application is complete and includes all required information to avoid delays and additional costs.
  • Experienced Draughtspeople: Use experienced draughtspeople to prepare your plans and specifications, reducing the risk of errors and omissions.

By following these tips, you can reduce your building consent costs and ensure a smoother project process.

FAQ

Here are some frequently asked questions about building consent costs in NZ:

  1. How long does building consent take in NZ? Building consent can take anywhere from 2-6 weeks, depending on the complexity of the project and the council's processing times.
  2. How much does a consent cost for a house extension? The cost of a consent for a house extension can range from $1,000 to $5,000, depending on the size and complexity of the project.
  3. Can I act as my own project manager? Yes, you can act as your own project manager, but it's recommended to hire a professional project manager or builder to ensure compliance with the Building Act and to manage the consent process.

  5. Can I get a refund if my consent is declined? Refunds are typically not available if your consent is declined. However, you may be able to appeal the decision or resubmit your application with revised plans.

FAQ

  1. How long does a building consent take in NZ? A building consent in New Zealand typically takes around 20-30 working days to process, but this can vary depending on the complexity of the project and the council's workload. It's essential to factor in this timeframe when planning your project to avoid any delays. Some councils may offer a fast-track service for an additional fee, which can reduce the processing time to around 10-15 working days. However, this service is not available for all types of projects, and the council will assess the eligibility of your project on a case-by-case basis. For example, a simple renovation project may be eligible for fast-tracking, while a complex new build may not.

  2. How much does a consent cost for a house extension? The cost of a building consent for a house extension in New Zealand can range from $2,000 to $10,000 or more, depending on the size and complexity of the extension. The cost will include the processing fee, MBIE levy, and inspections. For example, a small single-storey extension may cost around $2,500, while a larger two-storey extension may cost around $8,000. It's crucial to get a detailed estimate from your local council or a professional to get a more accurate quote. Additionally, you should also consider the cost of any additional requirements, such as resource consent or engineering reports, which can add to the overall cost.

  3. Can I do building work without consent and get it approved retrospectively? While it's possible to apply for a retrospective building consent, it's not recommended to do building work without consent in the first place. If you've done work without consent, you'll need to apply for a certificate of acceptance, which can be a more complex and costly process. The council may also impose penalties or require additional work to be done to bring the building up to code. It's always best to obtain a building consent before starting any work to avoid any potential issues. In some cases, the council may not approve a retrospective consent, and you may be required to demolish or alter the work that has been done.

  4. What is a Project Information Memorandum (PIM) and when do I need one? A Project Information Memorandum (PIM) is a document that provides information about the proposed building work, including any potential risks or issues. You'll need a PIM if your project involves work that requires a building consent, such as a new build, extension, or significant renovation. The PIM will help you identify any potential issues and ensure that you're aware of the requirements for your project. You can apply for a PIM through your local council, and it's usually issued within 10-20 working days. The PIM will provide you with valuable information about your project, including any necessary consents, permits, or approvals.

  5. Do decks need building consent in NZ? In New Zealand, decks that are more than 1.5 metres above the ground or have a total floor area of more than 20 square metres require a building consent. If your deck is smaller or lower to the ground, you may not need a consent, but it's always best to check with your local council to confirm. Even if a consent is not required, you'll still need to ensure that your deck is built to the requirements of the building code and is safe for use. You should also consider factors such as drainage, structural integrity, and accessibility when designing and building your deck.

  6. What happens if I do building work without consent? If you do building work without consent, you risk facing penalties, fines, or even having to demolish the work. The council may also require you to apply for a certificate of acceptance, which can be a more complex and costly process. Additionally, if the work is not up to code, you may be putting the safety of occupants at risk. It's always best to obtain a building consent before starting any work to avoid any potential issues. In some cases, the council may also impose additional requirements, such as engineering reports or inspections, to ensure that the work is safe and compliant with the building code.
