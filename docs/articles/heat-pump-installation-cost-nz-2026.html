Heat Pump Installation Cost NZ 2026 — Supply, Install & Running Costs

Published 14 June 2026 · NZ Tradie Tools
heat pumpHVACinstallationcostsNZ2026

A heat pump is the most efficient way to warm a Kiwi home, and for most households it's the single biggest comfort upgrade you can make heading into winter. But "how much does a heat pump cost to install" doesn't have one answer — it depends on whether you're heating one room or the whole house, the size of the unit, and whether your electrical setup is ready for it. This guide covers realistic 2026 prices for every type of install, what's included (and what isn't), running costs, and the rebates worth knowing about.

Heat Pump Installation Cost by Type

These are supply-and-install prices for 2026 — the unit plus a standard installation.

Single-room wall unit (small, 2.5–3.5kW): $1,800–$3,500. Ideal for a bedroom, study or small living space. This is the most common install and the entry point for most homes.

Mid-range single room (5–7kW): $2,500–$5,000. Sized for a larger living area or open-plan space. The bigger the room and the higher the heat output, the more you'll pay.

Multi-split (3 indoor units + 1 outdoor): $7,000–$14,000. One outdoor compressor feeding several indoor heads — good for heating multiple rooms without an outdoor unit on every wall.

Ducted whole-house: $12,000–$25,000+. Discreet ducted vents in each room running off a central system, controlled as zones. The premium option for whole-home heating and cooling.

What's Included — and What's Not

A standard install quote usually covers:

  • The heat pump unit (indoor and outdoor)
  • The refrigerant line set connecting them
  • The electrical connection
  • The wall bracket for the outdoor unit
  • The condensate drain pipe

What's typically not included is a switchboard upgrade. Older homes sometimes don't have the spare capacity or a modern board to safely run a heat pump, and bringing it up to scratch can add $800–$2,500. A good installer will flag this before quoting, but it's worth asking up front so it doesn't arrive as a surprise.

Brands Common in NZ

The heat pump market in New Zealand is dominated by a handful of reliable brands: Mitsubishi Electric, Daikin, Fujitsu, Panasonic, Hitachi and Haier. Mitsubishi Electric and Daikin tend to sit at the premium end for performance and quiet running; Haier and some Panasonic models offer good value at the budget end. Your installer will usually recommend a brand and model sized to the room — and the right size matters more than the badge.

Running Costs

Heat pumps are cheap to run because they move heat rather than generate it. A 2.5kW unit costs roughly $0.30–$0.50 per hour at around 14c/kWh electricity. The key to keeping costs low is sizing: a heat pump is most efficient running steadily at 60–70% of its capacity, not blasting flat-out or short-cycling on and off. An oversized unit in a small room actually costs more to run than a correctly sized one, which is why getting the sizing right at install time pays off for years.

Rebates and Funding

If cost is a barrier, check whether you qualify for help. The Warmer Kiwi Homes programme offers eligible lower-income homeowners a rebate of up to around $3,000 toward an efficient heat pump. Some councils also run their own warm-home or rebate schemes. Eligibility depends on your income, your property and current programme criteria, so confirm what's on offer before you commit — it can make a meaningful dent in the price.

Who Can Install a Heat Pump?

This isn't a DIY job. Heat pump installation must be done by a certified HVAC technician or registered refrigeration engineer, because it involves handling refrigerant under regulation. The electrical connection must be carried out by a licensed electrician. Using qualified trades isn't just about compliance — it protects your manufacturer's warranty, which is usually void if the unit wasn't installed by an accredited installer.

Placement and Maintenance

Good placement makes a real difference to performance. Keep the outdoor unit out of direct, all-day sun where possible, and position the indoor unit centrally so warm air circulates through the room rather than getting trapped in a corner.

Maintenance is simple but worth doing: clean the filters monthly during heavy use, and book a professional service every two years (around $150–$250). Clean filters and a serviced system run more efficiently and last longer — neglected units lose heating performance and cost more to run.
