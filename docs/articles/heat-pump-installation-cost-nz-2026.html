Heat Pump Installation Cost NZ 2026

Published 14 June 2026 · NZ Tradie Tools

Introduction

Heat pumps have become the most popular heating choice for New Zealand homes, and for good reason. They offer an efficient and cost-effective way to warm your home, especially when compared to traditional resistive heating methods. With the ability to provide both heating and cooling, heat pumps are a versatile solution for Kiwi homeowners. In this article, we will cover everything you need to know about heat pump installation costs in New Zealand, including how pricing works, the cost breakdown by home size, top brands, regional cost variations, running costs, grants, and subsidies. By the end of this article, you will be well-equipped to make an informed decision about installing a heat pump in your home.

As the New Zealand government continues to promote energy-efficient solutions, heat pumps have become an attractive option for homeowners looking to reduce their energy consumption and lower their power bills. The Energy Efficiency and Conservation Authority (EECA) has implemented various initiatives to encourage the adoption of heat pumps, including the Warmer Kiwi Homes programme, which provides a $2,000 subsidy for eligible households. With the help of organisations like the Master Plumbers, Registered Master Builders, and the Electrical Workers Registration Board (EWRB), Kiwi homeowners can ensure that their heat pump installation is done safely and efficiently.

How Heat Pump Pricing Works in NZ

When it comes to heat pump pricing in New Zealand, there are several factors to consider. The cost of a heat pump can be split into two main components: supply and labour. The supply cost refers to the cost of the heat pump unit itself, while the labour cost covers the installation and any additional work required, such as electrical connections and ducting. The supply cost can vary depending on the brand and type of heat pump, with high-end brands like Mitsubishi Electric and Daikin typically costing more than generic or budget-friendly options.

In New Zealand, heat pumps are typically installed by HVAC (heating, ventilation, and air conditioning) companies or electricians who are registered with the EWRB. These professionals have the necessary expertise and qualifications to ensure that your heat pump is installed correctly and safely. When getting quotes from installers, it's essential to ask about their experience and qualifications, as well as their pricing structure. Some installers may charge a fixed price for the installation, while others may charge an hourly rate. Be sure to also ask about any additional costs, such as travel fees or accommodation costs for rural installations.

Cost Breakdown by Home Size

The cost of a heat pump installation can vary significantly depending on the size of your home. Here is a breakdown of the typical costs for different home sizes:

  • Single room (2-4kW): $2,500-$4,500 installed
  • Medium home (5-8kW): $3,500-$6,500 installed
  • Large home multi-split (10-16kW): $8,000-$18,000 installed
  • Ducted systems: $12,000-$35,000

When it comes to heat pumps, there are several top brands to consider. Here are some of the most popular brands in New Zealand, along with their typical supply costs:

  • Mitsubishi Electric: $1,500-$3,500
  • Daikin: $1,800-$4,000

  • Fujitsu: $1,500-$3,200

Regional Cost Variation

The cost of a heat pump installation can vary depending on your location in New Zealand. Labour rates, travel costs, and accommodation costs for rural installations can all impact the final price. Here is a comparison of the typical costs for Auckland, Wellington, and Christchurch:

  • Auckland: $2,500-$5,000 (single room), $4,000-$8,000 (medium home), $8,000-$15,000 (large home)
  • Wellington: $2,000-$4,000 (single room), $3,500-$6,500 (medium home), $7,000-$12,000 (large home)
  • Christchurch: $1,800-$3,500 (single room), $3,000-$5,500 (medium home), $6,000-$10,000 (large home)

These estimates are approximate and can vary depending on the specific location, installer, and other factors.

Running Costs and Payback Period

The running cost of a heat pump can vary depending on several factors, including the size of your home, the efficiency of the heat pump, and your electricity rates. On average, Kiwi homeowners can expect to pay around 35c/kWh for electricity. Based on this rate, here are some estimated annual running costs for different home sizes:

  • Single room: $200-$400
  • Medium home: $400-$800
  • Large home: $800-$1,600
  • Ducted system: $1,000-$2,500

The payback period for a heat pump installation can vary depending on the cost of the installation, the running costs, and the savings compared to traditional resistive heating methods. On average, Kiwi homeowners can expect a payback period of 5-20 years, depending on the size of their home and the efficiency of the heat pump.

Grants and Subsidies

The New Zealand government offers various grants and subsidies to encourage the adoption of energy-efficient solutions, including heat pumps. The EECA Warmer Kiwi Homes programme provides a $2,000 subsidy for eligible households to install a heat pump. To qualify for this subsidy, you must meet certain criteria, including:

  • Being a homeowner or landlord
  • Having a household income of $100,000 or less
  • Living in a home that is not well-insulated or does not have a fixed form of heating
  • Installing a heat pump that meets the EECA's minimum energy efficiency standards

To apply for the subsidy, you will need to provide documentation, including proof of income and ownership, as well as a quote from a registered installer. You can find more information about the Warmer Kiwi Homes programme on the EECA website.

Choosing an Installer

When choosing an installer for your heat pump, it's essential to get multiple quotes and compare prices. Here are some tips to consider:

  • Get at least three quotes from different installers
  • Check the installer's registration with the EWRB
  • Ask about their experience and qualifications
  • Check their pricing structure and any additional costs
  • Ask about their warranty and after-sales service

By doing your research and choosing a reputable installer, you can ensure that your heat pump is installed correctly and safely, and that you receive a high-quality product that meets your needs.

FAQ

When it comes to heat pump installation in New Zealand, there are several questions that homeowners and tradies often ask. Here are six detailed FAQ questions and answers that can help you make an informed decision:

  1. What is the cheapest heat pump I can get installed in NZ? The cheapest heat pump you can get installed in NZ can vary depending on the brand, model, and size of the unit. However, on average, a basic single-room heat pump can cost between $1,200 to $2,500, including installation. For example, a 3.5 kW single-room heat pump from a reputable brand like Mitsubishi or Panasonic can cost around $1,800 to $2,200, including installation. It's essential to note that while cheaper options may be available, they may not be as energy-efficient or reliable as more expensive models. It's recommended to spend at least $2,000 to $3,000 for a decent single-room heat pump installation.

  2. Can I install a heat pump myself in NZ? While it's technically possible to install a heat pump yourself, it's not recommended. Heat pump installation requires specialized knowledge and skills, and improper installation can lead to reduced efficiency, increased energy bills, and even safety hazards. In NZ, it's also required by law to have a certified electrician or heat pump installer to connect the unit to the electrical supply. Additionally, most manufacturers' warranties require professional installation to be valid. Hiring a professional installer can cost between $800 to $1,500, depending on the complexity of the job, but it's worth the investment for peace of mind and optimal performance.

  3. Does a heat pump installation require building consent? In most cases, a heat pump installation does not require building consent in NZ. However, if the installation involves significant modifications to the building's structure, such as installing a new outdoor unit or modifying the electrical supply, a building consent may be required. It's essential to check with your local council to determine if a building consent is needed for your specific installation. The cost of obtaining a building consent can range from $500 to $2,000, depending on the complexity of the project.

  4. How long does a heat pump installation take? The time it takes to install a heat pump can vary depending on the complexity of the job and the size of the unit. On average, a single-room heat pump installation can take around 2-4 hours, while a multi-room installation can take 1-2 days. It's essential to factor in additional time for site visits, heat loss calculations, and paperwork. A good installer will provide a detailed timeline and schedule to ensure minimal disruption to your daily routine. The cost of installation can range from $800 to $1,500, depending on the complexity of the job and the installer's hourly rate.

  5. What warranty should I expect on a heat pump in NZ? When purchasing a heat pump in NZ, you should expect a minimum warranty of 5 years on parts and labor. However, some manufacturers offer more comprehensive warranties, such as 6-10 years on parts and labor, and even 10-20 years on compressors. It's essential to check the warranty terms and conditions before making a purchase, as some warranties may have specific requirements, such as regular maintenance or registration. A good warranty can provide peace of mind and protect your investment, with the average cost of a heat pump replacement ranging from $1,500 to $3,000.

  6. Which heat pump brand is most reliable in NZ? While there are several reliable heat pump brands available in NZ, some of the most popular and reputable brands include Mitsubishi, Panasonic, and Daikin. These brands offer high-quality products, comprehensive warranties, and excellent after-sales support. According to a survey by the NZ Ministry of Business, Innovation and Employment, Mitsubishi and Panasonic are among the top-rated heat pump brands in NZ, with over 80% of respondents reporting high satisfaction with their performance and reliability. The cost of a heat pump from these brands can range from $1,500 to $3,500, depending on the model and size of the unit.

Choosing the Right Installer

Choosing the right installer is crucial to ensure a successful heat pump installation. Here are some tips to help you find a reliable and competent installer: To get started, it's recommended to get at least three quotes from different installers. This will give you a basis for comparison and help you find the best value for your money. A good quote should include a site visit to assess your home's specific needs, a heat loss calculation to determine the required capacity of the heat pump, and a written quote that outlines the scope of work, materials, and costs. The cost of a site visit can range from $100 to $300, depending on the location and complexity of the job.

When evaluating quotes, make sure to check if the installer is ECANZ (Electrical Contractors Association of New Zealand) registered. This ensures that the installer has the necessary qualifications, experience, and insurance to perform the job safely and efficiently. You can check the ECANZ website to verify an installer's registration status. Additionally, read reviews on Google, Builderscrack, or other online platforms to get an idea of the installer's reputation and customer satisfaction. Look for reviews that mention the installer's professionalism, communication, and workmanship.

Some red flags to avoid when choosing an installer include: * Unrealistically low prices: If a quote seems too good to be true, it may be a sign of poor quality workmanship or materials. * Lack of transparency: Be wary of installers who are unclear about their processes, materials, or costs. * No written quote: A written quote is essential to ensure that you and the installer are on the same page. * No ECANZ registration: This is a critical requirement for any electrical work in NZ. * Poor communication: If an installer is unresponsive or unprofessional, it may be a sign of poor customer service.

By doing your research, getting multiple quotes, and checking an installer's credentials, you can find a reliable and competent installer to ensure a successful heat pump installation.
