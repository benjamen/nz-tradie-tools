## Introduction
As a homeowner in New Zealand, having a reliable hot water cylinder (HWC) is essential for daily life. However, like all appliances, HWCs have a limited lifespan and will eventually need replacing. If you're experiencing cold showers, noticing rust or leaks, or if your HWC is nearing the end of its expected lifespan (usually around 10-15 years), it's time to start planning ahead. Waiting for your HWC to fail can lead to inconvenience, damage to your home, and higher replacement costs. In this article, we'll guide you through the process of replacing your HWC, including the different types of cylinders available, sizing, costs, and what to expect from the replacement process.

## Types of Hot Water Cylinders
When it comes to choosing a new HWC, there are several options to consider. The most common type is the electric storage cylinder, which is available in sizes ranging from 180-300 litres and costs between $1,200-$2,500 (supply only). However, there are other options that may be more efficient or cost-effective in the long run.

* **Electric Storage Cylinder**: This is the most common type of HWC in New Zealand. It's a simple and affordable option, but it can be less efficient than other types.
* **Heat Pump Hot Water**: This type of HWC uses a heat pump to heat the water, which can be 2-3 times more efficient than electric storage cylinders. Brands like Sanden, Rheem, and Bosch offer heat pump hot water systems, which cost between $2,500-$5,000 (supply only) and $500-$1,000 (installation).
* **Gas Continuous Flow**: This type of HWC uses gas to heat the water as it flows through the system, eliminating the need for a storage tank. Brands like Rinnai and Rheem offer gas continuous flow systems, which cost between $1,000-$2,500 (supply only).
* **Solar Thermal**: This type of HWC uses solar panels to heat the water, which can be a cost-effective option in the long run. However, it requires a significant upfront investment, with costs ranging from $5,000-$10,000 (installed).

The following table compares the different types of HWCs:

| Solar Thermal | $5,000-$10,000 | Low | Large homes with good solar exposure |

## Sizing Guide
When choosing a new HWC, it's essential to consider the size of the cylinder. A general rule of thumb is to allow 45-60 litres of water per person in the household. For example, a family of four would require a cylinder with a minimum capacity of 180-240 litres. You should also consider the peak draw-off, which is the amount of hot water required at any one time. For example, if you have a large family with multiple showers and baths, you may need a larger cylinder to meet the peak demand.

It's also important to consider the difference between off-peak and anytime tariff cylinders. Off-peak cylinders are designed to heat water during off-peak hours (usually at night) and store it for use during the day. Anytime tariff cylinders, on the other hand, can heat water at any time. If you have a large family or high hot water demands, an anytime tariff cylinder may be more suitable.

## Cost of Replacement
The cost of replacing a HWC can vary depending on the type and size of the cylinder, as well as the labour costs involved. The supply cost of a standard electric storage cylinder can range from $1,200-$2,500. Labour costs can add an additional $350-$650, depending on the complexity of the job and the plumber's rates. Other costs to consider include:

* Draincock: $50-$100
* Pressure limiting valve: $100-$200
* Thermostat expansion valve: $50-$100
* Disposal of old unit: $100-$200

The total typical cost of replacing a standard electric storage cylinder can range from $2,000-$4,000.

## Heat Pump Hot Water — Is it Worth it?
Heat pump hot water systems can be a cost-effective option in the long run, with potential electricity savings of $600-$1,200 per year. The EECA Warmer Kiwi Homes subsidy can also provide up to $2,500 towards the cost of a heat pump hot water system for eligible households. However, the upfront cost of a heat pump system can be higher than other types of HWCs.

To determine whether a heat pump hot water system is worth it, you'll need to consider the payback period. This is the amount of time it takes for the system to pay for itself through energy savings. For example, if a heat pump system costs $3,000 more than a standard electric storage cylinder, but saves $800 per year in energy costs, the payback period would be approximately 3.75 years.

## Off-Peak Tariff
Off-peak tariff pricing works by charging a lower rate for electricity used during off-peak hours (usually at night). This can be a cost-effective option for households with high hot water demands, as it allows you to heat water during off-peak hours and store it for use during the day. Several electricity retailers in New Zealand offer off-peak tariffs, including Contact Energy and Mercury Energy.

When considering an off-peak tariff, it's essential to consider the sizing of your HWC. Off-peak cylinders are designed to heat water during off-peak hours, so you'll need to ensure that your cylinder is sized correctly to meet your hot water demands during the day.
