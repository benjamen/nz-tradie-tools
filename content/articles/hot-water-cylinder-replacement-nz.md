## Introduction
As a homeowner in New Zealand, having a reliable hot water cylinder (HWC) is essential for the comfort and convenience of your family. However, like all appliances, HWCs have a limited lifespan and will eventually need to be replaced. Signs that your HWC needs replacing include its age (most last between 10-15 years), rust, leaks, or if you're experiencing cold showers. Rather than waiting for your HWC to fail, it's wise to plan ahead and consider replacing it before it becomes an emergency. This not only saves you from the inconvenience of sudden hot water loss but also allows you to take advantage of newer, more efficient models that can save you money in the long run. In this article, we'll guide you through the process of replacing your hot water cylinder in New Zealand, covering the types of cylinders available, sizing, costs, and what to expect from the replacement process.

## Types of Hot Water Cylinders
When it comes to choosing a new hot water cylinder, you have several options, each with its own set of advantages and disadvantages. The most common types include electric storage cylinders, heat pump hot water cylinders, gas continuous flow systems, and solar thermal systems.

- **Electric Storage Cylinder**: This is the most common type of HWC in New Zealand, typically ranging from 180 to 300 litres in capacity. The cost for an electric storage cylinder can range from $1,200 to $2,500, supply only. They are straightforward to install and maintain but can be less efficient than other options.
- **Heat Pump Hot Water**: Heat pump systems are known for their efficiency, offering 2-3 times the efficiency of traditional electric storage cylinders. Brands like Sanden, Rheem, and Bosch offer high-quality heat pump hot water systems. The supply cost can range from $2,500 to $5,000, with an additional $500 to $1,000 for installation. They are a more environmentally friendly option but require a larger upfront investment.
- **Gas Continuous Flow**: Gas continuous flow systems do not store hot water in a tank; instead, they heat water as it's needed. Rinnai and Rheem are popular brands offering these systems. The supply cost can range from $1,000 to $2,500. They are highly efficient and can be more cost-effective for larger households but require a gas supply.
- **Solar Thermal**: Solar thermal systems use solar panels to heat water, often with an electric boost for cloudy days. These systems are the most environmentally friendly option but also the most expensive, with costs ranging from $5,000 to $10,000 installed.

## Sizing Guide
Choosing the right size of hot water cylinder is crucial to ensure your household has enough hot water without wasting energy. A general rule of thumb is to allow 45-60 litres per person. For example, a family of four would require a cylinder with a capacity of at least 180-240 litres. Additionally, you need to consider peak draw-off (the maximum amount of hot water used at any one time) and whether you are on an off-peak or anytime tariff. Off-peak tariffs can affect the sizing of your cylinder, as they often require a larger cylinder to take advantage of cheaper nighttime electricity rates.

## Cost of Replacement
The cost of replacing a hot water cylinder can vary widely depending on the type of cylinder, installation costs, and other factors. Here's a breakdown of the typical costs involved:
- Supply cost: $1,200-$2,500 for a standard electric storage cylinder
- Labour: $350-$650 for a licensed plumber
- Additional components: Draincock, pressure limiting valve, thermostat expansion valve
- Disposal of the old unit: $100-$200
The total typical cost for replacing a standard electric hot water cylinder can range from $2,000 to $4,000.

## Heat Pump Hot Water — Is It Worth It?
Heat pump hot water systems are significantly more efficient than traditional electric storage cylinders, offering substantial electricity savings. The Energy Efficiency and Conservation Authority (EECA) offers the Warmer Kiwi Homes subsidy, which can provide up to $2,500 for eligible households to install a heat pump hot water system. With typical electricity savings ranging from $600 to $1,200 per year, the payback period for the additional upfront cost of a heat pump system can be relatively short, making it a worthwhile investment for many homeowners.

## Off-Peak Tariff
Off-peak tariffs, such as Economy 7 or controlled load, offer cheaper electricity rates during nighttime hours. To take full advantage of these tariffs, you'll need a hot water cylinder that can store enough hot water to last throughout the day. This often means a larger cylinder than what would be required for an anytime tariff. Several electricity retailers in New Zealand offer off-peak tariffs, and it's essential to check with your provider to see if this option is available and suitable for your household.

## Do You Need a Licensed Plumber?
Yes, in New Zealand, replacing a hot water cylinder is considered restricted plumbing work and requires a licensed plumber. The plumber must be registered with the Plumbers, Gasfitters, and Drainlayers Board (PGDB) and provide a Certificate of Compliance upon completion of the work.
