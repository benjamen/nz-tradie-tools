## Introduction
Home renovation is a significant investment for any homeowner in New Zealand. However, it's common for renovation budgets to blow out, leaving homeowners stressed and financially strained. According to the Ministry of Business, Innovation and Employment (MBIE), there are three main causes of budget blowouts: inadequate planning, unforeseen site costs, and scope creep. This guide will help you plan your home renovation budget realistically, covering the true cost of a renovation, indicative costs per room type, and factors that drive cost variation. By the end of this article, you'll be well-equipped to create a comprehensive budget and avoid costly surprises.

## The True Cost of a Renovation
The cost of a home renovation in New Zealand can be broken down into several components. Construction costs, including labour and materials, typically account for 70-80% of the total budget. Council and consent fees range from 2-8%, while designer and architect fees can add up to 5-12% of the construction cost. Engineer fees are usually around 1-3%, and a contingency fund of 15-20% is essential to cover unexpected expenses. Additionally, if you need to relocate during the renovation, temporary accommodation costs will need to be factored in. Finally, don't forget to include the 15% Goods and Services Tax (GST) on all costs.

| Component | Percentage of Total Budget |
| --- | --- |
| Construction Costs | 70-80% |
| Council/Consent Fees | 2-8% |
| Designer/Architect Fees | 5-12% |
| Engineer Fees | 1-3% |
| Contingency | 15-20% |
| Temporary Accommodation | variable |
| GST | 15% |

It's essential to work with a registered builder, such as those affiliated with the Registered Master Builders or the Building and Construction Industry Training Organisation (BCITO), to ensure compliance with New Zealand's building codes and regulations.

## What Drives Cost Variation in NZ
Several factors can drive cost variation in New Zealand, including:

* Region: Renovations in Auckland tend to be 15-25% more expensive than in other parts of the country.
* Site Access: Difficult site access can increase labour costs and require specialized equipment.
* Existing Condition: The age and condition of your home can impact the cost of renovation, particularly if asbestos or other hazardous materials are present.
* Finish Level: The quality of materials and finishes can significantly impact the overall cost of the renovation.
* Plumbing and Electrical: Moving or upgrading plumbing and electrical systems can add significant costs to your renovation.

It's essential to work with a builder or project manager who understands these factors and can provide you with a detailed, realistic quote for your renovation.

## How to Get an Accurate Budget
To get an accurate budget for your renovation, it's essential to start with a concept design. This will help you define the scope of work and identify potential costs. Next, obtain quotes from at least three registered builders, ensuring they are quoting on the same scope of work. Don't approach builders with vague ideas, as this will likely result in vague prices. Consider engaging a Quantity Surveyor (QS) for larger projects, as their expert advice can help you save money in the long run. A QS report can cost between $2,000-$8,000, but it's a worthwhile investment to ensure your budget is realistic.

## The Contingency — Non-Negotiable
A contingency fund is essential for any renovation project in New Zealand. With a minimum of 15% of the total budget, this fund will cover unexpected expenses, such as:

* Hidden damage or structural issues
* Asbestos or other hazardous materials
* Scope creep or changes to the original plan

A real-life example of the importance of a contingency fund is a Christchurch bathroom renovation that discovered black mould and rotten wall framing behind the tiles. The additional cost of $12,000 was not accounted for in the original budget, highlighting the importance of having a sufficient contingency fund in place.

## Finance Options
There are several finance options available for home renovations in New Zealand, including:

* Top-up mortgage: This is often the cheapest option, but you'll need to have sufficient equity in your home.
* Home renovation loan: This type of loan doesn't require equity, but interest rates may be higher.
* Credit cards: While credit cards can provide quick access to funds, they often come with high interest rates and should be used with caution.
* Construction loan: This type of loan is suitable for larger projects and typically requires a fixed-price contract.

It's essential to consult with a financial advisor to determine the best finance option for your renovation project.

## How to Control Costs
To control costs during your renovation, consider the following:

* Fixed-price contract vs cost-plus: A fixed-price contract can provide certainty, but may not account for unexpected expenses. A cost-plus contract can be more flexible, but may lead to cost blowouts if not managed carefully.
* Variation management: Establish a clear process for managing changes to the original scope of work.
* Design before you demo: Ensure your design is complete and approved before commencing demolition or construction work.
* Avoid in-build changes: Try to avoid making changes to the original plan during the construction phase, as this can lead to cost increases and delays.
