## Introduction
Exterior house painting is one of the most important maintenance jobs on a New Zealand home. The country's unique combination of high UV exposure, heavy rainfall, and strong winds can cause significant damage to a house's exterior surfaces if left unchecked. Regular painting not only enhances the appearance of a property but also protects it from the elements, preventing costly repairs down the line. However, knowing when to repaint and how to do it correctly can be a daunting task for many homeowners. In this guide, we will cover the essentials of exterior house painting in NZ, including how often to repaint, surface types and considerations, preparation, paint selection, and costs. Whether you're a seasoned DIY enthusiast or planning to hire a professional, this article will provide you with the knowledge and tools to get the job done right.

## How Often Does Exterior Paint Last in NZ?
The lifespan of exterior paint in New Zealand varies significantly depending on the region and climate. In coastal areas such as Northland and Auckland, the high salt content in the air and intense UV radiation can reduce the paint's lifespan to 7-10 years. In central NZ, the paint can last 10-15 years, while in Canterbury and Otago, the combination of UV exposure and frost can result in a longer lifespan of 12-18 years. Signs that it's time to repaint include peeling, chalking, cracking, bare timber showing, and mould that won't wash off. It's essential to address these issues promptly, as delaying the repaint can lead to more extensive and costly repairs.

## Surface Types and Considerations
Different surface types require unique considerations when it comes to exterior painting. 

* **Weatherboard (Timber)**: Weatherboard surfaces are the most prep-intensive, requiring sanding and priming of bare patches. A flexible paint, such as Resene Lumbersider or Dulux Weathershield, is recommended to accommodate the natural movement of the timber.
* **Fibrous Cement (Hardiflex)**: Fibrous cement surfaces, commonly found in older homes, can contain asbestos if installed before 1990. It's crucial to test the surface before sanding to avoid asbestos exposure. Resene Lumbersider or Dulux 10yr exterior paint is suitable for fibrous cement surfaces.
* **Plaster/Render (Solid Plaster, Roughcast, Monolithic)**: Plaster surfaces require breathable paint to prevent moisture buildup. Resene Roof & Spouting or specialist plaster paints are recommended. For monolithic plaster homes, it's essential to check for leaky home symptoms before painting.
* **Colosteel/Metal Surfaces**: Metal surfaces require a rust-inhibiting primer and a direct-to-metal top coat to ensure a durable and long-lasting finish.

## Prep — The Most Important Step
Preparation is the most critical step in exterior painting. A well-prepared surface ensures a smooth, even finish and helps prevent premature peeling and cracking. The prep process typically includes:

* **Water Blasting**: Hire a water blaster for $80-$150 per day or hire a contractor for $300-$800 to blast the entire house. This step helps remove dirt, grime, and loose paint.
* **Drying Time**: Allow 2-3 fine days for the surface to dry completely before painting.
* **Scrape and Sand**: Scrape off peeling paint and sand the surface to create a smooth finish.
* **Fill Cracks**: Fill cracks with exterior-grade filler, such as Resene Filler or Selleys No More Gaps exterior.
* **Spot Prime**: Spot prime bare timber and repairs to ensure a uniform finish.
* **Mask Up**: Mask up glass and trim areas where the colour is changing to prevent paint overspray.

## Paint Selection
When it comes to exterior paint, water-based options are now the preferred choice among NZ painters. Water-based paints offer faster drying times, better flexibility, and lower VOCs (volatile organic compounds) compared to oil-based paints. The sheen level is also important, with low-sheen finishes recommended for walls and semi-gloss finishes for trim. Resene, Dulux, and Wattyl are the three main NZ brands offering full exterior paint systems.

## Number of Coats
One coat of paint is rarely enough for exterior surfaces. Two coats are standard, while three coats may be necessary for previously bare or heavily sanded surfaces. Applying multiple coats ensures a durable and long-lasting finish.

## DIY vs Hire a Painter
A full exterior repaint of a 3-bedroom house is a significant undertaking, requiring 2-3 people and a week to complete. The job involves working at heights, which can be hazardous, and requires specialized equipment, such as scaffolding. While some homeowners may be able to tackle ground-level and garage areas, two-storey houses often require professional assistance. ## FAQ
Here are some frequently asked questions about exterior house painting in NZ:

1. **How often should you paint the exterior of a house in NZ?**: The frequency of exterior painting depends on the region and climate, but generally, it's recommended to repaint every 7-18 years.
2. **How do I get rid of mould before painting?**: Remove mould by washing the surface with a solution of water and bleach, then rinsing thoroughly. Allow the surface to dry completely before painting.
3. **Can I paint fibrous cement cladding myself?**: While it's possible to paint fibrous cement cladding yourself, it's essential to test the surface for asbestos before sanding. If you're not comfortable with the process, consider hiring a professional.
4. **What's the best exterior paint for NZ conditions?**: Water-based paints from reputable brands like Resene, Dulux, and Wattyl are well-suited for NZ's climate.
5. **Do painters supply the paint or do I?**: Some painters may supply the paint, while others may require you to purchase it. Clarify this with your painter before hiring them.
