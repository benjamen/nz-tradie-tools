## Introduction
Exterior house painting is one of the most important maintenance jobs on a New Zealand home. The country's unique combination of high UV exposure, heavy rainfall, and strong winds can cause significant wear and tear on a house's exterior surfaces. If left unchecked, this can lead to costly repairs and even compromise the structural integrity of the building. Knowing when to repaint and how to do it properly is crucial to protecting your investment. In this guide, we will cover the key aspects of exterior house painting in New Zealand, including how often to repaint, surface types and considerations, preparation, paint selection, and costs. Whether you're a homeowner looking to DIY or hire a professional, this guide will provide you with the information you need to get the job done right.

## How Often Does Exterior Paint Last in NZ?
The lifespan of exterior paint in New Zealand varies depending on the region and climate. In coastal areas such as Northland and Auckland, the high salt content in the air and intense UV radiation can reduce the paint's lifespan to 7-10 years. In central New Zealand, the paint can last 10-15 years, while in the southern regions of Canterbury and Otago, it can last 12-18 years due to the lower UV radiation and colder temperatures. However, these are general guidelines, and the actual lifespan of the paint will depend on various factors, including the quality of the paint, the surface it's applied to, and the level of maintenance.

Signs that it's time to repaint include:
* Peeling or chalking paint
* Cracking or flaking surfaces
* Bare timber showing through
* Mould or mildew that won't wash off
If you notice any of these signs, it's essential to address them promptly to prevent further damage.

## Surface Types and Considerations
Different surface types require different preparation and painting techniques. Here are some common surface types found in New Zealand homes:

* **Weatherboard (timber)**: This is the most prep-intensive surface type, requiring sanding and priming of bare patches. A flexible paint such as Resene Lumbersider or Dulux Weathershield is recommended to accommodate the natural movement of the timber.
* **Fibrous cement (Hardiflex)**: If your home has fibrous cement cladding, it's essential to check if it contains asbestos, especially if it was installed before 1990. If it does, you'll need to take special precautions when sanding or drilling to avoid releasing asbestos fibers into the air. Resene Lumbersider or Dulux 10yr exterior paint is suitable for fibrous cement surfaces.
* **Plaster/render (solid plaster, roughcast, monolithic)**: Plaster surfaces require a breathable paint to prevent moisture buildup and damage. Resene Roof & Spouting or specialist plaster paints are recommended. If you have a monolithic plaster home, it's crucial to check for leaky home symptoms before painting, as these can be indicative of more significant issues.
* **Colosteel/metal surfaces**: Metal surfaces require a rust-inhibiting primer and a direct-to-metal top coat to prevent corrosion.

## Prep — The Most Important Step
Preparation is the most critical step in exterior house painting. It's what sets the stage for a successful and long-lasting paint job. Here are the key steps to follow:

* **Water blasting**: Hire a water blaster for $80-$150 per day or hire a contractor for $300-$800 to blast your entire house. This will remove dirt, grime, and loose paint, ensuring a smooth surface for painting.
* **Drying time**: Allow 2-3 fine days for the surface to dry completely before painting. This will prevent moisture from becoming trapped under the paint and causing it to peel or blister.
* **Scrape and sand**: Scrape off peeling paint and sand the surface to create a smooth finish.
* **Fill cracks**: Fill any cracks or holes with an exterior-grade filler such as Resene Filler or Selleys No More Gaps exterior.
* **Spot prime**: Spot prime any bare timber or repairs to ensure a strong bond between the paint and the surface.
* **Mask up**: Mask up any glass, trim, or areas where you're changing the colour to prevent paint from getting on these surfaces.

## Paint Selection
When it comes to exterior paint, there are two main types: water-based and oil-based. In New Zealand, water-based exterior paint is the preferred choice due to its faster drying time, better flexibility, and lower VOC (volatile organic compound) content. Most NZ painters now use water-based exterior paint, and it's available from all major brands, including Resene, Dulux, and Wattyl.

The sheen level of the paint is also important, with low-sheen paint recommended for walls and semi-gloss paint for trim.

## Number of Coats
One coat of paint is rarely enough on exterior surfaces. Two coats are standard, and three coats may be necessary on previously bare or heavily sanded surfaces. This will ensure a strong, even finish that will last for years to come.

## DIY vs Hire a Painter
A full exterior repaint of a 3-bedroom house is a significant job that requires a lot of time, effort, and expertise. It's a 2-3 person week job that involves working at heights and accessing difficult areas, such as scaffolding or ladders. While some homeowners may be able to tackle the ground-level and garage areas, two-storey homes require scaffolding, which can be hired for $400-$1,200 per week.

However, the risk of falls and other accidents is high, and it's often better to hire a professional painter who has the right equipment and expertise to do the job safely and properly. Professional painters will also do the necessary prep work, including water blasting, sanding, and filling cracks, to ensure a smooth, even finish.

The cost of exterior painting in New Zealand varies depending on the surface type, size of the house, and location. Here are some estimated costs:

## FAQ
Here are some frequently asked questions about exterior house painting in New Zealand:

1. **How often should you paint the exterior of a house in NZ?**: The frequency of exterior painting depends on the region, climate, and surface type. As a general rule, paint every 7-18 years, depending on the factors mentioned earlier.
2. **How do I get rid of mould before painting?**: To get rid of mould, use a solution of bleach and water to kill the mould, then rinse the area thoroughly and allow it to dry before painting.
3. **Can I paint fibrous cement cladding myself?**: While it's possible to paint fibrous cement cladding yourself, it's essential to take precautions when sanding or drilling to avoid releasing asbestos fibers into the air. If in doubt, hire a professional painter who has experience with fibrous cement surfaces.
4. **What's the best exterior paint for NZ conditions?**: The best exterior paint for NZ conditions is a water-based paint with a high level of UV resistance and flexibility. Resene, Dulux, and Wattyl are all reputable brands that offer high-quality exterior paints.
5. **Do painters supply the paint or do I?**: It's common for painters to supply the paint, but it's essential to clarify this with your painter before hiring them. If you're supplying the paint, make sure to choose a high-quality paint that's suitable for your surface type and climate.
