## Introduction
Exterior house painting is one of the most important maintenance jobs on a New Zealand home. The country's unique climate, with high levels of UV exposure, rain, and wind, can cause significant wear and tear on a house's exterior surfaces. If left unchecked, this can lead to costly repairs and even compromise the structural integrity of the building. Knowing when to repaint and how to do it properly is crucial to protecting your investment. In this guide, we'll cover the key aspects of exterior house painting in NZ, including how often to repaint, surface types and considerations, preparation, paint selection, and costs. Whether you're a homeowner looking to DIY or planning to hire a professional, this guide will provide you with the information you need to get the job done right.

## How often does exterior paint last in NZ?
The lifespan of exterior paint in NZ varies depending on the region and climate. In coastal areas like Northland and Auckland, the high levels of salt and UV exposure can reduce the paint's lifespan to 7-10 years. In central NZ, the paint can last 10-15 years, while in Canterbury and Otago, the combination of UV and frost can extend the lifespan to 12-18 years. However, these are general guidelines, and the actual lifespan of the paint will depend on various factors, including the quality of the paint, the surface it's applied to, and the level of maintenance. Signs that it's time to repaint include peeling, chalking, cracking, bare timber showing, and mould that won't wash off. If you notice any of these signs, it's essential to address them promptly to prevent further damage.

## Surface types and considerations
Different surface types require different approaches to exterior painting. Here are some common surface types and considerations:

* **Weatherboard (timber)**: Timber weatherboards are the most prep-intensive surface type. They require sanding and priming of bare patches, and a flexible paint like Resene Lumbersider or Dulux Weathershield is recommended.
* **Fibrous cement (Hardiflex)**: Fibrous cement surfaces can contain asbestos if they were installed before 1990. It's essential to test for asbestos before sanding or drilling into these surfaces. If asbestos is present, it's recommended to hire a professional to handle the removal and disposal. For fibrous cement surfaces, Resene Lumbersider or Dulux 10yr exterior paint is suitable.
* **Plaster/render (solid plaster, roughcast, monolithic)**: Plaster surfaces require a breathable paint to prevent moisture buildup. Resene Roof & Spouting or specialist plaster paints are recommended. For monolithic plaster homes, it's crucial to check for leaky home symptoms before painting.
* **Colosteel/metal surfaces**: Metal surfaces require a rust-inhibiting primer and a direct-to-metal top coat.

## Prep — the most important step
Preparation is the most critical step in exterior house painting. Here are the key steps to follow:

* **Water blasting**: Hire a water blaster for $80-$150 per day or hire a contractor for $300-$800 to blast your entire house. This will help remove dirt, grime, and loose paint.
* **Drying time**: Allow 2-3 fine days for the surfaces to dry before painting.
* **Scrape and sand**: Scrape off peeling paint and sand the surfaces to create a smooth finish.
* **Fill cracks**: Fill cracks with exterior grade filler like Resene Filler or Selleys No More Gaps exterior.
* **Spot prime**: Spot prime bare timber and repairs to ensure a strong bond between the paint and the surface.
* **Mask up**: Mask up glass and trim where you're changing the colour to prevent paint from getting on these areas.

## Paint selection
When it comes to paint selection, there are two main types: water-based and oil-based. Most NZ painters now use water-based exterior paint due to its faster drying time, better flexibility, and lower VOC (volatile organic compound) levels. The sheen level is also important, with low-sheen recommended for walls and semi-gloss for trim. Resene, Dulux, and Wattyl are the three main NZ brands that offer full exterior systems.

## Number of coats
One coat of paint is rarely enough for exterior surfaces. Two coats are standard, but three coats may be necessary on previously bare or heavily sanded surfaces.

## DIY vs hire a painter
A full exterior repaint of a 3-bedroom house is a significant undertaking that requires 2-3 people for a week. It involves working at heights and accessing hard-to-reach areas, which can be risky. While some homeowners may be able to handle ground-level and garage painting, two-storey houses require scaffolding, which can be hired for $400-$1,200 per week. Professional painters have the right equipment and expertise to do the job safely and efficiently. They will also ensure that the prep work is done properly, which is critical to a successful paint job. For job management and invoicing, consider using tools like Xero, Fergus, or Tradify to streamline your workflow.

## Exterior painting costs NZ
The cost of exterior painting in NZ varies depending on the surface type, size of the house, and location. Here are some estimated costs:

* **Professional painter — weatherboard 3-bed house**: $6,000-$15,000 (includes prep, materials, and 2 coats of paint on walls and trim)
* **Professional painter — plaster house (less prep)**: $5,000-$12,000
* **Per m² rate**: $20-$45 per m² of wall area (surfaces vary significantly)
* **Scaffolding (if required)**: $800-$2,500 extra
* **Materials only (DIY)**: $1,500-$4,000 for a 3-bed house

Get at least three quotes from different painters and compare them to find the best value for your money.

## FAQ
Here are some frequently asked questions about exterior house painting in NZ:

1. **How often should you paint the exterior of a house in NZ?**: The frequency of painting depends on the region and climate, but as a general rule, it's recommended to repaint every 7-18 years.
2. **How do I get rid of mould before painting?**: To get rid of mould, use a solution of bleach and water to scrub the affected area. Rinse thoroughly and allow the area to dry before painting.
3. **Can I paint fibrous cement cladding myself?**: While it's possible to paint fibrous cement cladding yourself, it's recommended to hire a professional if the cladding contains asbestos. If you're unsure, it's always best to err on the side of caution and seek professional advice.
4. **What's the best exterior paint for NZ conditions?**: The best exterior paint for NZ conditions is a water-based paint with a high level of UV resistance and flexibility. Resene, Dulux, and Wattyl offer a range of exterior paints that are suitable for NZ conditions.
5. **Do painters supply the paint or do I?**: It's common for painters to supply the paint, but it's essential to clarify this with your painter before hiring them. Some painters may charge extra for materials, so make sure to factor this into your budget.
