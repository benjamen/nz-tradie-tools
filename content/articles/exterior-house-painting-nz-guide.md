## Introduction
Exterior house painting is one of the most important maintenance jobs on a New Zealand home. The country's unique climate, with high levels of UV exposure, rain, and wind, can cause significant wear and tear on a house's exterior surfaces. If left unchecked, this can lead to costly repairs and even compromise the structural integrity of the building. Knowing when to repaint and how to do it correctly is crucial to protecting your investment. In this guide, we will cover the essential steps to prepare your home's exterior for painting, the best materials to use, and what it costs to get the job done. Whether you're a homeowner looking to DIY or hire a professional, this article will provide you with the knowledge you need to make informed decisions.

## How often does exterior paint last in NZ?
The lifespan of exterior paint in New Zealand varies significantly depending on the region and climate. In coastal areas such as Northland and Auckland, the high levels of salt and UV exposure can reduce the paint's lifespan to 7-10 years. In central New Zealand, the paint can last 10-15 years, while in the southern regions of Canterbury and Otago, it can last 12-18 years due to the lower UV levels and colder temperatures. However, these are general guidelines, and the actual lifespan of the paint will depend on the quality of the paint, the surface it's applied to, and the level of maintenance.

Signs that it's time to repaint your home's exterior include:
* Peeling or chalking paint
* Cracking or crazing of the paint surface
* Bare timber showing through
* Mould or mildew that won't wash off

If you notice any of these signs, it's essential to address the issue promptly to prevent further damage.

## Surface types and considerations
Different exterior surfaces require unique considerations when it comes to painting. Here are some of the most common surface types and what you need to know:

* **Weatherboard (timber)**: This is the most prep-intensive surface, requiring sanding and priming of bare patches. It's essential to use a flexible paint, such as Resene Lumbersider or Dulux Weathershield, to accommodate the natural movement of the timber.
* **Fibrous cement (Hardiflex)**: If your home has fibrous cement cladding, it's crucial to test for asbestos before sanding, especially if the cladding was installed before 1990. Once you've confirmed it's asbestos-free, you can use a paint specifically designed for fibrous cement, such as Resene Lumbersider or Dulux 10yr exterior.
* **Plaster/render (solid plaster, roughcast, monolithic)**: For plaster surfaces, it's essential to use a breathable paint to prevent moisture from becoming trapped. Resene Roof & Spouting or specialist plaster paints are good options. If you have a monolithic plaster home, it's crucial to check for leaky home symptoms before painting.
* **Colosteel/metal surfaces**: For metal surfaces, you'll need to use a rust-inhibiting primer and a direct-to-metal top coat to ensure a durable finish.

## Prep — the most important step
Preparation is the key to a successful exterior paint job. Here are the essential steps to follow:

* **Water blasting**: Hire a water blaster for $80-$150 per day or hire a contractor for $300-$800 to blast your entire house. This will remove dirt, grime, and loose paint.
* **Drying time**: Allow 2-3 fine days for the surface to dry completely before painting.
* **Scrape and sand**: Scrape off any peeling paint and sand the surface to create a smooth finish.
* **Fill cracks**: Use an exterior-grade filler, such as Resene Filler or Selleys No More Gaps exterior, to fill any cracks or gaps.
* **Spot prime**: Apply a primer to any bare timber or repairs to ensure a strong bond between the paint and the surface.
* **Mask up**: Mask any glass, trim, or areas where you're changing the colour to prevent paint from getting on these surfaces.

## Paint selection
When it comes to exterior paint, there are two main types: water-based and oil-based. In New Zealand, most painters now use water-based exterior paint due to its faster drying time, better flexibility, and lower VOC (volatile organic compound) levels. When choosing a paint, consider the sheen level: low-sheen is suitable for walls, while semi-gloss is better for trim. The three main NZ brands with full exterior systems are Resene, Dulux, and Wattyl.

## Number of coats
One coat of paint is rarely enough for an exterior surface. Two coats are standard, but if you're painting over a previously bare or heavily sanded surface, you may need to apply three coats to achieve a durable finish.

## DIY vs hire a painter
A full exterior repaint of a 3-bedroom house is a significant undertaking, requiring 2-3 people for a week. It involves working at heights and accessing difficult areas, which can be hazardous. While it's possible to DIY, it's essential to assess your abilities honestly. Most homeowners can paint ground-level and garage areas, but two-storey houses require scaffolding, which can be hired for $400-$1,200 per week. Professional painters have the right equipment and expertise to do the job safely and efficiently. They will also ensure that the prep work is done correctly, which is crucial for a long-lasting finish. ## Exterior painting costs NZ
The cost of exterior painting in New Zealand varies depending on the surface type, size of the house, and location. Here are some estimated costs:

* **Professional painter — weatherboard 3-bed house**: $6,000-$15,000 (includes prep, materials, and 2 coats of paint on walls and trim)
* **Professional painter — plaster house (less prep)**: $5,000-$12,000
* **Per m² rate**: $20-$45 per square meter of wall area (surfaces vary significantly)
* **Scaffolding (if required)**: $800-$2,500 extra
* **Materials only (DIY)**: $1,500-$4,000 for a 3-bed house

## FAQ
Here are some frequently asked questions about exterior painting in New Zealand:

1. **How often should you paint the exterior of a house in NZ?**
The frequency of exterior painting depends on the region, climate, and surface type. As a general rule, paint every 7-18 years, depending on the factors mentioned earlier.
2. **How do I get rid of mould before painting?**
To remove mould, use a solution of water and bleach, and scrub the area thoroughly. Rinse with clean water and allow the surface to dry completely before painting.
3. **Can I paint fibrous cement cladding myself?**
While it's possible to paint fibrous cement cladding yourself, it's essential to test for asbestos first, especially if the cladding was installed before 1990. If you're not comfortable with the process, consider hiring a professional.
4. **What's the best exterior paint for NZ conditions?**
The best exterior paint for NZ conditions is a water-based paint with a high level of UV resistance and flexibility. Resene, Dulux, and Wattyl are reputable brands that offer high-quality exterior paints.
5. **Do painters supply the paint or do I?**
It depends on the agreement you have with the painter. Some painters may include the cost of materials in their quote, while others may require you to supply the paint. Be sure to clarify this before hiring a painter.
