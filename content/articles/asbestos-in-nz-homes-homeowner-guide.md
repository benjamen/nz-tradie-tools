## Introduction
Asbestos in New Zealand homes is a significant concern, with approximately one in three homes built before 1990 containing asbestos-containing materials (ACM). Asbestos is a group of naturally occurring minerals that were widely used in building materials due to their durability, fire resistance, and insulation properties. However, it is now well-known that asbestos poses serious health risks, including lung cancer, mesothelioma, and asbestosis. The good news is that asbestos in good condition, which is not disturbed, is generally safe. This guide aims to provide homeowners with essential information on asbestos in NZ homes, including where it is found, how to identify it, and what to do if you need to remove it.

As a homeowner, it is crucial to understand the risks associated with asbestos and take necessary precautions to ensure your safety and the safety of your family. The Ministry of Business, Innovation and Employment (MBIE) and WorkSafe NZ have implemented regulations to manage the risks associated with asbestos, and it is essential to comply with these regulations when dealing with asbestos-containing materials. In this article, we will cover the key aspects of asbestos in NZ homes, including the locations where it is commonly found, how to identify it, and the costs associated with removal.

## Where Asbestos is Found in NZ Homes
Asbestos-containing materials can be found in various locations in NZ homes, including:

* Exterior cladding: Fibrous cement sheet (Hardiflex, Monlex, Super Six), corrugated roof sheets
* Interior linings: Textured ceilings ("popcorn" ceilings), stipple texture paint
* Flooring: Vinyl floor tiles and the adhesive underneath (common in homes 1950s-1980s)
* Insulation: Pipe lagging, some ceiling batts in older homes
* Roof: Super Six corrugated asbestos cement roofing sheets
* Bathrooms: Some wall tiles and tile adhesive
* Window surrounds, gutters, soffits (older fibrous cement products)

It is essential to note that asbestos can be found in many other locations, and it is crucial to exercise caution when renovating or demolishing any part of your home. The Registered Master Builders and Master Plumbers associations recommend that homeowners take necessary precautions when dealing with potential asbestos-containing materials.

## How to Identify Asbestos
Visual inspection is not a reliable method for identifying asbestos, as it can be difficult to distinguish from other materials. The only definitive way to confirm the presence of asbestos is through laboratory testing. To arrange an asbestos test in NZ, you can take a small sample yourself, wearing gloves and a mask, and seal it in a zip-lock bag. The sample can then be sent to an accredited NZ laboratory, such as Hill Laboratories or ALS Limited, for testing. The cost of testing can range from $50 to $150 per sample, depending on the laboratory and the type of test required.

Alternatively, you can hire an asbestos inspector to conduct a thorough inspection of your home and provide a detailed report. The cost of an asbestos inspection can range from $200 to $600, depending on the size of your home and the complexity of the inspection. ## What You Can Legally Do Yourself
Under the Health and Safety at Work (Asbestos) Regulations 2016, homeowners can remove up to 10m² of non-friable (bonded) asbestos from their own home without a licensed removalist. This includes small areas of fibrous cement sheet if undamaged. However, it is essential to note that you cannot remove friable asbestos (loose or powdery — insulation, sprayed coatings) or any asbestos from the roof if the area is large. It is also crucial to follow safe removal practices, including wearing personal protective equipment (PPE) and using a wet method to minimize dust generation.

For example, if you are planning to remove a small section of fibrous cement sheet from your exterior cladding, you can do so yourself, but you must ensure that you follow the necessary safety precautions and dispose of the material correctly. ## When to Use a Licensed Removalist
Friable asbestos always requires the services of a licensed removalist. Additionally, if you need to remove more than 10m² of any asbestos-containing material, you must hire a licensed removalist. WorkSafe NZ has notification requirements for larger jobs, and it is essential to comply with these regulations to ensure your safety and the safety of others.

To find a Class A (friable) or Class B (non-friable) licensed removalist, you can search online or contact WorkSafe NZ for a list of approved removalists in your area. It is essential to ensure that the removalist you hire is licensed and experienced in handling asbestos-containing materials.

## Safe DIY Removal (if Legal)
If you are legally allowed to remove asbestos-containing materials yourself, it is essential to follow safe removal practices to minimize the risk of exposure. This includes:

* Using a wet method to minimize dust generation
* Wearing a full face P2 respirator (not just a dust mask)
* Wearing disposable coveralls
* Double-bagging in 0.2mm polythene bags labelled "ASBESTOS"
* Disposing of the material at a council-approved asbestos disposal facility

Most NZ councils have an asbestos disposal facility, and it is essential to check with your local council for specific requirements and regulations.

## Renovation Rule: Always Test Before Demo
The most important rule when renovating a pre-1990 home is to always test for asbestos before starting demolition. This can help prevent unnecessary exposure to asbestos and ensure that you comply with NZ regulations. The Building and Construction Industry Training Organisation (BCITO) and the Electrical Workers Registration Board (EWRB) recommend that homeowners take necessary precautions when dealing with potential asbestos-containing materials.

## FAQ
Here are some frequently asked questions about asbestos in NZ homes:

1. **Is fibrous cement cladding dangerous?**
Fibrous cement cladding is not typically considered dangerous if it is in good condition and not disturbed. However, if it is damaged or deteriorated, it can release asbestos fibres, which can be hazardous to health.
2. **Do I have to disclose asbestos when selling my NZ home?**
Yes, under NZ law, you are required to disclose the presence of asbestos-containing materials in your home when selling. This is typically done through a vendor statement or a pre-purchase inspection report.
3. **Can I paint over asbestos fibrous cement?**
It is not recommended to paint over asbestos fibrous cement, as this can damage the material and release asbestos fibres. If you need to paint your exterior cladding, it is best to use a specialized coating that is designed for use on asbestos-containing materials.
4. **How much does asbestos testing cost?**
The cost of asbestos testing can range from $50 to $150 per sample, depending on the laboratory and the type of test required.
5. **What is a friable vs non-friable asbestos?**
Friable asbestos refers to loose or powdery asbestos-containing materials, such as insulation or sprayed coatings. Non-friable asbestos refers to bonded asbestos-containing materials, such as fibrous cement sheet or corrugated roof sheets.
