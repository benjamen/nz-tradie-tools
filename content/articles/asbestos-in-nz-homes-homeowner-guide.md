## Introduction to Asbestos in NZ Homes
Asbestos is a hidden danger lurking in many New Zealand homes, particularly those built before 1990. It's estimated that about one in three homes from this era contain asbestos-containing materials (ACM). The presence of asbestos can be a significant concern for homeowners, as it poses serious health risks if not handled properly. However, it's essential to note that asbestos in good condition, which is not disturbed, is generally safe. This comprehensive guide aims to educate homeowners on the risks associated with asbestos, where it's commonly found, how to identify it, and the necessary steps to take for safe removal.

Asbestos was widely used in construction due to its fire-resistant and insulating properties. However, the health risks associated with asbestos exposure, including lung cancer and mesothelioma, have led to strict regulations and guidelines for its handling and removal. The Ministry of Business, Innovation and Employment (MBIE) and WorkSafe NZ are key organisations that oversee asbestos management in New Zealand.

## Where Asbestos is Found in NZ Homes
Asbestos can be found in various parts of a home, both internally and externally. Some common locations include:

* Exterior cladding: Fibrous cement sheets, such as Hardiflex, Monlex, and Super Six, were commonly used for exterior cladding. Corrugated roof sheets made from asbestos cement were also widely used.
* Interior linings: Textured ceilings, often referred to as "popcorn" ceilings, and stipple texture paint may contain asbestos.
* Flooring: Vinyl floor tiles and the adhesive used to secure them, particularly in homes built between the 1950s and 1980s, may contain asbestos.
* Insulation: Pipe lagging and some ceiling batts in older homes may contain asbestos.
* Roof: Super Six corrugated asbestos cement roofing sheets were a common roofing material.
* Bathrooms: Some wall tiles and tile adhesive may contain asbestos.
* Window surrounds, gutters, and soffits: Older fibrous cement products were often used for these applications.

It's crucial to be aware of these potential locations to ensure safe handling and removal of asbestos-containing materials.

## How to Identify Asbestos
Visual inspection alone is not a reliable method for identifying asbestos. The only definitive way to confirm the presence of asbestos is through laboratory testing. Homeowners can arrange for an asbestos test by taking a small sample themselves, using gloves, a mask, and sealing it in a zip-lock bag, and sending it to an accredited New Zealand laboratory. The cost of testing ranges from $50 to $150 per sample. Alternatively, hiring an asbestos inspector can provide a comprehensive report, with costs ranging from $200 to $600 for a home inspection.

Key laboratories in New Zealand that offer asbestos testing services include Hill Laboratories, IANZ, and AsureQuality. It's essential to choose a laboratory that is accredited by International Accreditation New Zealand (IANZ) to ensure accurate and reliable results.

## What You Can Legally Do Yourself
Under the Health and Safety at Work (Asbestos) Regulations 2016, homeowners are permitted to remove up to 10m² of non-friable (bonded) asbestos from their own home without engaging a licensed removalist. This includes small areas of fibrous cement sheet, provided it is undamaged. However, it's essential to note that the removal of friable asbestos (loose or powdery) and any asbestos from the roof, if the area is large, requires the services of a licensed removalist.

## When to Use a Licensed Removalist
Friable asbestos always requires the services of a licensed removalist. Additionally, if the area of asbestos-containing material exceeds 10m², a licensed removalist must be engaged. WorkSafe NZ has specific notification requirements for larger asbestos removal jobs, and it's essential to comply with these regulations to ensure a safe and legal removal process.

To find a licensed removalist, homeowners can search for Class A (friable) or Class B (non-friable) licensed removalists through the WorkSafe NZ website or the Asbestos Removal Contractors Association (ARCA) New Zealand.

## Costs of Asbestos Removal in NZ
The costs associated with asbestos removal in New Zealand vary widely, depending on the type and quantity of asbestos-containing material, as well as the location and accessibility of the area. Some estimated costs include:

* Small removal of fibrous cement (under 10m²): $500 to $2,000
* Fibrous cement cladding replacement (whole house): $15,000 to $50,000
* Roof sheet removal and replacement: $5,000 to $20,000
* Friable asbestos (pipe lagging, ceiling insulation): $3,000 to $20,000+

It's essential to obtain quotes from licensed removalists to get an accurate estimate of the costs involved.

## Safe DIY Removal (if legal)
If homeowners are permitted to remove asbestos themselves, it's crucial to follow safe removal practices to minimize the risk of exposure. This includes using the wet method, wearing a full face P2 respirator (not just a dust mask), disposable coveralls, and double-bagging the asbestos in 0.2mm polythene bags labelled "ASBESTOS". The asbestos must then be disposed of at a council-approved facility, as most New Zealand councils have designated asbestos disposal facilities.

## Renovation Rule: Always Test Before Demo
The most critical rule when renovating a pre-1990 home is to always test for asbestos before commencing demolition. This simple step can help prevent exposure to asbestos and ensure a safe renovation process. Never start demolition without first testing for asbestos, as this can release deadly fibres into the air.

## Frequently Asked Questions
### Q: Is fibrous cement cladding dangerous?
A: Fibrous cement cladding is generally safe if it's in good condition and not damaged. However, if it's damaged or deteriorated, it can release asbestos fibres, posing a health risk.

### Q: Do I have to disclose asbestos when selling my NZ home?
A: Yes, under the Real Estate Agents Act 2008, sellers are required to disclose any known asbestos-containing materials in the property.

### Q: Can I paint over asbestos fibrous cement?
A: It's not recommended to paint over asbestos fibrous cement, as this can damage the material and release asbestos fibres. Instead, consider sealing or encapsulating the material.

### Q: How much does asbestos testing cost?
A: The cost of asbestos testing ranges from $50 to $150 per sample, depending on the laboratory and the type of test required.

### Q: What is a friable vs non-friable asbestos?
A: Friable asbestos refers to loose or powdery asbestos-containing material, which can easily release fibres into the air. Non-friable asbestos, on the other hand, is bonded asbestos-containing material, which is less likely to release fibres.
