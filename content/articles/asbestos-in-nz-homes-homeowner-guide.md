## Introduction to Asbestos in NZ Homes
Asbestos is a highly hazardous substance that was widely used in New Zealand homes built before 1990. It's estimated that about one in three homes from this era contain asbestos-containing materials (ACM). The presence of asbestos can be a significant concern for homeowners, particularly when it comes to renovation or demolition projects. However, it's essential to note that asbestos in good condition, which is not disturbed, is generally safe. The key is to understand where asbestos is likely to be found, how to identify it, and what to do if you encounter it.

As a homeowner, it's crucial to be aware of the risks associated with asbestos and take necessary precautions to ensure your safety and the safety of others. This comprehensive guide will cover everything you need to know about asbestos in NZ homes, from where it's commonly found to how to identify and remove it safely. We'll also discuss the costs associated with asbestos removal and provide answers to frequently asked questions.

## Where Asbestos is Found in NZ Homes
Asbestos was a popular building material in the mid-20th century due to its durability, fire-resistance, and insulation properties. As a result, it can be found in various parts of NZ homes, including:

* Exterior cladding: Fibrous cement sheet (such as Hardiflex, Monlex, and Super Six) and corrugated roof sheets are common examples of asbestos-containing materials.
* Interior linings: Textured ceilings, also known as "popcorn" ceilings, and stipple texture paint may contain asbestos.
* Flooring: Vinyl floor tiles and the adhesive underneath, commonly used in homes built between the 1950s and 1980s, can contain asbestos.
* Insulation: Pipe lagging and some ceiling batts in older homes may contain asbestos.
* Roof: Super Six corrugated asbestos cement roofing sheets are a common type of asbestos-containing material.
* Bathrooms: Some wall tiles and tile adhesive may contain asbestos.
* Window surrounds, gutters, and soffits: Older fibrous cement products can contain asbestos.

It's essential to be aware of these potential asbestos-containing materials when planning renovation or demolition projects.

## How to Identify Asbestos
Visual inspection is not a reliable method for identifying asbestos, as it can be difficult to distinguish from other materials. The only definitive way to confirm the presence of asbestos is through laboratory testing. To arrange an asbestos test in NZ, you can take a small sample yourself, wearing gloves and a mask, and seal it in a zip-lock bag. The sample can then be sent to an accredited NZ laboratory, such as Hill Laboratories or ALS Limited, for analysis. The cost of asbestos testing can range from $50 to $150 per sample.

Alternatively, you can hire an asbestos inspector to conduct a thorough inspection of your home and provide a detailed report. The cost of an asbestos inspection can range from $200 to $600, depending on the size of your home and the complexity of the inspection.

## What You Can Legally Do Yourself
Under the Health and Safety at Work (Asbestos) Regulations 2016, homeowners are allowed to remove up to 10m² of non-friable (bonded) asbestos from their own home without a licensed removalist. This includes small areas of fibrous cement sheet, provided it is undamaged. However, it's essential to note that you cannot remove friable asbestos (loose or powdery) or any asbestos from the roof if the area is large.

## When to Use a Licensed Removalist
If you're dealing with friable asbestos or more than 10m² of any asbestos-containing material, it's essential to hire a licensed removalist. WorkSafe NZ requires notification for larger asbestos removal jobs, and a licensed removalist will ensure that the work is done safely and in compliance with regulations.

To find a Class A (friable) or Class B (non-friable) licensed removalist, you can search online or check with your local council for recommendations. Make sure to check the removalist's qualifications and experience before hiring them.

## Costs of Asbestos Removal in NZ
The cost of asbestos removal in NZ can vary widely, depending on the type and quantity of asbestos-containing material, as well as the complexity of the removal process. Here are some estimated costs:

* Small removal of fibrous cement (under 10m²): DIY if careful, or $500-$2,000
* Fibrous cement cladding replacement (whole house): $15,000-$50,000
* Roof sheet removal and replacement: $5,000-$20,000
* Friable asbestos (pipe lagging, ceiling insulation): $3,000-$20,000+

## Safe DIY Removal (if Legal)
If you're allowed to remove asbestos yourself, it's essential to follow safe procedures to minimize the risk of exposure. This includes using the wet method, wearing a full face P2 respirator (not just a dust mask), and disposable coveralls. You should also double-bag the asbestos in 0.2mm polythene bags labelled "ASBESTOS" and dispose of it at a council-approved asbestos disposal facility.

## Renovation Rule: Always Test Before Demo
The most important rule when renovating a pre-1990 home is to always test for asbestos before starting demolition. This will help you identify potential asbestos-containing materials and take necessary precautions to avoid exposure.

## Frequently Asked Questions
Here are some frequently asked questions about asbestos in NZ homes:

* Q: Is fibrous cement cladding dangerous?
A: Fibrous cement cladding is generally safe if it's in good condition and not disturbed. However, if it's damaged or deteriorated, it can release asbestos fibres, which can be hazardous to health.
* Q: Do I have to disclose asbestos when selling my NZ home?
A: Yes, you are required to disclose the presence of asbestos in your home to potential buyers.
* Q: Can I paint over asbestos fibrous cement?
A: It's not recommended to paint over asbestos fibrous cement, as this can damage the material and release asbestos fibres.
* Q: How much does asbestos testing cost?
A: The cost of asbestos testing can range from $50 to $150 per sample, depending on the laboratory and the type of test.
* Q: What is a friable vs non-friable asbestos?
A: Friable asbestos is loose or powdery and can be easily crumbled, while non-friable asbestos is bonded and less likely to release fibres.
