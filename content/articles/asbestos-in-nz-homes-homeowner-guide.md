## Introduction
Asbestos in New Zealand homes is a significant concern, with approximately one in three homes built before 1990 containing asbestos-containing materials (ACM). Asbestos was widely used in construction due to its durability, fire-resistance, and insulation properties. However, it is now well-known that asbestos poses serious health risks, including lung cancer, mesothelioma, and asbestosis. The good news is that asbestos in good condition, which is not disturbed, is generally safe. This comprehensive guide will cover everything homeowners need to know about asbestos in NZ homes, from identification and testing to removal and safety precautions.

As a homeowner, it is essential to understand the risks associated with asbestos and take necessary steps to ensure your safety and the safety of your family. The Ministry of Business, Innovation and Employment (MBIE) and WorkSafe NZ provide guidelines and regulations for handling asbestos, and it is crucial to follow these guidelines to avoid any potential health risks. In this article, we will delve into the world of asbestos, exploring where it is found, how to identify it, and what you can do to safely remove it from your home.

## Where Asbestos is Found in NZ Homes
Asbestos-containing materials can be found in various parts of NZ homes, including:

* Exterior cladding: Fibrous cement sheets, such as Hardiflex, Monlex, and Super Six, were commonly used for exterior cladding. Corrugated roof sheets made from asbestos cement were also widely used.
* Interior linings: Textured ceilings, also known as "popcorn" ceilings, and stipple texture paint may contain asbestos.
* Flooring: Vinyl floor tiles and the adhesive underneath, commonly used in homes built between the 1950s and 1980s, may contain asbestos.
* Insulation: Pipe lagging and some ceiling batts in older homes may contain asbestos.
* Roof: Super Six corrugated asbestos cement roofing sheets were widely used in NZ homes.
* Bathrooms: Some wall tiles and tile adhesive may contain asbestos.
* Window surrounds, gutters, and soffits: Older fibrous cement products may contain asbestos.

It is essential to note that asbestos can be found in various forms, including bonded (non-friable) and loose (friable) asbestos. Bonded asbestos is generally considered safer, as the asbestos fibres are bound together with a cement-like material. However, friable asbestos is highly hazardous, as the loose fibres can easily become airborne and be inhaled.

## How to Identify Asbestos
Visual inspection is not a reliable method for identifying asbestos, as it can be difficult to distinguish from other materials. The only definitive way to confirm the presence of asbestos is through laboratory testing. To arrange an asbestos test in NZ, you can take a small sample yourself, using gloves, a mask, and sealing it in a zip-lock bag, and send it to an accredited NZ laboratory. The cost of testing can range from $50 to $150 per sample, depending on the laboratory and the type of test.

Alternatively, you can hire an asbestos inspector to conduct a thorough inspection of your home. The cost of an asbestos inspection report can range from $200 to $600, depending on the size of your home and the complexity of the inspection. Some key laboratories in NZ that offer asbestos testing services include:

* Hill Laboratories
* IANZ (International Accreditation New Zealand)
* AsureQuality

It is crucial to note that only accredited laboratories can provide reliable and accurate test results.

## What You Can Legally Do Yourself
Under the Health and Safety at Work (Asbestos) Regulations 2016, homeowners can remove up to 10m² of non-friable (bonded) asbestos from their own home without a licensed removalist. This includes small areas of fibrous cement sheet, provided it is undamaged. However, there are certain restrictions on what you can do yourself:

* You cannot remove friable asbestos (loose or powdery) without a licensed removalist.
* You cannot remove any asbestos from the roof if the area is large.
* You must follow safe removal procedures, including using personal protective equipment (PPE) and containing the asbestos material.

It is essential to note that while you can remove small amounts of non-friable asbestos yourself, it is still a hazardous material, and you must take necessary precautions to avoid exposure.

## When to Use a Licensed Removalist
If you need to remove friable asbestos or more than 10m² of non-friable asbestos, you must hire a licensed removalist. WorkSafe NZ requires notification for larger asbestos removal jobs, and a licensed removalist will ensure that the work is done safely and in compliance with regulations.

To find a licensed removalist, you can:

* Check the WorkSafe NZ website for a list of licensed asbestos removalists.
* Contact the New Zealand Demolition and Asbestos Association (NZDAA) for a list of members who are licensed removalists.
* Search online for licensed removalists in your area.

It is essential to note that there are two types of licensed removalists: Class A (friable) and Class B (non-friable). Make sure to hire a removalist who is licensed for the type of asbestos you need to remove.

## Costs of Asbestos Removal in NZ
The cost of asbestos removal in NZ can vary widely, depending on the type and amount of asbestos, as well as the location and accessibility of the material. Here are some approximate costs:

* Small removal of fibrous cement (under 10m²): $500 to $2,000
* Fibrous cement cladding replacement (whole house): $15,000 to $50,000
* Roof sheet removal and replacement: $5,000 to $20,000
* Friable asbestos (pipe lagging, ceiling insulation): $3,000 to $20,000+

It is essential to note that these costs are approximate and can vary depending on the complexity of the job and the location.

## Safe DIY Removal (if Legal)
If you are legally allowed to remove asbestos yourself, it is crucial to follow safe removal procedures to avoid exposure. Here are some steps to follow:

* Use a wet method to minimize dust and fibre release.
* Wear a full face P2 respirator (not just a dust mask).
* Wear disposable coveralls and gloves.
* Double-bag the asbestos material in 0.2mm polythene bags labelled "ASBESTOS".
* Dispose of the asbestos material at a council-approved facility.

Most NZ councils have an asbestos disposal facility, and you can contact your local council to find out more about their disposal procedures.

## Renovation Rule: Always Test Before Demo
The most important rule when renovating a pre-1990 home is to always test for asbestos before starting demolition work. This will help you identify any asbestos-containing materials and take necessary precautions to avoid exposure.

## FAQ
Here are some frequently asked questions about asbestos in NZ homes:

* Q: Is fibrous cement cladding dangerous?
A: Fibrous cement cladding is generally considered safe if it is in good condition and not disturbed. However, if it is damaged or deteriorated, it can release asbestos fibres, which can be hazardous.
* Q: Do I have to disclose asbestos when selling my NZ home?
A: Yes, you are required to disclose any known asbestos-containing materials in your home when selling.
* Q: Can I paint over asbestos fibrous cement?
A: It is not recommended to paint over asbestos fibrous cement, as this can damage the material and release asbestos fibres.
* Q: How much does asbestos testing cost?
A: The cost of asbestos testing can range from $50 to $150 per sample, depending on the laboratory and the type of test.
* Q: What is a friable vs non-friable asbestos?
A: Friable asbestos is loose or powdery, while non-friable asbestos is bonded with a cement-like material. Friable asbestos is highly hazardous, as the loose fibres can easily become airborne and be inhaled.
