## Introduction
Fencing is a popular DIY project in New Zealand, and for good reason. Not only can it add value to your property, but it can also provide a sense of security and privacy. However, before you start digging, it's essential to determine whether you can tackle the project yourself or if you need to hire a professional. Generally, if your fence is under 2.5 metres high and doesn't require any complex construction, you can DIY it. But, if your fence is higher or more intricate, it's best to consult with a licensed builder or fencer. This comprehensive guide will walk you through the step-by-step process of building a fence in NZ, covering everything from materials to painting or staining. Whether you're a seasoned DIYer or a beginner, this guide will provide you with the knowledge and confidence to build a sturdy and attractive fence.

## Do you need building consent?
Before you start building your fence, it's crucial to check if you need a building consent. Under Schedule 1 of the Building Act 2004, fences under 2.5 metres high do not require a building consent. However, you must check your district plan for boundary setbacks and height rules, as these can vary depending on your location. For example, if you live in a heritage zone or a coastal area, there may be specific regulations governing fence height and design. Additionally, if you're planning to build a pool fence, you'll need to comply with the Fencing of Swimming Pools Act 1987, which requires a building consent and strict safety standards. It's always best to consult with your local council or a licensed builder to determine if you need a building consent for your fence.

## Materials
Choosing the right materials for your fence is crucial to its durability and appearance. Here are some popular options:

* **Timber**: H3.2 treated pine is a popular choice for fence posts, as it's resistant to rot and insect damage. However, if the posts will be in contact with the ground, you'll need to use H4 treated timber. Macrocarpa and cedar are also excellent options, but they can be more expensive. When choosing post size, a minimum of 90x90mm is recommended for a 1.8-metre fence.
* **Metal**: Colorbond, tubular steel, and aluminium are popular metal fencing options. They're durable, low-maintenance, and can be powder-coated in a range of colours.
* **Concrete**: Pre-cast concrete panels are a cheap and quick option, but they can be heavy and require additional support.
* **Bamboo and privacy screens**: Bamboo and privacy screens are great options for adding a natural, rustic touch to your fence. They're also eco-friendly and can be used to create a sense of seclusion.
* **Post caps**: Post caps are essential for preventing water ingress and rot. They come in a range of materials, including metal, plastic, and timber.

When selecting materials, consider factors such as durability, maintenance, and cost. For example, treated pine is a cost-effective option, but it may require more maintenance than metal or concrete. On the other hand, metal and concrete are more durable, but they can be more expensive.

## Laying out the fence line
Laying out the fence line is a critical step in the fencing process. Here's how to do it:

* Use a string line and spirit level to mark out the fence line.
* Check the boundary setbacks and height rules in your district plan.
* Mark the corner posts and ensure they're square to the fence line.
* Use a measuring tape to mark out the post spacing, which should be between 1.8-2.4 metres for timber fences.

## Setting posts
Setting posts is the most important step in the fencing process. Here's how to do it:

* Dig holes that are at least 1/3 of the post length in the ground, typically 600mm for a 1.8-metre fence.
* The hole diameter should be at least 3 times the post width.
* Tamp the first 100mm of soil to create a solid base.
* Fill the hole with rapid-set concrete, making sure to keep the post plumb.
* Brace the post while the concrete sets, which can take 24-48 hours.

## Rails and palings
Once the posts are set, you can start installing the rails and palings. Here's how to do it:

* Use a spirit level to ensure the rails are level.
* Nail or screw the palings to the rails, leaving a 15mm gap between each paling to allow for swelling.
* Use galvanised fixings to prevent rust and corrosion.
* Check the level as you go to ensure the fence is straight and level.

## Gates
Gates are an essential part of any fence. Here's how to build a sturdy gate:

* Use a hinge sizing chart to determine the correct hinge size for your gate.
* Choose a latch type that suits your needs, such as a magnetic latch or a traditional latch.
* Brace the gate frame diagonally to prevent sagging.
* Allow clearance for ground movement to prevent the gate from getting stuck.

## Painting or staining
Once the fence is built, you can paint or stain it to protect the timber and enhance its appearance. Here's how to do it:

* Use a penetrating oil, such as Cutek or Deks Olje D1, to protect the timber and prevent rot.
* Apply the oil according to the manufacturer's instructions, typically every 6-12 months.
* Avoid using paint, as it can trap moisture and cause the timber to rot.

## Common mistakes
Here are some common mistakes to avoid when building a fence:

* Posts not deep enough, which can cause the fence to lean or collapse.
* Wrong timber treatment rating, which can lead to rot and decay.
* No post caps, which can cause water ingress and rot.
* Palings butted too tight, which can cause them to swell and warp.
* Not checking for underground services before digging, which can cause damage to pipes and cables.
