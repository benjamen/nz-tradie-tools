## Introduction to Building a Fence in NZ
Building a fence is a popular DIY project in New Zealand, and for good reason. Not only can it add value to your property, but it can also provide a sense of security and privacy. However, before you start digging, it's essential to determine whether you can DIY the project or if you need to hire a professional. If your fence is simple and under 2.5m high, you can likely tackle it yourself. However, if your fence is complex, high, or requires specialized materials, it's best to leave it to the experts.

This comprehensive guide will walk you through the step-by-step process of building a fence in NZ, covering everything from materials to painting or staining. We'll also discuss common mistakes to avoid and provide answers to frequently asked questions. By the end of this guide, you'll be well-equipped to build a sturdy and attractive fence that will last for years to come.

## Do You Need Building Consent?
Before you start building your fence, it's crucial to check if you need building consent. Under Schedule 1 of the Building Act 2004, fences under 2.5m high do not require a building consent. However, you must check your district plan for boundary setbacks and height rules, as these can vary depending on your location. For example, if you live in a heritage area or have a property with a specific covenant, you may need to comply with additional regulations.

Pool fencing, on the other hand, has separate mandatory requirements. According to the Fencing of Swimming Pools Act 1987, all pools must be surrounded by a fence that meets specific safety standards. If you're building a fence around a pool, it's essential to consult with your local council to ensure you comply with all regulations.

## Materials for Your Fence
The type of material you choose for your fence will depend on your budget, personal preference, and the style you're aiming to achieve. Here are some popular options:

* **Timber**: H3.2 treated pine is a popular choice for fence posts, as it's durable and resistant to rot. However, if the posts will be in contact with the ground, you'll need to use a minimum of H4 treated pine. Macrocarpa and cedar are also excellent options, but they can be more expensive. When choosing post size, a minimum of 90x90mm is recommended for a 1.8m high fence.
* **Metal**: Colorbond, tubular steel, and aluminium are all popular metal options for fencing. They're durable, low-maintenance, and can be powder-coated in a range of colours.
* **Concrete**: Pre-cast concrete panels are a cheap and quick option for fencing, but they can be heavy and require specialized equipment to install.
* **Bamboo and privacy screens**: Bamboo and privacy screens are great options for adding a natural, rustic touch to your fence. They're also relatively inexpensive and easy to install.
* **Post caps**: Post caps are essential for preventing water ingress and extending the life of your fence posts. You can choose from a range of materials, including timber, metal, and plastic.

The cost of materials can vary depending on the type and quality you choose. On average, you can expect to pay between $25-$60 per metre for DIY fencing materials. For example:

## Laying Out the Fence Line
Before you start digging, it's essential to lay out the fence line accurately. Here's how:

* Use a string line and spirit level to mark out the fence line.
* Check the post spacing, which should be between 1.8-2.4m centres for timber fences.
* Mark the location of the corner posts, which should be slightly larger than the standard posts.
* Use a measuring tape to double-check the distances and ensure the fence line is straight.

## Setting Posts
Setting the posts is the most critical step in building a fence. If you get this wrong, everything else will follow suit. Here's how to do it correctly:

* Dig holes that are at least 1/3 of the post length in the ground, typically 600mm for a 1.8m high fence.
* The hole diameter should be at least 3x the post width.
* Tamp the first 100mm of soil to create a solid base.
* Fill the hole with rapid-set concrete, making sure to fill it to the top.
* Keep the post plumb and bracing it while the concrete sets, which can take 24-48 hours.

It's essential to use the right tools for the job, such as a post hole digger and a spirit level. You can also use job management software like Xero or Fergus to help you organize your materials and labour costs.

## Rails and Palings
Once the posts are set, you can start attaching the rails and palings. Here's how:

* The rail height should be at least 100mm below the top of the post.
* Use galvanised fixings, such as nails or screws, to attach the palings to the rails. Avoid using untreated fixings, as they will rust in NZ's humid climate.
* Space the palings 15mm apart to allow for swelling and shrinkage.
* Check the level as you go to ensure the fence is straight and level.

## Gates
Gates can be a bit tricky to build, but with the right techniques, you can create a sturdy and functional gate. Here's how:

* Use hinge sizing that's suitable for the weight and size of the gate.
* Choose a latch type that's secure and easy to use.
* Brace the gate frame diagonally to prevent sagging.
* Allow clearance for ground movement, which can cause the gate to bind or sag.

## Painting or Staining
Once the fence is built, you can start thinking about painting or staining. Here are some options:

* **Penetrating oil**: Cutek and Deks Olje D1 are popular penetrating oils that can help protect the timber and enhance its natural colour.
* **Paint**: You can use a range of paints, including water-based and oil-based options. However, keep in mind that paint can peel and flake over time, especially in NZ's harsh climate.

It's essential to note that bare treated pine can last longer with oil than with paint. You should re-oil your fence every 1-2 years to maintain its integrity.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
Here are some common mistakes to avoid when building a fence:

* Posts not deep enough, which can cause the fence to lean or collapse.
* Wrong timber treatment rating, which can lead to rot and decay.
* No post caps, which can cause water ingress and damage the posts.
* Palings butted too tight, which can cause them to swell and warp.
* Not checking for underground services before digging, which can cause damage to pipes and cables.

## Frequently Asked Questions
Here are some frequently asked questions about building a fence in NZ:

* **How close to the boundary can I build a fence?**: You can build a fence up to the boundary, but you should check with your neighbour and local council to ensure you comply with all regulations.
* **How long will a treated pine fence last?**: A treated pine fence can last for 10-20 years, depending on the quality of the materials and the maintenance.
* **Do I need my neighbour's agreement for a boundary fence?**: Yes, you should try to get your neighbour's agreement for a boundary fence, as it can affect their property value and privacy.
* **How much does it cost to hire a fencer?**: The cost of hiring a fencer can vary depending on the location, materials, and complexity of the job. On average, you can expect to pay between $80-$160 per metre for professional fencing services.
* **Can I use untreated timber for my fence?**: No, it's not recommended to use untreated timber for your fence, as it can rot and decay quickly in NZ's humid climate.
