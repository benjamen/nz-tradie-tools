## NZ Building Code H1 Requirements
The 2023 update to the NZ Building Code H1 requirements introduced new minimum R-values for insulation, which vary by climate zone. The R-value is a measure of an insulation material's ability to resist heat flow, with higher R-values indicating better insulation performance. The minimum R-values for different climate zones in New Zealand are as follows:
- Ceiling: R6.6 (Auckland and Northland), R6.8 (Waikato, Bay of Plenty, and Gisborne), R7.0 (Hawke's Bay, Manawatu, and Taranaki)
- Underfloor: R2.5 (Auckland and Northland), R2.8 (Waikato, Bay of Plenty, and Gisborne), R3.2 (Hawke's Bay, Manawatu, and Taranaki)
- Walls: R2.4 (Auckland and Northland), R2.6 (Waikato, Bay of Plenty, and Gisborne), R2.8 (Hawke's Bay, Manawatu, and Taranaki)

A map of New Zealand's climate zones can be found on the MBIE website, helping you determine the specific requirements for your region. It's essential to note that these requirements apply to both new builds and retrofits, although the approach and costs may differ significantly between the two. For new builds, insulation can be easily integrated into the construction process, whereas retrofits often require more careful planning and execution to ensure compliance with the H1 requirements.

## Types of Insulation
There are various types of insulation available for different parts of the home, each with its own advantages and disadvantages.

### Ceiling Insulation
Ceiling insulation is one of the most effective ways to reduce heat loss in a home. The most common types of ceiling insulation are:
- Bulk batts: Made from materials like polyester, glasswool, or rockwool, these batts are designed to fit snugly between ceiling joists. Brands like Pink Batts, Bradford Gold, and Knauf offer high-quality bulk batts.
- Blown-in loose fill: This type of insulation involves blowing loose material, such as glasswool or cellulose, into the ceiling space. While it can be more expensive than bulk batts, it can provide better coverage and R-values.

### Underfloor Insulation
Underfloor insulation is more challenging to install, especially in existing homes. The most common types of underfloor insulation are:
- Foil: While foil insulation can be effective in some situations, its use is often limited due to concerns about its durability and potential for degradation over time. In some cases, the use of foil insulation may even be banned.
- Bulk batts: Stapling bulk batts to the underside of the floor joists is a common method for underfloor insulation. This approach can be DIY-friendly, but it requires careful planning and execution.
- Rigid foam boards: These boards can be installed under the floor, providing a high level of insulation. However, this approach can be more expensive and may require professional installation.

### Wall Insulation
Wall insulation is often the most challenging and expensive to install, especially in existing homes. The most common types of wall insulation are:
- Blown-in: This involves blowing insulation material, such as polystyrene beads or glasswool, into the wall cavity. This approach can be effective, but it requires careful planning and execution.
- Internal rigid foam: Installing rigid foam boards inside the wall cavity can provide high levels of insulation. However, this approach can be expensive and may require professional installation.
- External wrap: Installing an external insulation wrap around the entire home can provide high levels of insulation. However, this approach is often only feasible during recladding or major renovations.

## Warmer Kiwi Homes Subsidy
The Warmer Kiwi Homes programme, administered by the Energy Efficiency and Conservation Authority (EECA), provides subsidies for insulation and heating upgrades in low-income households. To be eligible for the subsidy, you must hold a Community Services Card or live in a low-income area. The subsidy can cover up to 80% of the cost of insulation, with a maximum cap of $2,500 for insulation and $2,500 for a heat pump or other heating system.

To apply for the Warmer Kiwi Homes subsidy, you will need to contact a registered provider, who will assess your eligibility and provide a quote for the insulation work. The registered provider will then submit the application on your behalf, and EECA will review and approve the subsidy.

## Is Ceiling Insulation DIY?
Yes, ceiling insulation can be a DIY-friendly project, especially when using bulk batts in an accessible ceiling space. However, it's essential to take necessary safety precautions, such as wearing a P2 mask, eye protection, and a full coverall. Additionally, it's crucial to ensure that no electrical work is involved, as this can pose a significant safety risk.

## Healthy Homes Standards for Landlords
The Healthy Homes Standards, introduced in 2019, set out minimum requirements for insulation in rental properties. The standards require a minimum R-value of R2.9 for ceiling insulation in most climate zones, with higher R-values required in colder climates. Landlords must comply with these standards by the specified deadlines, which vary depending on the type of rental property and the date of tenancy.
