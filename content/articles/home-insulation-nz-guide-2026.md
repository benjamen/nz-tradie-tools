## NZ Building Code H1 Requirements
The NZ Building Code H1 requirements outline the minimum R-values for insulation in different climate zones across New Zealand. As of 2023, the updated requirements are as follows:
- Ceiling insulation: R6.6–R7.0
- Underfloor insulation: R2.5–R3.2
- Wall insulation: R2.4–R2.8

A map of New Zealand's climate zones can be found on the MBIE website, which helps determine the specific requirements for your region. It's essential to note that new builds and retrofits have different requirements, with new builds typically needing to meet more stringent standards. For example, in Auckland, a new build would require a minimum R-value of R6.6 for ceiling insulation, while a retrofit might only require R3.6.

## Types of Insulation
Insulation comes in various forms, each with its own set of benefits and drawbacks. The most common types of insulation are:

### Ceiling Insulation
- Bulk batts: Made from materials like polyester, glasswool, or rockwool, these are the most common type of ceiling insulation. Brands like Pink Batts, Bradford Gold, and Knauf offer high-quality bulk batts.
- Blown-in loose fill: This type of insulation is made from loose material that is blown into the ceiling space, often using a machine. It's a good option for hard-to-reach areas or irregularly shaped ceilings.

### Underfloor Insulation
- Foil: While once a popular choice, foil insulation has limited effectiveness and is sometimes banned if it's old and degraded. It's not recommended for new installations.
- Bulk batts: Stapled up between the floor joists, bulk batts are a good option for underfloor insulation. However, installation can be tricky, and it's often best left to professionals.
- Rigid foam boards: These provide excellent insulation and can be installed under the floor, but they can be more expensive than other options.

### Wall Insulation
- Blown-in: This involves blowing polystyrene beads or glasswool into the wall cavity, often through holes drilled in the exterior cladding. It's a good option for retrofits, but it can be messy and requires specialized equipment.
- Internal rigid foam: Installing rigid foam boards inside the wall cavity is another option, but it can be more expensive and may require significant renovations.
- External wrap: This involves wrapping the entire exterior of the house in insulation, often during recladding. It's a comprehensive solution but can be costly and may not be feasible for all homeowners.

## Warmer Kiwi Homes Subsidy
The Warmer Kiwi Homes programme, administered by the Energy Efficiency and Conservation Authority (EECA), provides subsidies for insulation and heating upgrades. To be eligible, you must hold a Community Services Card or live in a low-income area. The subsidy covers up to 80% of the cost of insulation, with a cap of $2,500 for a full home insulation package. To apply, you'll need to find a registered provider, such as a local insulation installer, and work with them to complete the installation.

## Ceiling Insulation Costs
For a 100m² ceiling, the cost of insulation can vary significantly depending on the type and R-value. For example:
- Pink Batts R3.6 DIY: ~$700 (materials only)
- Pink Batts R3.6 installed: $1,800-$3,500
- R6.6 (code-compliant) installed: $2,800-$5,000

## Is Ceiling Insulation DIY?
Yes, ceiling insulation can be a DIY job, especially if you have an accessible ceiling space. However, it's essential to take safety precautions, such as wearing a P2 mask, eye protection, and a full coverall. It's also crucial to ensure that no electrical work is involved, as this can be hazardous and may require a licensed electrician.

## Return on Investment
Insulation can provide significant energy savings, with typical NZ heating cost savings ranging from $400 to $900 per year. The payback period for insulation can be as short as 3-8 years, depending on the type and quality of the insulation. Additionally, insulation can improve comfort and health benefits, particularly for vulnerable populations like the elderly and young children.

## Healthy Homes Standards for Landlords
As of 2019, the Healthy Homes Standards require landlords to meet minimum insulation requirements for rental properties. This includes:
- Ceiling insulation: R-value requirements vary by climate zone, but most areas require a minimum R-value of R3.6
- Compliance deadlines: Landlords have until 2025 to ensure their rental properties meet the new standards
