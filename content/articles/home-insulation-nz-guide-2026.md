## Introduction
Home insulation is widely regarded as the highest-return-on-investment home improvement in New Zealand, offering significant energy savings, improved comfort, and enhanced health benefits. As of 2023, the NZ Building Code H1 requirements have been updated to reflect the importance of insulation in reducing heat loss and maintaining a warm, dry, and healthy living environment. This guide will cover the essential aspects of home insulation in NZ, including the updated H1 requirements, types of insulation, costs, and how to access the Warmer Kiwi Homes subsidy. Whether you're a homeowner, landlord, or tradesperson, understanding the ins and outs of insulation is crucial for making informed decisions about your property.

## NZ Building Code H1 Requirements
The NZ Building Code H1 requirements outline the minimum insulation standards for new builds and retrofits. As of 2023, the updated H1 requirements specify minimum R-values for ceiling, underfloor, and wall insulation, varying by climate zone. The climate zones are divided into four regions: 
- Zone 1 (Auckland, Northland, and parts of Waikato): R6.6 for ceilings, R2.5 for underfloor, and R2.4 for walls
- Zone 2 (remainder of Waikato, Bay of Plenty, and parts of Hawke's Bay): R6.7 for ceilings, R2.6 for underfloor, and R2.5 for walls
- Zone 3 (remainder of Hawke's Bay, Manawatu-Wanganui, and parts of Wellington): R6.8 for ceilings, R2.7 for underfloor, and R2.6 for walls
- Zone 4 (remainder of Wellington, Tasman, and parts of Marlborough): R7.0 for ceilings, R2.8 for underfloor, and R2.7 for walls

A map of NZ climate zones is available on the MBIE website. It's essential to note that new builds must comply with the updated H1 requirements, while retrofits are encouraged to meet these standards to ensure optimal energy efficiency and comfort.

## Types of Insulation
The type of insulation suitable for your home depends on the area to be insulated: ceiling, underfloor, or walls. 

### Ceiling Insulation
Ceiling insulation can be installed using bulk batts (polyester, glasswool, or rockwool) or blown-in loose fill. Bulk batts are a popular choice, with brands like Pink Batts, Bradford Gold, Knauf, and Autex offering high-quality products. The R-value of ceiling insulation ranges from R2.5 to R7.0, with higher R-values providing better thermal performance. DIY installation is possible for bulk batts, but it's crucial to ensure proper safety measures, such as wearing a P2 mask, eye protection, and a full coverall.

### Underfloor Insulation
Underfloor insulation can be installed using foil, bulk batts stapled up, or rigid foam boards. Foil insulation is limited in its effectiveness and may be banned in some areas if it's old and degraded. Bulk batts stapled up are a more effective option, but DIY installation can be tricky. Rigid foam boards offer high R-values but are more expensive.

### Wall Insulation
Wall insulation is the most challenging to retrofit, as it often requires significant construction work. Blown-in insulation (polystyrene beads or glasswool) is one option, while internal rigid foam or external wrap are other possibilities. However, wall insulation is usually only addressed during recladding or major renovations.

## Warmer Kiwi Homes Subsidy
The Warmer Kiwi Homes programme, administered by EECA, offers subsidies for insulation and heating upgrades to eligible homeowners. To be eligible, you must hold a Community Services Card or live in a low-income area. The subsidy amount is up to 80% of the total cost, with a cap of $2,500 for insulation and $2,500 for a heat pump or other approved heating solution. To apply, you'll need to contact a registered provider, who will assess your home and provide a quote for the work. Approved providers can be found on the EECA website.

## Ceiling Insulation Costs
The cost of ceiling insulation varies depending on the R-value and installation method. For a 100m² ceiling, the cost of Pink Batts R3.6 insulation is around $700 for DIY installation, while professional installation can range from $1,800 to $3,500. For a code-compliant R6.6 insulation, the installed cost can range from $2,800 to $5,000.

## Underfloor Insulation Costs
The cost of underfloor insulation also varies depending on the type and installation method. For an 80m² underfloor area, the cost of foil insulation (if permitted) can range from $600 to $1,500, while bulk polyester batts installed can cost between $2,000 and $4,000.

## Is Ceiling Insulation DIY?
Yes, ceiling insulation can be a DIY project, especially when using bulk batts in an accessible ceiling space. However, it's essential to take safety precautions, such as wearing a P2 mask, eye protection, and a full coverall. Additionally, ensure you have a clear understanding of the installation process and any local building regulations.

## Return on Investment
The return on investment for insulation can be significant, with typical NZ heating cost savings ranging from $400 to $900 per year. The payback period for insulation can be as short as 3-8 years, depending on the type and quality of insulation, as well as the local climate. Moreover, insulation can improve comfort and health benefits, making it a worthwhile investment for homeowners.

## Healthy Homes Standards for Landlords
The Healthy Homes Standards, introduced in 2019, set minimum insulation requirements for rental properties. Landlords must ensure their properties meet these standards, which include:
- Ceiling insulation with a minimum R-value of R2.9
- Underfloor insulation with a minimum R-value of R1.3
- Wall insulation is not mandatory but is recommended
Compliance deadlines vary depending on the property type and location, but all rental properties must meet the standards by 2025.
