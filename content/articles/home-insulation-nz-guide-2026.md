Whether you're a homeowner looking to improve your living space or a landlord seeking to comply with the Healthy Homes Standards, this article will provide you with the knowledge you need to make informed decisions about insulation.

## NZ Building Code H1 Requirements
The NZ Building Code H1 requirements outline the minimum insulation standards for new builds and retrofits in New Zealand. As of 2023, the updated requirements specify minimum R-values for ceiling, underfloor, and wall insulation, varying by climate zone. The climate zones are divided into four regions: North, Central, South, and Alpine. A map of the NZ climate zones can be found on the MBIE website. For ceiling insulation, the minimum R-values range from R6.6 in the North climate zone to R7.0 in the Alpine climate zone. Underfloor insulation requires minimum R-values of R2.5 in the North climate zone to R3.2 in the Alpine climate zone. Wall insulation minimum R-values range from R2.4 in the North climate zone to R2.8 in the Alpine climate zone. It's essential to note that these requirements apply to both new builds and retrofits, although the specific requirements may vary depending on the age and condition of the property.

## Types of Insulation
There are several types of insulation available for ceilings, underfloor, and walls, each with its own advantages and disadvantages.

### Ceiling Insulation
Ceiling insulation is one of the most effective and accessible forms of insulation. Bulk batts, such as polyester, glasswool, and rockwool, are popular options, offering high R-values and ease of installation. Blown-in loose fill insulation is another option, providing a high R-value and flexibility in terms of installation. Brands like Pink Batts, Bradford Gold, Knauf, and Autex offer a range of ceiling insulation products. The R-value of ceiling insulation can vary significantly, with DIY options starting from around R2.5 and installed options reaching up to R6.6 or higher.

### Underfloor Insulation
Underfloor insulation is more challenging to install, particularly in existing homes. Foil insulation is sometimes used, although its effectiveness is limited, and it may be banned in certain areas due to concerns about old, degraded foil. Bulk batts stapled up to the underside of the floor are a more effective option, while rigid foam boards offer high R-values and durability. DIY installation is possible but can be tricky, and it's often recommended to hire a professional.

### Wall Insulation
Wall insulation is the most difficult to retrofit, as it often requires significant modifications to the building structure. Blown-in insulation, such as polystyrene beads or glasswool, can be used to fill wall cavities, while internal rigid foam boards or external wrap insulation can be installed during recladding. However, wall insulation is usually only done during major renovations or rebuilds.

## Warmer Kiwi Homes Subsidy
The Warmer Kiwi Homes subsidy programme, administered by the Energy Efficiency and Conservation Authority (EECA), provides financial assistance to low-income households to improve their home insulation. The programme offers up to 80% of the insulation cost, with a maximum subsidy of $2,500 for insulation and $2,500 for a heat pump. To be eligible, households must hold a Community Services Card or live in a low-income area. The subsidy can be applied for through a registered provider, and a list of approved providers can be found on the EECA website.

## Ceiling Insulation Costs
The cost of ceiling insulation can vary significantly depending on the R-value and installation method. For a 100m² ceiling, the cost of Pink Batts R3.6 insulation can range from around $700 for DIY installation to $1,800-$3,500 for professional installation. For a higher R-value of R6.6, which meets the NZ Building Code H1 requirements, the installed cost can range from $2,800 to $5,000.

## Underfloor Insulation Costs
The cost of underfloor insulation also varies depending on the type and installation method. For an 80m² underfloor area, the cost of foil insulation (if permitted) can range from $600 to $1,500. For bulk polyester batts installed by a professional, the cost can range from $2,000 to $4,000.

## Is Ceiling Insulation DIY?
Yes, ceiling insulation can be a DIY job, particularly for bulk batts in an accessible ceiling space. However, it's essential to take safety precautions, including wearing a P2 mask, eye protection, and a full coverall. No electrical work is involved, making it a relatively straightforward DIY project.

## Return on Investment
The return on investment for insulation can be significant, with typical NZ heating cost savings ranging from $400 to $900 per year. In addition to the financial benefits, insulation can also improve comfort and health, particularly for vulnerable populations such as the elderly and young children.

## Healthy Homes Standards for Landlords
The Healthy Homes Standards, introduced in 2019, set minimum insulation requirements for rental properties. The standards require a minimum R-value of R2.9 for ceiling insulation in the North climate zone and R3.3 in the South climate zone. The compliance deadline for the standards is July 2024 for new tenancies and July 2025 for existing tenancies.
