## Do you need building consent for a deck?
Under Schedule 1 of the Building Act 2004, decks that are less than 1.5m above ground level generally do not require building consent. However, there are some exceptions and additional considerations to keep in mind. For example, if your deck is near a pool, you may need to comply with pool fencing requirements. Additionally, if your deck is attached to a consented structure in a way that affects its weathertightness, or if it includes a significant structural element, you may need to obtain consent. It's also important to check your district plan for any setback rules or other regulations that may apply. ## Timber selection
When it comes to selecting timber for your deck, there are several options to consider, each with its own advantages and disadvantages. Here are some of the most popular types of timber used for decking in NZ:

* **Pine (treated)**: H3.2 treated pine is suitable for above-ground decking, while H4 treated pine is required for posts that will be in contact with the ground. Kiln-dried pine is recommended for decking to minimize cupping and warping. The cost of pine decking ranges from $30 to $60 per linear metre.
* **Hardwood (kwila/merbau, vitex)**: Hardwood decking offers a beautiful finish and is more durable than pine. However, it is also more expensive, with prices ranging from $60 to $120 per linear metre.
* **Composite decking (Trex, Futurewood, EasyDek)**: Composite decking is a low-maintenance option that is resistant to splintering and rot. It is also more expensive than traditional timber options, with prices ranging from $100 to $200 per square metre. Composite decking often comes with a 25-year warranty.

## Design considerations
When designing your deck, there are several factors to consider to ensure that it is safe, functional, and aesthetically pleasing. Here are some key design considerations:

* **Board spacing**: The spacing between decking boards will depend on the type of timber used. For timber decking, a 5mm gap is recommended, while composite decking should be spaced according to the manufacturer's specifications.
* **Joist spacing**: The typical joist spacing for a 90mm decking board is 450mm centres.
* **Post sizing**: The minimum post size for a deck is 90x90mm.
* **Ledger attachment**: The ledger attachment to the house is critical for ensuring weathertightness.
* **Drainage falls**: The deck should be designed with a minimum fall of 1:100 to ensure that water drains away from the house.

## Step-by-step construction overview
Building a deck can be a complex process, but here is a step-by-step overview of the construction process:

1. **Layout and posts**: Mark out the deck area and dig holes for the posts. Concrete the posts into place and allow to set.
2. **Bearer installation**: Install the bearers between the posts, ensuring that they are level and securely attached.
3. **Joist frame**: Install the joist frame, spacing the joists according to the design specifications.
4. **Decking boards**: Install the decking boards, starting from the outside edge and working inwards.
5. **Balustrades**: If required, install the balustrades, ensuring that they meet the minimum height and opening requirements.
6. **Finishing**: Finish the deck with oil or paint, depending on the type of timber used.

## Balustrade requirements
Balustrades are required for decks that are more than 1m above the adjacent ground level. The minimum height for balustrades is 1m, and openings must not exceed 100mm to ensure child safety. Balustrades can be DIY or purchased as kit systems.

## Deck maintenance
To ensure that your deck lasts for many years, regular maintenance is essential. Here are some tips for maintaining your deck:

* **Pine**: Re-oil or paint every 2-3 years to protect the timber from the elements.
* **Hardwood**: Oil annually for the first 2 years, then as needed to maintain the timber's natural colour and texture.
* **Composite**: Wash annually with soap and water to keep the deck clean and free from debris. No oiling is required.
