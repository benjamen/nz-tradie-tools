## Do you need building consent for a deck?
Under Schedule 1 of the Building Act 2004, decks that are less than 1.5m above ground level generally do not require building consent. However, there are some important exceptions to consider. If your deck is attached to a consented structure, such as a house, in a way that affects its weathertightness, you may need to obtain consent. Additionally, if your deck has a significant structural element, such as a large beam or post, you may also need to obtain consent. It's also important to check your district plan, as some councils have specific setback rules or requirements for pool fencing if your deck is near a pool. Balustrade height rules also apply, with a minimum height of 1m required where the fall from the deck exceeds 1m. To determine if you need building consent, it's always best to call your local council's duty planner for free advice. ## Timber selection
When it comes to selecting timber for your deck, there are several options to consider. Here are a few popular choices:
* Pine (treated): H3.2 for above-ground decking and H4 for posts in the ground. Kiln-dried pine is a good option for decking, as it is less prone to cupping and warping. Price: $30–$60 per linear metre.
* Hardwood (kwila/merbau, vitex): These timbers offer a beautiful finish and are more dense than pine. However, they are also more expensive. Price: $60–$120 per linear metre.
* Composite decking (Trex, Futurewood, EasyDek): Composite decking is a low-maintenance option that is resistant to splintering and rot. It also comes with a 25-year warranty. Price: $100–$200 per square metre.

## Design considerations
When designing your deck, there are several key considerations to keep in mind. These include:
* Board spacing: 5mm for timber decking, and as per the manufacturer's specifications for composite decking.
* Joist spacing: 450mm centres are typical for 90mm decking.
* Post sizing: 90×90 minimum is recommended.
* Ledger attachment to house: Weathertightness is critical, so make sure to use a suitable flashing system.
* Drainage falls: A minimum fall of 1:100 is recommended to ensure water runs away from the house.

## Step-by-step construction overview
Here is a step-by-step guide to constructing your deck:
1. Layout and posts: Mark out the deck area and dig holes for the posts. Pour concrete into the holes and set the posts.
2. Bearer installation: Install the bearers on top of the posts, ensuring they are level and securely attached.
3. Joist frame: Install the joists on top of the bearers, spacing them according to your design specifications.
4. Decking boards: Install the decking boards on top of the joists, ensuring they are securely attached and spaced correctly.
5. Balustrades: If required, install balustrades to ensure the deck is safe and compliant with building regulations.
6. Finishing: Oil or paint the deck to protect it from the elements and enhance its appearance.

## Balustrade requirements
If your deck is more than 1m above the adjacent ground, you will need to install balustrades to ensure safety. The minimum height for balustrades is 1m, and openings must not exceed 100mm to prevent child safety hazards. You can either build your own balustrades or purchase a kit system.

## Deck maintenance
To keep your deck looking its best, regular maintenance is essential. Here are some tips:
* Pine: Re-oil or paint every 2–3 years to protect it from the elements.
* Hardwood: Oil annually for the first 2 years, then as needed.
* Composite: Wash annually with soap and water, and avoid oiling or painting.
