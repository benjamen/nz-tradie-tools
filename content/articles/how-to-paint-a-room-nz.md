## Introduction
Painting a room is one of the most rewarding and cost-effective DIY jobs for New Zealand homeowners. Not only can it completely transform the look and feel of a space, but it's also a great way to add value to your property. With the right tools, materials, and a bit of patience, you can achieve professional-looking results without breaking the bank. In this comprehensive guide, we'll take you through the step-by-step process of painting a room in NZ, covering everything from preparation to completion. By the end of this article, you'll be equipped with the knowledge and confidence to tackle your next painting project with ease.

## What You'll Need
Before you start painting, it's essential to gather all the necessary tools and materials. Here's a list of what you'll need:

* Roller and extension pole ($20-$50)
* Paint tray ($10-$20)
* Brushes (2.5" angled sash) ($10-$30)
* Drop cloths ($20-$50)
* Masking tape ($5-$10)
* Filler (Polyfilla or equivalent) ($10-$20)
* Sandpaper ($5-$10)
* Sugar soap ($10-$20)
* Paint (water-based or oil-based) ($35-$80 per litre)

When it comes to choosing the right paint, there are several factors to consider. Water-based paints are generally easier to clean up and dry faster, while oil-based paints provide a harder, more durable finish. In NZ, popular paint brands include Resene, Dulux, and Wattyl. For a standard room, you can expect to pay between $150-$300 for materials, depending on the size of the room and the type of paint you choose.

To calculate how much paint you'll need, you'll need to measure the surface area of the walls, ceiling, and trim. A general rule of thumb is to allow for 1 litre of paint per 12-16 square metres of surface area, depending on the porosity of the surface. For example, if you're painting a room with a surface area of 40 square metres, you'll need approximately 2.5-3.5 litres of paint.

## Prep (The Key to a Good Result)
Preparation is the key to achieving a professional-looking finish. Here are the steps you'll need to follow:

* Wash the walls with sugar soap to remove any grease, grime, or old smoke residue.
* Fill any holes or cracks with Polyfilla or equivalent, and sand back to a smooth finish.
* Sand any rough patches or areas where the filler has been applied.
* Mask up skirting boards, architraves, and power points using masking tape.
* Protect floors with drop cloths to prevent any paint spills or stains.
* Prime new GIB or repaired patches to ensure a uniform finish. This is especially important if the new surface is a different colour to the surrounding area.

It's essential to take your time during the preparation phase, as a good prep job will make all the difference in the final result. Don't be tempted to skip any of these steps, as it can lead to a subpar finish and potentially costly repairs down the line.

## Order of Painting
When it comes to painting, it's essential to follow a specific order to avoid lap marks and ensure a smooth finish. Here's the order you should follow:

1. Ceiling: Start by painting the ceiling, working in sections to maintain a wet edge.
2. Walls: Once the ceiling is complete, move on to the walls, working from top to bottom.
3. Trim: Finally, paint the trim, including skirting boards, architraves, and doors.

By following this order, you'll be able to avoid lap marks and ensure a seamless finish. It's also essential to work in a consistent pattern, using a W-pattern or a circular motion to apply the paint.

## Cutting In
Cutting in is the process of painting the edges and corners of a room, where the walls meet the ceiling, trim, and floors. It's a crucial step in achieving a professional-looking finish, and it requires a bit of skill and patience. Here's how to do it:

* Use an angled brush (2.5" sash) to cut in along the edges and corners.
* Work in small sections, maintaining a wet edge to avoid lap marks.
* Use the 3-wet-edge technique, where you paint the edge, the corner, and the adjacent surface in one continuous motion.

Rushing the cutting-in process is one of the biggest amateur mistakes you can make. Take your time, and work slowly and carefully to achieve a smooth, even finish.

## Rolling
Once you've completed the cutting-in process, it's time to start rolling. Here are some tips to keep in mind:

* Use a W-pattern or a circular motion to apply the paint, working in sections to maintain a wet edge.
* Keep the roller loaded with paint, but not so much that it drips or runs.
* Avoid applying too much pressure, which can create roller marks or texture.
* Work in a consistent pattern, using a level to ensure the roller is straight and even.

It's essential to maintain a wet edge when rolling, as this will help to prevent lap marks and ensure a smooth finish. If you need to apply a second coat, wait until the first coat is touch dry, but not bone dry.

## Trim and Skirting
When it comes to painting trim and skirting, it's essential to use the right brush and technique. Here are some tips to keep in mind:

* Use a high-quality brush with synthetic bristles, which will provide a smooth finish and minimal brush marks.
* Choose the right paint type for the job. Oil-based paints are often preferred for trim and skirting, as they provide a harder, more durable finish.
* Work in small sections, using a steady hand and a consistent pattern to achieve sharp, clean lines.

## Drying Times and Second Coats
The drying time for paint will depend on the type of paint you're using, as well as the temperature and humidity of the environment. Here are some general guidelines to keep in mind:

* Resene and Dulux water-based paints typically take 2-4 hours to dry, depending on the conditions.
* Wattyl oil-based paints can take 4-6 hours to dry, depending on the conditions.
* If you need to apply a second coat, wait until the first coat is touch dry, but not bone dry.

It's essential to wait the recommended amount of time before applying a second coat, as this will help to prevent the paint from becoming uneven or textured.

## Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them
Here are some common mistakes to avoid when painting a room:

* Skipping the primer: This can lead to a subpar finish and potentially costly repairs down the line.
* Painting over dusty or dirty surfaces: This can lead to a rough, uneven finish and can compromise the adhesion of the paint.
* Using the wrong roller nap: This can lead to texture or roller marks, and can compromise the overall finish.
* Painting in direct sunlight: This can lead to uneven drying and can compromise the finish.

By avoiding these common mistakes, you can achieve a professional-looking finish and ensure a successful painting project.
