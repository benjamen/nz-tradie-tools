## Introduction
Painting a room is one of the best DIY jobs for NZ homeowners, offering a cost-effective way to refresh and revitalise any space. With the right tools, materials, and techniques, you can achieve professional-looking results without breaking the bank. In this comprehensive guide, we'll take you through the step-by-step process of painting a room in NZ, covering everything from preparation to completion. By the end of this article, you'll be equipped with the knowledge and confidence to tackle your next painting project with ease.

Whether you're looking to update a single room or your entire home, painting is a great way to add value, improve aesthetics, and create a fresh new look. With a wide range of colours and finishes available, you can choose the perfect hue to suit your style and decor. In NZ, popular paint brands like Resene, Dulux, and Wattyl offer high-quality products that are designed to withstand our unique climate and conditions.

## What You'll Need
Before you start painting, it's essential to gather the right tools and materials. Here's a list of what you'll need:

* Roller and extension pole ($20-$50)
* Paint tray ($10-$30)
* Brushes (2.5" angled sash) ($10-$30)
* Drop cloths ($20-$50)
* Masking tape ($5-$10)
* Filler (Polyfilla or equivalent) ($10-$20)
* Sandpaper ($5-$10)
* Sugar soap ($10-$20)
* Paint (water-based or oil-based) ($35-$80 per litre)

When it comes to choosing the right paint, there are several factors to consider. Water-based paints are a popular choice in NZ, offering a low-odour, easy-to-clean finish. Oil-based paints, on the other hand, provide a harder, more durable finish, but can take longer to dry and have stronger fumes. Some popular paint types in NZ include:

* Flat (matte) finish: $35-$50 per litre
* Low-sheen finish: $40-$60 per litre
* Semi-gloss finish: $50-$70 per litre
* Gloss finish: $60-$80 per litre

To calculate how much paint you'll need, you'll need to consider the surface area of the walls, ceiling, and trim. A general rule of thumb is to allow for 1 litre of paint per 12-16 square metres of surface area, depending on the colour and finish. You can use online paint calculators or consult with a professional to get a more accurate estimate.

## Prep (The Key to a Good Result)
Preparation is key to achieving a professional-looking finish. Here are the steps to follow:

* Wash the walls with sugar soap to remove grease, grime, and old smoke residue.
* Fill any holes or cracks with Polyfilla or equivalent, and sand back to a smooth finish.
* Sand any rough patches or areas with uneven texture.
* Mask up skirting boards, architraves, and power points using masking tape.
* Protect floors with drop cloths to prevent paint spills and stains.
* Prime new GIB or repaired patches to ensure a uniform finish. This is especially important if the new surface is a different colour or texture to the surrounding area.

Priming is a crucial step that many DIY painters skip, but it's essential for achieving a professional-looking finish. Without primer, the paint may not adhere properly, leading to a patchy or uneven finish. In NZ, popular primer brands like Resene and Dulux offer a range of products specifically designed for our unique climate and conditions.

## Order of Painting
When it comes to painting, it's essential to follow the right order to avoid lap marks and uneven finishes. Here's the recommended order:

* Ceiling: Start by painting the ceiling, working in sections to maintain a wet edge.
* Walls: Once the ceiling is complete, move on to the walls, working from top to bottom.
* Trim: Finally, paint the trim, including skirting boards, architraves, and doors.

This order makes sense because the ceiling is the largest surface area, and painting it first allows you to work in a logical and efficient manner. By working from top to bottom, you can prevent paint from dripping or running onto freshly painted surfaces.

## Cutting In
Cutting in is the process of painting the edges and corners of a room, where the walls meet the ceiling, trim, and floors. This is a critical step that requires patience and attention to detail. Here's how to do it:

* Use an angled brush (2.5" sash) to paint the edges and corners.
* Work in small sections, maintaining a wet edge to prevent lap marks.
* Use the 3-wet-edge technique: paint the edge, then the corner, and finally the adjacent surface.
* Take your time and work slowly, as rushing cutting in is the biggest amateur mistake.

Cutting in is a skill that takes practice, so don't be discouraged if it takes a few attempts to get it right. Remember to keep a wet edge, work in small sections, and take your time to achieve a professional-looking finish.

## Rolling
Rolling is the process of applying paint to large surface areas using a roller and extension pole. Here's how to do it:

* Use a W-pattern to apply paint, working in sections to maintain a wet edge.
* Keep the roller loaded with paint, but not too much, as this can lead to roller marks.
* Work in a consistent and methodical manner, maintaining a steady pace.
* Apply the second coat once the first coat is touch dry, but not bone dry.

Rolling is a physical and time-consuming process, but with the right technique and tools, you can achieve a smooth and even finish. Remember to keep a wet edge, work in small sections, and take regular breaks to avoid fatigue.

## Trim and Skirting
Trim and skirting require a different approach to painting, as they are typically painted with a brush rather than a roller. Here's how to do it:

* Use a high-quality brush (2.5" angled sash) to paint trim and skirting.
* Work in small sections, maintaining a wet edge to prevent lap marks.
* Use oil-based paint for trim and skirting, as it provides a harder and more durable finish.
* Apply a second coat once the first coat is touch dry, but not bone dry.

Trim and skirting are critical areas that require attention to detail and a steady hand. Remember to work slowly, maintain a wet edge, and take your time to achieve a professional-looking finish.

## Drying Times and Second Coats
Drying times and second coats are critical factors to consider when painting. Here are some general guidelines:

* Resene and Dulux recommend recoating times of 2-4 hours, depending on the paint type and conditions.
* Humidity and temperature can impact drying times, so it's essential to check the paint manufacturer's recommendations.
* Wait until the first coat is touch dry, but not bone dry, before applying the second coat.

Drying times and second coats are critical factors that can make or break a painting project. Remember to follow the manufacturer's recommendations, check the paint's drying time, and wait until the first coat is touch dry before applying the second coat.

## Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them
Here are some common mistakes to avoid when painting:

* Skipping primer: Primer is essential for achieving a professional-looking finish, so don't skip it.
* Painting over dusty or dirty surfaces: Clean the surface thoroughly before painting to ensure a smooth and even finish.
* Using the wrong roller nap: Choose the right roller nap for the job, as this can impact the finish and texture.
* Painting in direct sunlight: Avoid painting in direct sunlight, as this can cause the paint to dry too quickly and unevenly.

By avoiding these common mistakes, you can achieve a professional-looking finish and avoid costly rework.

## FAQ
Here are some frequently asked questions about painting:

* Q: How long does it take to paint a room?
A: The time it takes to paint a room depends on the size of the room, the number of coats, and the complexity of the job. On average, it can take 2-4 hours to paint a small room, and 4-6 hours to paint a larger room.
* Q: How much paint do I need for a standard bedroom?
A: The amount of paint needed for a standard bedroom depends on the size of the room and the number of coats. On average, you'll need 2-3 litres of paint for a small bedroom, and 4-6 litres for a larger bedroom.
* Q: Can I paint over mould?
A: No, it's not recommended to paint over mould. Mould can be a sign of a more serious issue, such as water damage or poor ventilation. It's essential to address the underlying issue before painting.
* Q: What's the best paint for NZ kitchens and bathrooms?
A: The best paint for NZ kitchens and bathrooms is a water-based paint with a semi-gloss or gloss finish. This provides a durable and easy-to-clean finish that can withstand the moisture and humidity of these areas.
* Q: How much does it cost to paint a room?
A: The cost of painting a room depends on the size of the room, the number of coats, and the materials used. On average, it can cost $150-$300 to paint a small room, and $300-$600 to paint a larger room.
