## Introduction
As a homeowner in New Zealand, it's essential to know when your roof needs replacing, rather than just repairing. The country's unique climate, with its high UV levels, significant rainfall, and strong winds, can be harsh on roofing materials. A well-maintained roof is crucial to protect your home from the elements and ensure the safety and comfort of your family. This comprehensive guide will cover the signs that indicate you need a new roof, the various roofing materials available, their costs, and what to expect from the replacement process. Whether you're a homeowner or a tradesperson, this article will provide you with the necessary information to make informed decisions about roof replacement in New Zealand.

## Signs You Need a New Roof
Determining whether your roof needs replacing can be a challenging task, especially if you're not familiar with the signs of wear and tear. Here are some indicators that suggest it's time for a new roof:
* Age: Corrugated iron roofs typically last between 30 to 50 years, while concrete tile roofs can last between 40 to 60 years. If your roof is approaching or has exceeded its expected lifespan, it may be time to consider replacement.
* Visible rust: If you notice rust on your corrugated iron roof, it's a sign that the material is deteriorating and may need to be replaced.
* Lifted or missing tiles: If tiles are lifting or missing, it can allow water to enter your home, causing damage to the interior and potentially leading to more severe issues.
* Interior leaks: If you notice water stains or leaks on your ceiling, it may indicate that your roof is no longer watertight.
* Sagging: A sagging roof can be a sign of structural issues or water damage, which may require a new roof to ensure the safety of your home.
* Gutters full of granules: If your gutters are filled with granules from your concrete tiles, it's a sign that the tiles are deteriorating and may need to be replaced.
* Failed flashings vs failed roof: It's essential to distinguish between failed flashings and a failed roof, as the cost of repair or replacement can vary significantly. Failed flashings can often be repaired, whereas a failed roof may require a full replacement.

## Roofing Materials Comparison
When it comes to choosing a roofing material, there are several options available in New Zealand. Here's a comparison of some of the most popular materials:
* Long-run steel (Colorsteel Endura, Maxx): $90–$140/m² installed, 50-year+ warranted life. Long-run steel is a popular choice for New Zealand homes, offering a durable and low-maintenance option.
* Corrugate/tray profile (traditional NZ iron look): $80–$130/m² installed. This traditional roofing material is still widely used in New Zealand, offering a cost-effective and familiar look.
* Concrete tile: $120–$180/m² installed, heavy (structural implications). Concrete tiles are a popular choice for their durability and aesthetic appeal, but they can be heavy, which may require additional structural support.
* Clay tile: $180–$280/m², heritage look, longest lifespan. Clay tiles offer a unique, heritage look and can last for many decades, but they come at a higher cost.
* Colorbond (Australia-manufactured, available in NZ): similar to Colorsteel. Colorbond is a popular choice in Australia, but it's also available in New Zealand, offering a similar product to Colorsteel.

## Full Cost Breakdown for a Typical NZ House
The cost of replacing a roof can vary significantly depending on the material, size, and pitch of the roof. Here's a breakdown of the costs for a typical 150m² house:
* Roofing material: $15,000–$35,000 depending on the material and pitch
* Removal and disposal: $2,000–$6,000
* Sarking (building wrap/underlay): $1,500–$3,500
* Flashings (ridgeline, valleys, penetrations): $2,000–$5,000
* Gutters and downpipes: $3,000–$8,000 (if replacing simultaneously)
* Regional variation: Auckland 20% premium, South Island 10–15% cheaper

It's essential to note that these costs are estimates, and the actual cost of replacing your roof may vary depending on your location, the complexity of the job, and other factors. It's recommended to get at least three quotes from licensed roofing contractors to compare prices and services.

## Does a New Roof Need Building Consent?
In New Zealand, like-for-like re-roofing is generally exempt from building consent under Schedule 1 of the Building Act. However, if you're changing the material type or making significant structural changes, you may require a building consent. It's recommended to contact your local council's duty planner to determine if a building consent is required for your specific project.

## What to Look for in a Roofing Contractor
When selecting a roofing contractor, it's essential to look for the following:
* Licensed Building Practitioner (LBP) for restricted building work
* Manufacturer-authorised installer (important for warranty)
* Written warranty on both product and workmanship
* At least three quotes from different contractors

Using a reputable job management and invoicing tool like Xero, Fergus, or Tradify can help you streamline your communication with contractors and ensure that you're getting the best possible service.

## Questions to Ask When Getting Quotes
When getting quotes from roofing contractors, it's essential to ask the right questions to ensure you're getting a comprehensive and accurate estimate. Here are some questions to ask:
* What's the warranty coverage?
* Who is the manufacturer-authorised installer?
* What underlay/sarking is included?
* Does the quote include gutters?

## Timing
The best time to reroof in New Zealand is during the spring or autumn, when the weather is mild and dry. Avoiding the rainy season can help ensure a smooth and efficient installation process. The typical job duration for a standard house is 3–7 days, depending on the complexity of the job and the number of contractors working on the project.

## FAQ
Here are some frequently asked questions about roof replacement in New Zealand:
1. How long does roof replacement take?
The duration of a roof replacement project can vary depending on the size and complexity of the job, but it typically takes 3–7 days for a standard house.
2. Does my insurance cover roof replacement?
It's essential to check your insurance policy to see if it covers roof replacement. Some policies may cover the cost of replacement, while others may only cover repairs.
3. Can I reroof myself in NZ?
While it's possible to reroof yourself, it's not recommended unless you have extensive experience with roofing and are confident in your ability to do the job safely and correctly. It's recommended to hire a licensed roofing contractor to ensure a professional and warranty-backed installation.
4. What's the best roofing material for NZ?
The best roofing material for New Zealand depends on your budget, personal preferences, and the specific needs of your home. Long-run steel and concrete tiles are popular choices, but it's essential to consider factors like durability, maintenance, and aesthetic appeal when making your decision.
5. How often should I inspect my roof?
It's recommended to inspect your roof at least once a year, preferably after a significant weather event, to ensure that it's in good condition and to identify any potential issues before they become major problems.
