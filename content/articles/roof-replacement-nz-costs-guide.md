## Signs You Need a New Roof
A roof's lifespan can vary depending on the material and quality of installation. Generally, a corrugated iron roof can last between 30 to 50 years, while a concrete tile roof can last between 40 to 60 years. However, there are several signs that indicate you need a new roof, rather than just repairing the existing one. These include:
* Visible rust on metal roofs
* Lifted or missing tiles on concrete or clay tile roofs
* Interior leaks, which can be a sign of a compromised roof
* Sagging, which can indicate structural issues
* Gutters full of granules from tile roofs, which can indicate wear and tear
* Failed flashings, which can be a sign of a larger issue with the roof

It's essential to distinguish between failed flashings and a failed roof, as the cost of replacement can vary significantly. Failed flashings can often be repaired or replaced without needing to replace the entire roof, whereas a failed roof may require a full replacement.

## Roofing Materials Comparison
When it comes to choosing a new roof, there are several materials to consider. Here are some of the most popular options:
* Long-run steel (Colorsteel Endura, Maxx): $90–$140/m² installed, 50-year+ warranted life
* Corrugate/tray profile (traditional NZ iron look): $80–$130/m² installed
* Concrete tile: $120–$180/m² installed, heavy (structural implications)
* Clay tile: $180–$280/m², heritage look, longest lifespan

## Full Cost Breakdown for a Typical NZ House
The cost of replacing a roof on a typical New Zealand house can vary depending on the material and pitch of the roof. For a 150m² house, the cost can range from $15,000 to $35,000, depending on the material chosen. Here's a breakdown of the costs involved:
* Removal and disposal: $2,000–$6,000
* Sarking (building wrap/underlay): $1,500–$3,500
* Flashings (ridgeline, valleys, penetrations): $2,000–$5,000
* Gutters and downpipes: $3,000–$8,000 (if replacing simultaneously)

Regional variation can also impact the cost, with Auckland tenders often coming in at a 20% premium, while the South Island can be 10–15% cheaper.

## Does a New Roof Need Building Consent?
In most cases, like-for-like re-roofing is generally Schedule 1 exempt, meaning that building consent is not required. However, if you're changing the material type or making significant structural changes, you may need to obtain consent. It's always best to consult with a council duty planner to determine if consent is required.

## What to Look for in a Roofing Contractor
When selecting a roofing contractor, it's essential to look for a Licensed Building Practitioner (LBP) who is qualified to perform restricted building work. You should also ensure that the contractor is a manufacturer-authorised installer, as this is often a requirement for warranty purposes. A written warranty on both the product and workmanship is also essential. It's recommended to get at least three quotes from different contractors to compare prices and services.

## Questions to Ask When Getting Quotes
When getting quotes from roofing contractors, there are several questions you should ask, including:
* What's the warranty coverage?
* Who is the manufacturer-authorised installer?
* What underlay/sarking is included?
* Does the quote include gutters?

## Timing
The best time to reroof in New Zealand is during the spring or autumn, when the weather is mild and dry. Avoiding the rainy season can help prevent delays and ensure a smooth installation process. The typical job duration for a standard house can range from 3 to 7 days, depending on the complexity of the job and the number of contractors working on it.
