## Signs You Need a New Roof
A roof's lifespan can vary depending on the material and conditions it's exposed to. Generally, a corrugated iron roof can last between 30 to 50 years, while a concrete tile roof can last between 40 to 60 years. However, there are several signs that may indicate your roof needs replacing sooner rather than later. These include:
* Visible rust on metal roofs
* Lifted or missing tiles
* Interior leaks, which can be a sign of a more significant problem
* Sagging, which can be a sign of structural issues
* Gutters full of granules, which can indicate that your tile roof is nearing the end of its lifespan
It's essential to distinguish between failed flashings and a failed roof, as the cost of repair or replacement can vary significantly. Failed flashings can often be repaired, whereas a failed roof may require a full replacement.

* Long-run steel (Colorsteel Endura, Maxx): $90–$140/m² installed, 50yr+ warranted life

## Full Cost Breakdown for a Typical NZ House
The cost of replacing a roof can vary significantly depending on the material chosen, the size of the house, and the complexity of the job. For a typical 150m² house, the cost of a new roof can range from $15,000 to $35,000, depending on the material and pitch of the roof. Here's a breakdown of the costs involved:
* Removal and disposal: $2,000–$6,000
* Sarking (building wrap/underlay): $1,500–$3,500
* Flashings (ridgeline, valleys, penetrations): $2,000–$5,000
* Gutters and downpipes: $3,000–$8,000 (if replacing simultaneously)
It's also worth noting that there can be regional variations in cost, with Auckland tend to be around 20% more expensive than other parts of the country, and the South Island tend to be 10–15% cheaper.

## Does a New Roof Need Building Consent?
In most cases, like-for-like re-roofing is exempt from requiring a building consent, as outlined in the Building Act 2004 and the Building Regulations 1992, administered by the Ministry of Business, Innovation and Employment (MBIE). However, if you're changing the type of material or making significant structural changes, you may need to obtain a consent. It's always best to check with your local council's duty planner to confirm whether a consent is required.

## What to Look for in a Roofing Contractor
When choosing a roofing contractor, it's essential to ensure they are a Licensed Building Practitioner (LBP) for restricted building work, as required by the Building Act 2004. You should also look for a manufacturer-authorised installer, as this will ensure that your roof is installed correctly and that you're eligible for any warranties. A written warranty on both the product and workmanship is also essential. It's recommended to get at least three quotes from different contractors to compare prices and services.

## Questions to Ask When Getting Quotes
When getting quotes from roofing contractors, there are several questions you should ask to ensure you're getting the best deal. These include:
* What's the warranty coverage?
* Who is the manufacturer-authorised installer?
* What underlay/sarking is included?
* Does the quote include gutters and downpipes?
