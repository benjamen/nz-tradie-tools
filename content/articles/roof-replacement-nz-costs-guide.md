## Introduction to Roof Replacement in NZ
As a homeowner in New Zealand, it's essential to understand when your roof needs replacing, rather than just repairing. The country's unique climate, with its high levels of UV radiation, heavy rainfall, and strong winds, can be particularly harsh on roofs. If you're unsure whether your roof needs replacing, this guide will walk you through the signs to look out for, the different materials available, and what to expect from the replacement process. We'll cover the costs, materials, and regulations surrounding roof replacement in NZ, ensuring you're well-informed to make the best decision for your home.

## Signs You Need a New Roof
A roof's lifespan can vary greatly depending on the material and conditions it's exposed to. Generally, a corrugated iron roof can last between 30 to 50 years, while concrete tiles can last between 40 to 60 years. However, there are several signs that may indicate your roof needs replacing sooner rather than later. These include:
* Visible rust on metal roofs
* Lifted or missing tiles
* Interior leaks, which can be a sign of a more significant issue
* Sagging, which can be a sign of structural damage
* Gutters full of granules from tile roofs, indicating the tiles are breaking down
* Failed flashings, which can be a sign of a more significant issue, but may not necessarily mean the entire roof needs replacing

It's crucial to distinguish between failed flashings and a failed roof, as the cost of replacement can vary significantly. Failed flashings can often be repaired or replaced at a lower cost than replacing the entire roof. However, if the roof itself is failing, it may be more cost-effective to replace it entirely.

## Roofing Materials Comparison
When it comes to choosing a roofing material, there are several options available in NZ. Here are some of the most popular materials, their costs, and characteristics:
* Long-run steel (Colorsteel Endura, Maxx): $90–$140/m² installed, with a 50-year+ warranted life
* Corrugate/tray profile (traditional NZ iron look): $80–$130/m² installed
* Concrete tile: $120–$180/m² installed, heavy (structural implications)
* Clay tile: $180–$280/m², heritage look, longest lifespan
* Colorbond (Australia-manufactured, available in NZ): similar to Colorsteel

## Full Cost Breakdown for a Typical NZ House
The cost of replacing a roof on a typical NZ house can vary greatly depending on the material and pitch of the roof. For a 150m² house, the cost can range from $15,000 to $35,000, depending on the material chosen. Here's a breakdown of the costs involved:
* Removal and disposal: $2,000–$6,000
* Sarking (building wrap/underlay): $1,500–$3,500
* Flashings (ridgeline, valleys, penetrations): $2,000–$5,000
* Gutters and downpipes: $3,000–$8,000 (if replacing simultaneously)

Regional variation can also impact the cost, with Auckland tenders often coming in at a 20% premium, while the South Island can be 10–15% cheaper.

## Does a New Roof Need Building Consent?
In most cases, like-for-like re-roofing is exempt from requiring a building consent, as outlined in Schedule 1 of the Building Act. However, if you're changing the material type or making significant structural changes, you may need to obtain a consent. It's recommended to call your local council's duty planner to confirm whether a consent is required.

## What to Look for in a Roofing Contractor
When selecting a roofing contractor, it's essential to ensure they are a Licensed Building Practitioner (LBP) for restricted building work. You should also look for a manufacturer-authorised installer, as this is crucial for warranty purposes. A written warranty on both the product and workmanship is also essential. It's recommended to get at least three quotes from different contractors to compare prices and services.

## Questions to Ask When Getting Quotes
When getting quotes from roofing contractors, there are several questions you should ask:
* What's the warranty coverage?
* Who is the manufacturer-authorised installer?
* What underlay/sarking is included in the quote?
* Does the quote include gutters and downpipes?

## Timing
The best time to reroof in NZ is during the spring or autumn, when the weather is generally calmer. Avoiding the rainy season can help ensure the job is completed efficiently and safely. The typical job duration for a standard house is between 3–7 days, depending on the complexity of the job and the number of workers involved.
