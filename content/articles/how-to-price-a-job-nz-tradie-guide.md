Pricing a job wrong is the fastest way to go broke as a tradie. Too high and you lose the quote. Too low and you win the job — but make nothing, or worse, lose money.

This guide walks you through a repeatable, profitable pricing method that works for any NZ trade.

## Step 1: Know Your Costs

Before you can price anything, you need to know what it costs you to operate. Most tradies focus on job-level costs but forget about overhead — and that's where the money goes.

### Direct Job Costs

These change with every job:
- **Labour** — your time or your employees' time (at their actual cost to you, not what you charge)
- **Materials** — at cost price from your supplier
- **Subcontractors** — at their quoted price
- **Equipment hire** — any hired plant or tools

### Overhead Costs (Fixed)

These go on whether you work or not:
- Vehicle running costs (estimate monthly, divide by billable days)
- Phone, internet, software subscriptions
- Insurance (liability, tools, income protection)
- Tool maintenance and replacement
- Advertising and website
- Accounting fees
- Yard or workshop rent (if applicable)

Add up your annual overhead and divide by your billable hours/days to get a per-hour overhead rate.

**Example:**
- Annual overhead: $48,000
- Billable hours: 1,600/year
- Overhead per hour: $30

## Step 2: Calculate Your Labour Cost

Don't just use your hourly wage as your "labour cost" — your real labour cost includes:
- Your effective hourly rate (target income ÷ billable hours)
- Overhead per hour (from Step 1)
- Profit margin

Use the [Hourly Rate Calculator](/calculators/hourly-rate-calculator.html) to work this out properly.

**Example:**
- Target income: $90,000/year
- Billable hours: 1,600/year
- Overhead rate: $30/hr
- Labour cost: ($90,000 ÷ 1,600) + $30 = $56.25 + $30 = $86.25/hr

## Step 3: Estimate Time Accurately

Accurate time estimation is a skill. Common mistakes:
- **Not including travel time** — if you're driving 45 minutes each way, that's 1.5 hours every day
- **Forgetting setup and cleanup** — often 30–60 minutes per day on site
- **Underestimating complexity** — assume things go wrong (they often do)
- **Missing remediation** — patching, painting, making good

Build a **10–15% time contingency** into every quote for unknowns.

## Step 4: Apply the Right Markup

Markup is the percentage you add above your cost to create your selling price.

**Common markup rates:**
- Labour: 20–30%
- Materials: 15–25%
- Subcontractors: 10–15%

Use the [Markup vs Margin Calculator](/calculators/markup-margin-calculator.html) to ensure you're not confusing markup with margin — a mistake that costs NZ tradies thousands every year.

## Step 5: Add GST

All NZ tradie quotes to GST-registered customers should show GST separately.

- **Ex-GST price** (your subtotal after markup)
- **+ GST (15%)**
- **= Total inc GST**

Use the [GST Calculator](/calculators/gst-calculator.html) or the [Job Cost Calculator](/calculators/job-cost-calculator.html) to automate this.

## Step 6: Build Your Quote Document

A professional quote builds trust and wins jobs. Include:

1. Your business name, logo, contact details, and GST number
2. Client name and address
3. Job address
4. Quote date and validity period (e.g. "valid for 30 days")
5. Clear description of what's included — and what's not
6. Itemised breakdown (labour, materials, subcontractors)
7. Payment terms (e.g. deposit required, payment on completion, net 7)
8. GST breakdown (ex-GST, GST, total inc GST)

[Fergus](https://www.fergus.com/) and [Tradify](https://www.tradifyhq.com/) both produce professional quotes from your phone in minutes.

## Step 7: Follow Up

Don't send a quote and forget it. Follow up after 3–5 days:
- "Hi [name], just checking you received our quote for [job]. Happy to answer any questions."

Most tradies never follow up. The ones that do win more jobs.

## What's a Healthy Gross Profit Margin?

| Business size | Target gross margin |
|---|---|
| Sole trader | 30–40% |
| Small team (2–5 staff) | 25–35% |
| Medium business (5–20 staff) | 20–30% |

Gross margin = (Revenue - Direct costs) ÷ Revenue × 100

If your gross margin is below 20%, you're not covering overhead and profit. Below 15% and you may be working hard for very little return.

## Common Pricing Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Pricing to win at all costs** — some jobs aren't worth having
2. **Forgetting travel time** — it's your time, it has value
3. **Underpricing materials** — add your markup, don't give them away
4. **No contingency** — something always goes wrong
5. **Confusing markup and margin** — see the calculator above
6. **Not reviewing past jobs** — track what you actually spent vs. quoted
